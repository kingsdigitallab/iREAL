7/8/1893 - Barrington -- objection to proposed amalgamation of
Aborigines school with Barrington school. "On behalf of a meeting of the
parents of the children resident in the Barrington school district, we
were deputed this afternoon to respectfully draw your attention to a
report that is current in this locality to the effect that there is a
probability of a change in the arrangements of the schools of the
locality. We are directed to respectfully point out that there is a
strong feeling, locally, against the union of the Aborigines School,
whether temporarily or otherwise, with the Barrington Public School and
that should the Aboriginals be sent to the Barrington public school
nearly the whole of the residents will be compelled to withdraw their
children and have them educated at home or elsewhere. Hoping that you
will not compel them to take this step. (Signed by three local residents
-- Bell, Taylor and Cornish).

Memo from Department of Public Instruction -- "Objecting to proposed
amalgamation of Aborigines school with... I recommend that the residents
be informed that there is no intention of altering the mode of working
the Barrington Public School, or amalgamating the Aborigines Camp School
with it."

15/1/1897 -- To Minister of Public instruction. "The Petition of the
undersigned parents and guardians of the pupils attending Barrington
Public School respectfully request that, whereas your petitioners are
informed that the Half Time school at the Aborigines Camp at Barrington
has been closed and that the aborigines children propose to attend the
Barrington Public School at the termination on the Christmas holidays on
the 17 January 1897 you will be good enough to prevent the attendance of
the Aborigines at Barrington Public School, and grant the Aborigines an
Evening Public School in lieu of the previously existing Half Time
School for the following reasons:

a\) That at different times infectious diseases which have had their
origin at the Aborigines Camp have spread through this locality and that
our children will be much more liable to catch such diseases if closed
up in the same school-room with the aborigines.

b\) That the camp is frequently visited by Aboriginals from other
districts of the colony, who have introduced various forms of diseases
into this district

c\) that there is every probability of the "moral tone" of he pupils of
the local public school being much reduced by contacts with the
Aborigine's children more especially relating to neatness and
cleanliness.

d\) that previous Ministers of Public Instruction have acknowledged the
propriety of keeping pupils of European origin separate from aborigines
as shown in the erection of separate school houses at Barrington and
other places

e\) that no additional expenses will be involved in establishing an
Evening Public School, except the payment of the Teachers Salary, as a
school building and necessary furniture are now upon the ground.

Trusting the foregoing will receive your early and favourable
consideration your petitions as in duty bound will ever pray."

Names of 13 petitioners are listed underneath.

15/1/1897 - Letter to Department Public Instruction -- Re Aborigines
attending Barrington Public School

"Sir, I have the honour to enclose from the parents of the pupils
attending the Public School at Barrington asking that the aborigines not
be allowed to attend the Barrington school. I may mention that there is
a very strong feeling here against he Blacks attending the school. There
would not be the same objection were they living with Europeans who
would send them in a clean and tidy condition to school. The objection
is against the Blacks from the Camp -- some of whom have neither father
or mother to look after them. Most of the residents here consider that
an evening school under the teacher of the Barrington school will supply
all that is necessary for the Aborigines as they learn quickly to a
certain point and do not seem capable of going beyond it. As the time
for opening the school after vacation is now so short would greatly
oblige by telegraphing your decision to the teacher or to myself. We did
not know until today that Aborigines proposed sending their children to
the Public School. Signed Angus Beaten.

In memo from Chief Inspector 10/12/1895 " I have just been informed that
all the blacks have left Barrington. Is this correct? How many are
likely to return, and how many children are likely to attend school for
the remainder of the year? "Reply -- "Sir, The camp at present is
deserted, the families are spread about. Two families are living near
Shaws, the Cook family are at the Bowman, and I think will not return.
The number to attend for the rest of the remainder of the year is 5
unless the Cooks return." (William Griffiths)

4/1/1896 -- Memo -- Prospects of School Aborigines Camp School
Barrington

"Of late the attendance at this school has been very poor, as you will
see by my returns. The cause of poor attendance was, that parents and
children left camp for a while, owing I imagine to superstition. During
the last few months no less than 3 of them died. With respect to your
letter I had an interview with the parents of the children, they
informed me, that under present circumstances, it was almost impossible
to send the children to school, owing to insufficient rations and
clothing. The number of children of school age is 11. The parents
promised me that they would try in the future to send their children
more regularly, not much reliance can be placed in them." (William
Griffith)
