2/4/1907 -- Dept Public Instruction -- Question of admitting Aborigines
Coraki Public School -- Petition attached. "Several Aborigines presented
themselves for enrolment at the above school. They were admitted for a
few days, but the white residents at once strongly objected. I happened
to be in the town and the attached petition was presented. The feeling
was high, I advised the teacher to send the dark children home, pending
your decision. I may say the aborigines wore clean clothes, but "the
smell from them was disgustingly strong all the same." They came from an
adjacent camp. I informed the local police officer that our Department
would consider an application for a separate aborigines school. I
certainly think it is not advisable to enroll them at the local school.
Recommended that the teacher of Coraki Pub School be instructed that he
should not admit aboriginal children; but that he may inform their
parents or guardians the Minister will consider an application for the
establishment of a school for aborigines only."

Petition letter following from parents of the local white community. 11
people signed the petition. There is a note on it stating "I am of the
opinion that nearly every parent in the town would sign this if asked."
R.H

24/6/1908 -- Dept Publ. Instruction -- Re: Aborigines at Coraki:
Question of establishing a school. "Upwards of a year ago, these
children were excluded from the local public school (see forwarded
papers). The local Sergeant of Police has at my request furnished the
appended list of children. They are entirely without the means of
education. I am of opinion which is based upon the statements of local
residents that about fifteen children of school ages are likely to
remain on the camping ground (Aborigines Reserve). Recommended that I be
authorized to obtain tenders with a view to the erection of a suitable
building for school purposes. Approx. cost £60. Further recommended that
the APB be informed it is proposed to establish a Prov. School for
aborigines at Coraki" Signed R. Henderson Inspector.

Note from Chief Inspector 22/7/1908 -- "Owing to the nomadic instincts
of these people, their residence in any locality cannot be relied upon.
It is not advisable therefore to erect one of our usual buildings; but
one on a cheaper scale. I therefore propose to construct a schoolroom
18' \* 14' \* 10', with iron roof, and ceiled only. Two tenders are
submitted, both reasonable. Recommended I be authorized to have the
building erected by W. Dolby \[and Co\] Coraki, at a cost of £54.13.8"
Approved.

Extract from report by Inspector Donaldson of his inspection of the
Aborigines Reserve at Coraki, dated 5 July 1916. "School arrangements
are not satisfactory. Miss Green is now teaching, but is not recognized
by the Education Department. So far she has gets no salary, she will not
stop unless she is paid. She seems to be devoted to the children. It
would be a pity to lose her as her whole heart is in the work. She
sleeps in the school 5 nights of the week and goes to Coraki at
weekends. I told her that this could not continue, she said that if she
is not paid by the Education Department she will immediately leave. If
she is, she has already arranged where to board and live. At present she
cannot afford it as she has only a small income of her own. She has a
good control over all the people. She is tutor nurse and doctor to the
women and children. She is about 40 years of age and apparently well
about to take care of herself. She told me she has lived on more
isolated places than Coraki and she was never molested nor insulted by
the Aborigines. It should be ascertained what her position is, so that
she can make other arrangements. The Reverend Mr. Homersham, who was
with me says, it would be a big loss to the residents if she were to
leave. I recommend that the Board place her position before Mr. Dawson
with a view to having her appointed as Teacher. She has the offer of
employment on one of the Queensland Missions and will take it unless she
is recognized as a Teacher on this Reserve." Letter sent to APB for
consideration. Approved by APB.

Letter from APB to Dept. Education -- 3/11/1916 -- Re: Allowance paid to
the teacher of the Aborigines School at Coraki. "The Department of
Education recently approved of the appointment of Miss Green as teacher
of the Aborigines School at Coraki, with subsidy at the special rate of
£80 per annum. The Local Committee now ask that in view of the work done
by Miss Green on the Reserve, in nursing the sick, distributing
children's rations and a general supervision, her subsidy be
supplemented by an allowance from the Board." Notes from Inspector
Donaldson regarding this matter." In July last there were 20 children on
the roll with an attendance of 16.4. I cannot understand why the Dept.
punished this teacher whilst giving a living salary to others with a
smaller number of pupils. If Miss Green is not up to mark in her
qualifications for teacher aborigines children I would certainly advise
the Board to supplement her salary to a living wage on account of her
devotion to the children and her care of and sympathy with the adults.
But if she is as well qualified as the average of the teachers on these
reserves who receive £110 per annum I cannot see why the Board should be
called on to make up her salary. At the same time, if the Dept. will not
increase it I recommend the Board to make it up at least a living for
her as she well deserves it.

Correspondence from St. Mary Magdalene's Church Coraki to Local School
Inspector -- 9/10/1916 -- "Thankyou for your endeavors in servicing the
special rate of subsidy to Miss. Green. We hope that an allowance from

possible. Most likely the Committee will send in a report on the Girls

17/12/1932 -- Letter from Mrs. Mitchell to Department -- "I wish to
bring before your notice the inquiry of the dual system of control of
school by the Department of Education, and the Aborigines Protection
Board, and also the injustice meted out to the farming community. I was
a married woman teacher at the Aborigines School here, from 4^th^
December 1920. On 19^th^ December 1930, when the married women question
was well in the public eye the Aborigines Inspector came to my home,
telling me that the AP Board had decided to retire all married women
teachers living with their husbands. He had previously called at the
police station and told the Sergeant that such was the case...."

Further correspondence from Mrs. Mitchell asking if it were true that
all married woman teachers asked to resign are entitled to compensation.

Department of Education -- Coraki Aboriginal -- Resignation of Mrs. H.
Mitchell - "Mrs. Helen Mitchell, who was employed as Temporary Teacher
of the Aboriginal School at Coraki until 19^th^ December 1930, has
protested to the Minister, through Mr. W. T. Missingham, M.L.A.,
regarding the dual control of schools by this Department and the
Aborigines Protection Board and claims that she was forced to relinquish
her position at Coraki by the Aborigines Inspector and considers that
she should get some compensation on that account. She states that on the
19^th^ December, 1930, when the married women question was well in the
public eye, the Aborigines Inspector came to her home telling her that
the Aborigines Protection Board had decided to retire all married women
teachers living with their husbands. Mrs. Mitchell's resignation was
dated 19^th^ December, 1930, and was forwarded to this Department,
through the Aborigines Protection Board. As she had previously been
overpaid, she was asked to refund the amount of overpayment, and in
reply claimed that she was forced to resign through the Inspector's
statement regarding married women and that he had promised her that
payment of salary would be made during her vacation. This matter was
investigated in June, 1931, when a report was obtained from the
Aborigines Inspector concerned (Mrs. Smithers) and the Secretary of the
Aborigines Protection Board. Mr. Smithers report was to the effect that
when he visited Coraki on the 19^th^ December, 1930, the children were
in a filthy condition and the school was dirty, indicating that Mrs.
Mitchell had been negligent in her duties in Matron. The same condition
of affairs had applied when he visited six months before, but at that
time Mrs. Mitchell had informed him that she contemplated resigning. ON
his visit in December he asked Mrs. Mitchell, in the presence of her
husband, and she sated that she wished to do so in deference to her
husband's wishes as she could not attend to both her domestic duties and
the school work satisfactorily. She added that she had seen in the paper
where the Education Department was considering the retrenchment of
married teachers. Mrs. Mitchell was not aware of it he would, in view of
her proved inefficiency, have submitted a recommendation for her
retrenchment if she had not voluntary resigned. He had not reported on
this aspect at the time as, in view of Mrs. Mitchell's voluntary
resignation, he did not wish to record anything that would cause an
unfavorable impression when it could serve no purpose. The Inspector's
statement in this regard is supported by the Secretary of the Board. In
view of the reports, Mrs. Mitchell's resignation was accepted from the
31^st^ December, 1920, and nothing further was heard from her until
August last when she applied to the Aborigines' Protection Board for
payment of compensation in respect of the termination of her services.
This letter was referred to this Department, and in reply she was
advised that she would not be entitled to any payment as she had
voluntarily relinquished her position. She has now raised the matter
again. On the evidence before the Department there does not appear to
have been any injustice done to Mrs. Mitchell. Mr. Smithers is a
reliable officer of the Aborigines Protection Board and his statement of
what transpired at his interview with Mrs. Mitchell can be accepted. In
any case, if her work was unsatisfactory, her services would have been
terminated and she would have been entitled to no compensation in
respect of such termination. Her objection to dual control by the two
Departments is not a very real one. Women teachers appointed to the
change of Aboriginal Schools are also Matrons under the control of the
Aborigines Protection Board and there must be very close cooperation
between the two Departments. However, the school work is inspected by
the School Inspector and her work as Matron by the Aborigines Protection
Board Inspector. If she is doing unsatisfactory work in either
directions, her services as Matron-Teacher are terminated, but the
experience of the Department has been that these two Inspectors
co-operate in their work and no objection has previously been offered to
the procedure which is essential for the efficient working of the
administration. I RECOMMEND that Mrs. Missingham, M.L. A, be informed
that full inquiry has been made into this matter, and that it has been
found that Mrs. Mitchell has no ground for complaint in regard to her
treatment as Matron-Teacher of this School. HE might also be informed
that, if he so desires, the Departmental file will be made available to
him for inspection at his office." 21/1/1933

Letter from APB -- 10/6/1931 -- Concerning resignation of Mrs. Mitchell
from School. "I beg to forward for your information copy of a report
furnished by Inspector Smithers regarding the circumstances under which
Mrs. Mitchell resigned her position as Teacher. I desire to add that on
the occasion of Mrs. Smither's previous inspection he was not satisfied
with the manner in which Mrs. Mitchell appeared to be carrying out her
personal appearance of herself and also of the pupils, for the
cleanliness of which she was as the Matron of the Reserve, responsible.
It was decided to leave the matter in abeyance pending a further visit
to the Reserve by the Inspector, especially in view of the fact that
Mrs. Mitchell apparently contemplating retiring. In this connection I
might also say that she expressed a desire to resign on the occasion of
a previous visit to the Reserve by ex-Inspector Donaldson and myself
some time ago. Carefully considering the matter, I cannot see that Mrs.
Mitchell has suffered any injustice."

22/12/1932 -- Letter from Mrs. Mitchell concerning resignation --"... I
would like to make the position quite clear to you. I did not resign on
the [advice] of the AP Inspector, but on the
[demand]. He informed me that out of consideration for my
long service with the Board, he had been sent here specially to inform
me of the decision to retire all married woman living with husbands.
Although he informed me that I would be paid to the end of the school
holidays, I had to refund payment back to 31^st^ December 1930, so that
I had really only twelve days notice instead of the usual month. What I
complain of is the fact that the AP Board could demand my resignation
because I was married, put another married woman in my place (who is not
living with her husband), and then put the responsibility on the
Aborigines Protection Board asking if I also would partake of these
benefits..... I must conclude that I have been unjustly dealt with.... I
hope you will forgive me for troubling you, but I remember your kindness
to the dark people during a visit of Sir Dudly de Chair to the district
-- the only member who ever gave these poor children lollies! Do you
remember that time when "Old Peel" presented Sir. Dudley with a
boomerang, and upon being offered four shillings by the aid-de-camp,
demanded ten-bob!."

**Curriculum and Resources**

Notes from Miss. Greens attendance to observe teaching at a one-teacher
school. Observation notes are as follows:

Oakland Public School -

Children line up for general inspection of hands, nails teeth and
appearance. Examples in drill, deep breathing upper chest, lower chest,
arm flexions leg and knee flexions, neck flexions. Trunk exercises
skipping and running.

Children write in single ruled books a copy written between two ruled
lines on black board. "Every man to more of less matter of his own
fate." The teacher places the copy in the right hand corner of school
and the children are made to sit half side on facing it when writing.
The books are corrected and errors in lines and curves pointed out.
Percentage practice and other sums are written down on the Board by the
teacher, the children ask how to work the sums if not understood. When
the sums have been worked by the children, the teacher works them out on
the board. Moral Story -- Negro Slavery. The subject is introduced by
asking questions on the previous lesson. As who were the Serfs? Who were
the slaves? What difference between them? Then the teacher reads a
portion, and the children asks questions. Who are the Portuguese? Who
were the first to introduce slavery, where did they send them? What is
the name of the country? That nations had slavery? Who were the moors?
Who tried to put down slavery? How many Negroes were sent to America
each year? What work did they engage in?

Notes continue on lessons given at the school that Miss. Green observed.
