23/3/1908 -- Memo to Chief Inspector re Ngoorumba As to the temporary
employment of a teacher. "The proposed arrangement has fallen through.
The young man, referred to by Mrs. Broun, seems unwilling to face the
ordeal of submitting his credentials, and even his sponsor seems
doubtful now of he capabilities. Mrs. Broun now volunteers to take the
position herself on the same terms, and submits her own name on the form
I sent to hr for use of her protégé. I do not know very much of her,
personally, as I was only once at her station. She is 50 years of age,
and the wife of a "small" pastoralist. She has always taken a great
interest in these aboriginals, and it is her persistent advocacy of
their claims that has resulted in the establishment of a school. Her
attainments do not appear high, if her spelling is any criterion. I am
not without scruples as to recommending her temporary employment, and
for the following reasons:- 1. I am afraid that station and domestic
duties will stand first in demand upon her time, and that, as a
consequence her attendance at School will often be interrupted. 2. I am
afraid It will be difficult to get her into the way of keeping school
records and compiling returns. However I know of no other way of getting
the school going, and she will, I think, server our purposes until a
more suitable teacher can be found. I recommend that Mrs. Broun be
instructed to take charge of the school from Monday 30 March at a salary
of £50 a year, such appointment to be for three months only, and to be
subject to approval of the Public Service Board. If this recommendation
is approved a supply of blank forms, O.S. Stamps should be sent her. It
will be well to acquaint me at once, so that I may try to get her to put
in the way of keeping records."

12/10/1932 - Letter to Dept. Education -- "Dear Sir, We the promoters of
Long Gully Subsidised school desire to bring before you the following
matter. Long Gully is an Aboriginal settlement where the people have
gathered together on their own accord with the desire that they be left
to the care of the missionaries working with them. A school was started
over three years ago by them in an endevour to educate the children and
later on it was taken over by my daughter Miss D. V. Harris, as a
subsidized school, and is carried on as such at present. The
missionaries also voluntarily give their assistance as required in
teaching the children, and frequently there are the services of two
teachers at the disposal of the education department in this way. The
number of the pupils have steadily increased and this year there are
fifteen permanent ones on the roll book. Besides these there are others
who come for a short time and then move on. Now we desire to submit
through out teacher the names of eleven more children who will be
regular attendants, bringing the total up to 26. We would like to
suggest an alteration to the grade of the school and put forward the
following consideration 1. That the Long Gully school be recognized as
an Aboriginal School and the ordinary hours and lessons for such schools
be reverted to. 2. The present teachers services be retained at the
increased remuneration. 3. Realizing the need for economy at present, we
would be willing to have to have the school carried on as at present
with the Mission Church building while it is satisfactory to your
department. 4. The missionaries are willing to continue their assistance
as at present when needed. The people are well satisfied with the
present control by the police and missionaries and would much prefer not
to have a manager or strange teacher sent here. This is why we specially
desire that the present teachers services be retained. Trusting you will
favourally consider the above." Signed Florrie Munro and J. L. Murray
(?) Written in the hand writing of R. Harris

7/6/1933 -- Letter from Missionary Mr. R. Harris to Mr. Drummond,
Minister of Education -- "Dear Sir, I desire to place before you a
matter, which is still unsettled, and I would be grateful if you could
endevour to bring it to successful conclusion. My daughter Miss D.
Harris is classed as a subsidized teacher at the Aboriginal School at
Long Gully. This school was started by the missionaries in the first
place, and as it grew Miss Harris obtained the position mentioned above.
My wife and myself (the present missionaries) are still giving our
services gratuitously every day in educating these children. The subsidy
now received by my daughter is for a maximum of nine children. The
school has been gradually growing, and in October last year we had an
additional list of eleven pupils to be added to the seventeen then on
the roll. At the same time application was made for the school to be
classed as a Provisional School seeing there was a roll of twenty eight
scholars. This matter is still undecided although it is now seven months
since the application was made. During that time three of the pupils
have left, and there are seven others who have attended, bringing our
total over thirty, with an average daily attendance of about
twenty-three. Will you kindly look into the matter with a view of
settlement as to classification and remuneration?, which I think should
date back from the time the application was made. I understand there is
some delay with the Aborigines Protection Board, as these people are not
on a reserve, but is it fair to delay the recognition of the school and
the services rendered until these other matters are settled? Again the
supplies allowed for a Subsidised school of nine are hardly sufficient
for thirty children and we have great difficulty in carrying on with the
material on hand. Then the delay is making it very inconvenient for us
in more ways than one as future plans cannot be thought out until this
is finalized. Trusting you will be able to favour me with your attention
to this matter. With thanks I remain Yours Faithfully R. Harris
(Missionary)"

17/4/1934 -- Dept Education re Ngoorumba -- Removal of Building -- "A
letter was received by the Minister in October 1^st^ from Mrs. N. R.
Broun protesting against the proposal to move the Bassendean School
building. As the Department was not aware of any contemplated action
along these lines, the matter was referred to Mr. Inspector Kellor.
Perusal of the reports which have been furnished as a result disclose
that a school building is urgently required to replace the existing
unsatisfactory structure utilized in connection with the Aboriginal
Provisional School at Ngoorumba. The suggestion is that the Bassendean
building handed over by this Department to the Aborigines Board in 1925
who removed it there from Ten Mile, to be transferred to Ngoorumba. The
Aboriginal School which was accommodated in the building has ceased to
exist and a small subsidized school known as Broun's homestead has been
in occupation recently. Thus it is that Mrs. Broun makes the request
that the building be retained in its present position. It will be seen
that the structure in question is not the property of this Department
and that the decision as to whether it shall be removed or retained
rests with the Aborigines Protection Board, to whom it belongs. At the
same time, some suitable representation might be made to the Board
relative to the matter. It is recommended therefore that -- (a) the
terms of the report of Mr. Inspector Kellor dated the 2^nd^ March last
be conveyed to the Aborigines Protection Board with a view to
consideration being given to the question of providing a more
satisfactory building at Ngoorumba, (b) The tender etc. received from
the transfer of the Bassendean building there to be forwarded to the A.
P. Board. (c) The Board be informed that whilst this Department
recognizes its right to remove the building in question, this Department
would appreciate its retention in its present position, as good use of
it is being made by the Broun's Homestead Subsidised School, and would
be glad if some other means of meeting the requirements of Ngoorumba
could be found. (d) Mrs. Broun be informed that the building belongs to
the Aborigines Protection Board, to whom representations have been made
with a view to its retention in its present condition."

Copy of Letter from Mr. R. T. Harris. Missionary of the A.I.M at TINGHA
Dated 16 December 1935 to the Association for the Protection of Native
Races. "Dear Mr. Watson, I enclose rough sketch of land taken up by the
Aborigines Protection Board at the commencement of this year as a
Reserve for the dark people of this district. You will see how entirely
it is hemmed in between other leases, and how a large proportion is
occupied by a dry creek bed, in places fifteen to twenty feet deep, and
twice as wide. Also the only flat part where it is possible to grow
grass for the horses has been nearly all cut off, leaving two of the
home outside the boundary. The area granted as a reserve is totally
inadequate, and there is frequently friction between the holder of the
surrounding leases and the people. The additional area supplied for some
four to six months ago is shown with the red cross lines. This would
include Spl lease 28.35, as well as other portions which at present do
not seem to be put to very much use. In all it would be about from 200
to 25 acres. The approach to the present reserve is through the lease
mentioned and the including of this in the extension would be a very
welcome addition. It is imperative that something be done to relieve the
present unsatisfactory situation. Then the matter of the new school
building which is urgently required is as follows; Over six years ago we
commenced out missionary work among the dark people of Long Gully, at
first we had no building but carried on three days per week out in the
open, using logs and sheets of bark as desks. The men then erected a
rude shelter of saplings and bushes which was put into use. Just at this
time there was trouble with leasee of 28.35 which lease we were on, so
to avoid further trouble the whole encampment was moved to its present
position, where we have been for the past five and a half years. The
present school building was put up by the men as a Mission Church, and
school was held in it on week days with enrolment of under twenty
children. As time went on my daughter was appointed as subsidized
teacher, and received a subsidy from the Government according to scale.
Each year the attendance increased until the school was, about two years
ago, classed as a Provisional School. By this time the children had
attended in numbers which rendered the one building too small to
accommodate them. We have to divide them, and use a small cabin which
was built for our own use as a kindergarten school. At present there are
three of us teaching each school day, the services of two being
gratuitous. During this year, 1935 we have had to conduct the school in
two separate rooms. Application was made for my other daughter to be
recognized by the department as an assistant teacher. This was not
granted. How they expect one teacher to attend two separate buildings
over twenty yards apart is beyond my knowledge. All the time we have
been conducting school at Long Gully we have resided at Tingha, there
being no residence of any sort available closer to the Settlement. This
means a journey of four miles each way every school day. I have used my
car for the conveyance of the teacher daily in all weathers, and in the
last two years have carried aborigine children living near town to and
from their school. Not one penny has been allowed for this service by
the Education Department, and I think these pointes should be taken into
consideration in the urgent need for better accommodation at a very
early date. The seating and desk accommodation which fill the present
buildings is for 28 pupils. We have 44 on the roll at the close of this
term, and another five children ready to start in the new year. As it is
now they are packed together as tightly as possible, and that makes it
very difficult to get any good result from the children. We have given
our services willingly and freely for the good of these people for the
past six years and look for no compensation for it. Still I think that
the least the Education Department can do would be to provide the
necessary buildings and conveniences to carry on the work. The matter of
a new building has been brought under notice of the authorities from
time to time. The District Inspector off the Education Department was
very empathetic in his last report this year and remarked about the
school being carried on in two wretched huts which very fitly describes
the buildings in present use. The sizes of the buildings are 24 by 11
feet with 8 feet walls build of saplings lined with bags, dirt floor.
Roof made of old, rust and leaky iron, and when it rains it is
impossible for he children to carry on any lesson where the use of ink
is necessary. The other building is about 10 by 10 by 8 feet and into
this we have as many as fourteen children and the teacher. This is built
of scrap timber and roofed with rusty kerosene tins. Truly a credit to
our Departments. A suggestion was made some twelve months ago to put
some improvement on to the present buildings, but this would never meet
the situation. What is wanted is a new school to accommodate at least 50
children and a new area of ground so that a suitable portion can be set
apart for a playground. Again on the advice of the Inspector application
was made twelve months ago for the necessary tools to commence
instructions in hand work among the older boys. This has not even been
replied to although it is provided for in the syllabus of lessons for an
aboriginal school. (signed) R.T.Harris Missionary -- Note: At the last
census of aborigines June 1935 the total number for Tingha District was
129 of these about 100 live on the Settlement (Reserve). The rest are
outside the boundaries there being no room for them inside.

27/10/1933 -- Letter to Department Education from Miriam Broun at
Bassendean Tingha. "Dear Mr. Drummond, We have a school on our property,
which was shifted here from 10 miles out of Inverell some ten years ago.
I think the Department gave it to the Aborigines Protection Board when
the Aborigines left here we started to use it as our subsidized school
and have done so ever since. There is at present some talk of it being
removed -- We strongly object to its removal. It is not worth shifting
in the first place. The man who erected it here says it would not stand
another move. We have painted it and kept it in order and there are
always five children -- sometimes more using it. See what you can do for
us in the matter. I don't want to make a fuss about it, but we are
determined to put up a good fight before we allow it to be removed from
the freehold property. I'm sorry to trouble you over such a small
matter, but who else have we to turn to."

10/10/1934 -- Dept. Education re Unsatisfactory accommodation "The
question of providing more satisfactory accommodation for the Ngoorumba
Aboriginal School was recently referred to the Aborigines Protection
Board for favourable consideration. The Board replied that it had been
decided not to proceed with the matter for the time being in view of the
fact that finality had not been reaching concerning the setting aside of
an area of land as a permanent Aborigines Reserve at Long Gully
(Ngoorumba). When this reserve has been made available, a decision will
be given regarding the erection of a new building or the transfer of a
more suitable one. Mr. Inspector L. F. Kellor has now furnished a
further report with regard to the existing accommodation. "The attention
of the A.P. Board is again drawn to the unsatisfactory nature of the
school accommodation at this settlement. The defects of the existing
structure as a school room were mentioned in the February report.
Sanitary arrangements are gravely inadequate because of the unhygienic
nature of the closets. There is no water supply at the school -- not the
suggestion of a tank, or a wash-basin. The training in handwork, so
essential in a school of this kind, is limited to needlework, the
materials for which are collected in Tingha. Woodchipping and road
repairing represent the training for the boys. It would appear that the
religious devotion of the teacher and her parents enables them to endure
conditions which call for immediate attention." RECOMMENDED that a copy
of this report of Mr. Inspector Kelly be referred to the Secretary of
the Aborigines Protection Board for consideration."

12/12/1935 -- Copy of Letter sent re Ngoorumba Aborigines School Tingha,
from teacher D. N. Harris re Improvements to School Building -- "Nothing
has yet been done in this matter. About twelve months ago, the Police
advised that it was useless to try and put any improvements on to the
existing buildings owing to them being very much out of repair, and
totally unsuitable for school use. A recommendation was made for a new
building in place of the existing temporary ones. Although advised from
different sources twice again through the year absolutely nothing has
been done. We are at present carrying on in two huts, with earth floors,
built of saplings and rusty second-hand iron on roof. The total
accommodation is for 28 children. We have, at the close of this term 44
children on the roll and a possibility of 5 extra ones in the new year.
Unless something is done immediately to relieve the situation there will
be no alternative but to refuse the younger ones admittance for next
year. The lavatory accommodation is in a very bad state and by no means
in a sanitary condition. No clean water is available within the school
grounds and no provision can be made for washing hands etc. A new
building is an urgent necessity. Application has been made to the
Aborigines Protection Board for extra land as the portion now reserved
is too small for the settlement. In the even of this land being granted,
I would advise a new school upon this at once. Otherwise it could be
placed on the land now in use."

27/8/1909 -- Copy of Newspaper article re Ngoorumba . Article Reads \_
NGOORUMBA"S QUEEN -- PROGENITRESS OF AN ABORIGINAL SETTLEMENT. "The
leading aboriginal is a fine old woman of 76 years. She is virtually the
Queen, being either mother, grandmother, or great-grandmother of every
member of the settlement." These remarks were made by Inspector W. G.
Thomas, of the Education Department, in his report to the Aborigines
Board, on his annual inspection of the Ngoorumba Aboriginal School in
the Tingha district. There are 17 pupils (10 boys and seven girls),
taught by Mr. Coombes, who, it was stated, handled his dusky pupils with
consideration and tact, but he had to be watchful in regard to their
personal habits and cleanliness, for in spite of all their teaching they
were inclined to be light-fingered and pilfered chalk and small
articles. A neighboring clergyman paid great attention to the little
community, which comprised half-a-dozen families. The Queen, it was
stated, supported the teacher loyally, and made the children attend
regularly and punctually, and without her assistance the school would
not exist. There was a flower garden at the school. The pupils took keen
interest in its management, and willingly did what duties they were set,
but they did not display the least originality or initiative. About
coins they know nothing, and the young blacks found it difficult to
grasp the ideas of numbers. The Inspector added that the teacher was
worthy of a better school and had nominated for him a larger settlement.
The eldest pupil was 20 years of age, and the average of the lot was 11
years. All read well, spoke what they read correctly, and showed a taste
for poetry. The school building was in good order, and suitably
furnished, but a further supply of materials was necessary, owing to the
progress of the pupils." End of article. Note to Chief Inspector "The
publication of the above, which appeared in the "Evening News (?) of 31
July has aroused the indignation of Mrs. Broun of Bassendean Station,
Tingha who poses as the protectress of the local aboriginal settlement.
I cannot think that the contents of the report were sent to the
Aborigines Board with view to publication. May I ask to be furnished
with my report in order that I may compare it with the above." Signed
JW. G. Thomas.

18/10/1909 -- Police Station Bundarra Subject Mrs Broun's letter re
Aborigines' School and Rations -- "Senior Constable Clark begs to report
that the remarks contained in the attached letter as to the general good
behaviour of the pupils numbering from 18 to 10 are correct, and
considering that these children were making good progress under the
tuition of the later teacher, Mrs. Coombs, it is a pity that up till
date hereof there is no successor. Probably the cause of no fresh
appointment is due to the fact that Mrs. Broun did not give a kind
consideration to the teacher, who was without a doubt a worthy,
Godfearing young man, and one whose characteristics eminently fitted him
for such a school. His salary was not large, and the only place at which
he could obtain board was at Mrs. Broun's, (no other white families
living within the locality) and the amount of board he had to pay was
too high, and moreover charged him for the use of a horse when he wished
to go to town. He was at last obliged to erect a hut on the Reserve and
batch for himself, as will be seen by the Senior Inspectors report upon
the school inspection. It was without doubt the treatment meted out to
the teacher that caused him to seek removal. Mrs. Broun's remark as to
Mr. Coombs' treatment of the settlement in general is wrong, for the
whole of the Aborigines speak highly of the young gentleman, in reply to
inquiries made by the Senior Constable personally on various occasions.
There is no room for doubt whatsoever on this point. The Snr Constable
is of the same opinion as his predecessors, as previous reports will
show, that Mrs. Broun exerts an influence over the Camp generally to
gain her own ends. This is the opinion of private persons as well. The
rations referred to are supplied to those entitled, and the Contractor
can be commended on his good supplies."
