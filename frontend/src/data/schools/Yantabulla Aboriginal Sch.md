"Yantabulla is an extremely remote district, being eighty two miles west
of Bourke. Although the Department may feel sympathetically disposed to
the residents, the application for a Provisional School is one to which
favourable consideration is hardly possible. Sixteen children of school
age are mentioned in the schedule, but three of this number are thirteen
years old. Again, five reside eight miles or more from Yantabulla. As a
matter of fact four a fourteen miles away. It cannot be expected that
these children would attend regularly, if at all. Eleven children are
enrolled at the Subsidised school. It has been in operation only two
months of this year, when the necessary attendance was maintained. The
highest attendance during 1929, however, was only 8.6. It is hardly
likely the present success of the school will continue for any great
length of time. No building is available. IT IS RECOMMENDED that the
application be declined and Mr. W. J. Scully, M. L. A, informed in terms
of the draft reply submitted herewith.

9/11/1931 -- In August 1930, application was made for the establishment
of a Provisional School at Yantabulla ... The case was not strong enough
to warrant favourable consideration of the request and the parents were
urged to establish a subsidized school. This was done and the Subsidised
school has functioned fairly satisfactorily. Recently the figures have
risen above departmental requirements. The attendance this year have
been -- February 6.6, March 6.7, April 6.9, May 12.6, June 11.8, July
12, August 11.2, September 11.8. The main reason, and evidently the only
reason, for the increase is that a number of the aborigine families have
settled at Yantabulla. Their period of residence there is doubtful, but
the opinion is held that they are likely to remain so long as the dole
and other forms of relief are available. They have built themselves tin
huts, an indication that they intend to remain. The teacher-in-charge of
the Subsidised School recently reported the increase in the attendance
and asked if she would receive subsidy for the extra pupils. This led to
an enquiry into the present conditions. There is no doubt that, at
present, the total number of children (white and black) is sufficient to
maintain the departmental requirements, but for how long these
conditions will be maintained is problematic. It would be unwise to send
a teacher such a long distance without some very definite indications
that the present strength of the school would be maintained for a
reasonable period. Moreover, the short time before the Christmas
vacation precludes anything being done towards the appointment of a
departmental teacher. I RECOMMEND that the matter stand over for the
time being. The question might be discussed with Mr. Inspector W. G. Lee
during the Summer vacation and, perhaps, the teacher of the Subsidised
school might be asked to call at this office if in Sydney.

3/1932 -- Dept Education -- Yantabulla Educational requirements. "The
question of making better provision for the educational requirements of
Yantabulla (it is served by a Subsidised School at present) was under
consideration last year. The reports received showed that sufficient
children were available to meet the Departmental requirements, but as
the majority were aborigines, the permanency of whose residence was
doubtful, the Departmental hesitated to appoint a teacher. Further
reports, which have not come to hand from the local Police Officer and
the teacher of the Subsidised school, show that the sufficient permanent
children are available to satisfy the departmental conditions. Each
white children and six aborigines enrolled at the subsidized school
re-opened. Miss Cook says that there are at least twelve more aborigines
to enroll later. At present there are practically without clothing.
Constable Penny's estimate of the number of permanent aborigines
children of school age is 20. This centre is 82 miles north west of
Bourke and the appointment of a teacher would be costly. In view of the
preponderance of dark children, it is considered that the opening of an
aborigines' Provisional School is the best solution to the problem, Miss
Cook could be appointed to the position of teacher-in-charge and her
services could be terminated at any time this was considered necessary
on account of low attendance or any other reason. Recommended (1) that
an Aborigines' Provisional School be opened at Yantabulla in lieu of the
existing Subsidised school; (2) that Miss. G. Cook at present in charge
of the subsidized school be appointed as teacher in charge of the
Aborigines Provisional School, subject to the concurrence of the
Aborigines' Protection Board (3) that the Hon. M. A. Davidson, M. L. A
be informed in terms of the accompanying draft.

2/11/1932 -- Dept. Education "Yantabulla Aboriginal: Accommodation"

"This school is conducted in a galvanized iron shed owned by Mrs. A. E.
Sandford, for which the Aborigines' Protection Board pays a rental of
5/- per week. The decision was that this building was to be occupied
until 3^rd^ September 1932 when, it was thought, more favourable weather
conditions would permit of the occupation of the bough shed provided at
departmental expense. The teacher pointed out, however, that unless the
bough shed was renovated and provided with doors its occupation would
not be possible. A visit was recently made by the local Inspector and
his report is not to hand. From the photographs supplied, it is clear
that the bough shed is of no service. Even if is renovated, and the
local constable will arrange for this to be done, it will not be
possible to occupy during dusty or wet weather. The galvanized iron shed
is badly ventilated but Mrs. Sandford is prepared to make improvements
if the Board pays the outstanding rent and agrees to continue the
tenancy.

That the Department must remain in occupation of Mrs. Sandford's
building is clear but the tenancy can only be continued so long as the
necessary figures are maintained. There are indications of a falling-off
in the attendance. If renovated the bough shed will be available as
additional accommodation but its use will be possible only in fair
weather.

It is recommended:

that the Aborigines' Board be informed that, from the Inspector's report
the Department considers that the school should be continued in Mrs.
Sandford's shed;

that the Board be asked to agree to the continuance of the tenancy so
long as the necessary figures are maintained;

that, in the event of the Board agreeing to continue the tenancy, Mrs.
Sandford be asked to improve the ventilation, but advised that the
tenancy can be continued only so long as the aboriginal provisional
school is in operation.

2/10/1932 - Yantabulla: Special Report upon Accommodation -- Sir, I have
to report that I visited Yantabulla Aborigines' School on 28 September.
This school was raised from Subsidised to Provisional status at the
beginning of 1932. Many families had, at that time taken up residence
near the town. Mainly through police activity, all but one family has
not moved on. The enrolment now shows eight white children and five
abos. School is conducted in a galvanized iron shed (see photographs)
owned by Mrs. Sandford. This shed needs more ventilation. The bough shed
referred to, which I am informed cost the department £15, is in a bad
state of disrepair (see photograph). Still, the framework is good, and
it may be reconditioned by residents. I spoke to the local policeman Mr.
Penny, who is willing to assist in make the bough shed habitable. In
dusty weather however it will be an impossible place for a school. In
wet weather also it will be impossible. The bough shed is erected on
land owned by Mrs. Horton. Although it was erected without her consent,
Mrs. Horton informed me that she has no objection whatever to the use of
the land by the department. It would seem that both the galvanized room
and the bough shed are needed in this locality. The Aborigines'
Protection Board has promised Mrs. Sandford a rent of five shillings per
week ill 3^rd^ September. To date, nothing has been paid. Mrs. Sandford
is willing to supply the much needed extra ventilation on condition that
the Board pays the rent due to date, and decides to continue the
tenancy. This I would recommend. The bough shed is to be reconditioned
without expense to the Department. It might not be out of place to
mention that the school building once owned by the Department is now in
the possession of the local hotel keeper. Mr. Toohill. It is used as a
play room for his own children who are being educated by a governess."
Signed Inspector (Note to Wendy: This correspondence includes 2
photographs --one of the bough shed and the other of Miss Cook and
pupils in front of tin shed.)

8/7/1932 -- Accommodation and need for furniture and materials -- Memo

concerted to an Aborigines' Provisional School from Subsidised School
status. The present enrolment is 22 pupils, seven of whom are white and
15 of aborigine descent. The accommodation in use is an unlined and
unceiled galvanized iron shed. It is owned by Mrs. A. E. Sandford who

pay the rent of the present accommodation until 3^rd^ September, 1932,
when the school may be transferred to the Department's bough shed; (2)
