**Role of the Department and relationship to the APB**

**Establishment of School --** "Bassendean School for Aboriginal
children -- (20/1/1924) -- application was made some time last year for
the establishment of a provisional school at Bassendean, near Tingha.

Property -- W.J. Brown of Bassendean whose property is utilised for the
purposes of the school has written to the APB concerning the payment of
rent. The Department of Education does not agree to make payment for the
rental -- stating that "It is recommended that the Aborigines Protection
Board be informed that other than the payment of the teacher's salary
this Department is unable to consider any further expenditure. The
question of the payment of rent, if any, is a matter for adjustment
between the Board and the owner of the building utilized.

**Responsibility for opening of school -** Department of Education --
5/7 -- "Sir, This Department has had under notice the formal application
dated 7^th^ may last, for the establishment of a Public School at
Bassendean. In connection therewith I desire to inform you that it is
not the practice of this Department to establish a school for aboriginal
children. The entire responsibility of educating such children is a
function of the Aborigines' Protection Board. In the circumstances the
matter has been referred to the Aborigines' Protection Board for
whatever arrangements it considers necessary to provide educational
facilities for the children concerned" (S.H.Smith, Under Secretary)

Corresp. From Mr. Wood to Department Education "Dear Sir, I have a note

References for Mr. Wood from Inspector of Schools Lismore and others

conscientious worker and a capable teacher, that he had wide experience
as a teacher both in Scotland and in NSW. He is courteous and kind in

Mr. Wood recognized as a certified teacher in terms of article 7 of the

acted as an assistant teacher in Edinburgh since March 1917 -- stated

Mr. Wood is a thoroughly sober, respectable man.

Application to Department Education 10/1/1923 -- John Davidson Wood --

19/12/1870.

Memo Dept of Education - 12/12/1924 -- Question of the employment of a
Temporary Teacher of Sewing. Mr. Wood -- teacher in charge, applies for
authority for the employment of Mrs. Bawtree as Teacher of Sewing at the
school under his charge. There is an enrolment of 10 girls, with the
average attendance of 9.5.

The memo goes on to state "Under ordinary circumstances the appointment
of a Temporary Teacher of Sewing to a school under the charge of a
unclassified teacher would not be agreed to, Aborigines' schools,
however, must be treated differently. Sewing forms a definite part of
their curriculum. Under these circumstances I RECOMMEND that the
authority be given for the employment of Mrs. Bawtree as Temporary
Teacher of Sewing with payment at the rate of 2/6 per hour for not more
than four hours weekly. " Approved refer to Public Service Board. The
Public Service Board approve employment to be in terms of Section 44 of
the Public Service Act 1902.

25/6/1925 -- Application by Mr. J.P Howard, Teacher, Aboriginal School
Bassendean, for continuance of living allowance. -- The Public Service
Board do not see their way to approve Mr. Howard being paid living
allowance whilst stationed at Bassendean.

**Teacher** -- Memo re sewing allowance

11/2/1926 From Mr. Howard to Department Education -- requesting that the
sewing allowance usually paid to a Teachers wife doing this duty, might
be paid to Mrs. Howard. As she teaches over 20 students every week and a
splendid display of work done was made on the breaking up day, when each
child presented parents and lady missionaries with articles made by
themselves, clothing, towels, fancy work etc. Note from Department
"Needlework is an essential part of the training of Aboriginal girls.
Mrs. Howard teaches the subject with distinct success. The pupils work
is highly creditable. Mrs. Howard gives the girls a training in
housework as required by the syllabus for aboriginal schools as well as
teaches sewing."

14/7/1926 -- Memo re sewing allowance from Mr. Howard to Department.

Requesting that his wife can be put on the same basis as other Teachers
wives who are sewing teachers. "The work done here is very much more
difficult than with white children, and by no means congenial. We have
an enrolment of 30 pupils, 15 of each sex, and Mrs. Howard has taught
the sewing here since July of last year."

Application for increased sewing allowance by Mr.J.P Howard, Teacher
18/9/1926. Mr. Howard asks that his sewing allowance be increased to
(pound) 32:10:0 per annum, the allowance paid in the case of a
classified teacher in charge of a 6^th^ class school. Approval was given
by the Public Service Board, no 26/3724 for the employment of Mrs.
Howard with payment of the rate of 2/6 d. per hour for not more than
four hours weekly. In forwarding the application, Mr. Inspector McKenzie
stated "Mrs. Howard is at present paid on the basis of a temporary
teacher of sewing. I consider the request reasonable and recommend the
usual sewing allowance (pound) 32:10:00 -- for a Teachers wife be paid"
-- On reviewing the matter I am disposed to agree with Mr. McKenzie.
Sewing and Manual Arts form an important part of the Aboriginal School
Curriculum. Recommended.

5/5/1925 -- Memo from J.P. Howard Teacher in Charge at Bassendean.
Application for continuation of living allowance. M. Howard had been
transferred from Terry Hie Hie, Moree, to Bassendean, Tingha. His living
allowance had been stopped. It was stopped on 31/12/1924 and he did not
leave Terry Hie Hie until 28/2/1925. Mr. Howard has five children, one
very delicate and continually under medical treatment. He is finding
hard to manage -- they are living 12 miles from Tingha with no coach or
communication.

As above -- Chief Inspector writes to Mr. Howard "With reference to your
application for continuation of Living Allowance, I desire to inform you
that the Public Service Board do not see their way to approve for your
request." (H.D. McLelland)

21/1/1926 -- Mr. Howard writes to the Chief Inspector requesting an
interview with respect to a decision regarding his living allowance, and
also the granting of a sewing allowance to his wife.

Memo from Mr. J.P Howard to Department concerning Application for
consideration of payment of special allowance to meet extra cost of
living conditions due to being situated 12 miles from all sources of
supply.

Transcription of memo

"In was transferred to Bassendean in March 1925, from Terry Hie Hie,
Moree. There my salary was (pound) 273 p.a. plus living allowance. Here
we are outside the living allowance area and after 15 months residence
we are finding it hard to struggle to manage on the salary received. We
are 12 miles from Tingha, the nearest source of supply, and there is
neither mail nor carry-service in this direction. We have to go to
Tingha for everything, even our mails. As the railway service to
Inverell, from whence Tingha goods are received, comes through Moree, a
further hundred miles, then cartage to Tingha, it is clearly seen that
the cost of living is no less than at Moree, on top of which we have to
go to Tingha to get everything. I have five children, under 11 years,
one of whom is delicate and constantly under medical treatment. The
education of my children is a problem which will soon have to be faced,
they will have to be sent away, it being impossible for them to get what
is required at an Aboriginal school, apart from the undesirability of
their association with such. My own health is such that I carry as heavy
insurance as possible, for which I pay a loaded premium of (pound) 21
p.a. I have lost one kidney and have had a serious mastoid operation,
and a similar condition had developed in my other ear. I also pay
(pound) 15. p.a for superannuation.

Under the circumstances I would be very grateful if consideration could
be given to my position with a view to a special allowance being made to
meet the expensive conditions existing here. I might add that my
insurance policy was taken out some years ago before my family
obligations were so heavy, and under urgent medical advice. Such could
not have been done here, and although it is a heavy load to carry I do
not care to let it lapse after paying so long, and we are hoping to be
able to borrow something from the Company to help with the education of
our children."

note from Department ... facts are known to be correct. Concerned that
granting of a special allowance would set precedent which would involve
considerable expenditure.

Application forwarded to the Public Service Board for consideration --
27/7/1926
