16/1/1934 -- Letter from Police Station Maclean to Mr. L. G. Kendall
Kensington -- Dear Mr. Kendall, No doubt you are wondering why I have
not replied to your letter of the 29^th^ ultimo, but you will see by the
above address that I have left New Angledool. Your letter did not reach
me until the 13^th^ instant, but owing to my absence in Grafton on
important business, I have not had time to reply earlier. Regarding Mr.
Cavill. He was not charged with an offence "Driving a Car" under the
influence of liquor, during my time, but he was most fortunate in this
report. I saw him one afternoon at a Hospital meeting at Angledool, he
was well under the influence, and later, the same afternoon I saw his
car in front of Barrett's Hotel, a few minutes after six. I went to the
Hotel fully prepared to proceed against him if possible, but as I got to
the gate, he came out very much under the influence, but another man
came to his aid and drove the car home, with Cavill to the Mission. It
was owing to the fact that a sober and licensed driver took the car from
him, that I did not take proceedings. However the matter was
subsequently reported to Mr. Smithers, AP Board Inspector, and the
Commissioner of Police, forwarded the papers for me to report. My report
was very strong against Mr Cavill, and although Cavill denied the charge
of being drunk whilst he should have been on duty at the Mission, he was
told that his statement was unsatisfactory, and that there was no doubt
in the mind of the Commissioner that he was under the influence. At the
same time he received a final warning, that should he indulge in
intoxicating liquor in the future, steps would be taken to dispense with
his services. Apart from this, I have seen him under the influence of
liquor on several occasions. You probably know why he lost his job as
Manager at Angledool. It was proved that he was selling mutton, sugar,
flower &c to the Abos, and although he said it was his own property, the
Department had their own opinion, and he was transferred to Walgett, as
a teacher only. There are many other occasions whilst I was at
Angledool, that Mr. Cavill was absent from his station, sometime for
hours at a time, and on one occasion if my memory serves me right, he
was away for two days, and there is not the slightest doubt there were
days when the children had to the best for themselves at school. I was
also informed that near the latter part of his term there Mrs. Cavill
looked after the school. I do not know of my own person knowledge, but
it was generally known that once just prior to one of your visits of
Inspection, he was miles behind with his books, and when he heard from
Barrett's that you were coming, he spent the day and night entering up.
This of course may not be of any use to you, but it goes to show how
things were neglected. There is nothing else that I can say that would
help you, except that I am sure he was convicted for using obscene
language at Walgett, shortly after he went there. I am sorry that I have
not been able to reply before now, but your letter went to Angledool,
and was re-addressed here. I think it was the Prentice family that first
told us of your transfer, and I can assure you I was pleased to hear of
it. Maitland is a nice place, I know that part pretty well, and may see
you that way some time. You will also be in handy distance from the
City. I had a letter from Mr. Pettit just after Xmas, he appears to have
had a bad time, all have been on the sick list. I was transferred to
take charge of Maclean, in July last, and I can assure you I am also
pleased with the move. Now that I am settled down and have had a good
look round, I am quite sure that there are few better stations from a
police point of view in the State. I have wonderful quarters to live in,
electric light everywhere, and the town is very compact, with population
of almost 2000 in the Municipality. I have one foot and one mounted
Constable with me. Kind regards and best wishes for the future. Yours
Sincerely Andrew M(?))

1/7/1936 -- Dept of Education to APB -- Subject - A report on the Gingie
Gingie Mission Station -- The following is a copy of a report received
from Dr. Donnellan, who visited the Gingie Gingie Mission Station on the
13^th^ June, 1936. "On Saturday, June 13, 1936, in company with
Constable Southwell of Walgett, I proceeded to Gingie Gingie Mission
Station situated 6 miles from Walgett. I examined the eyes of 36 adults
and 26 children. 26 out of 36 adults were suffering trachoma of various
disease of chronicity. Two adults had conjunctivitis. Of the children,
20 of them had trachoma and five of them had conjunctivitis (non
purulent). I took conjunctival smears of seven cases of conjunctivitis
which have been forwarded by the Police to the Microbiologist,
Department of Health. These were all the cases which could be found that
day, but I understand from the Police that there are a number of
aborigines camped in various places round the town of Walgett. The huts
of these aborigines were more or less clean and tidy, but there are no
sanitary arrangements whatever. There is nobody in charge of the
Station. The school is closed. I consulted Dr. Schwartz of Walgett in
regards to the treatment of these cases, but he said that they would not
present themselves at the Hospital, or absented themselves after a short
time. I consider that the only way of effectively dealing with the eyes
of these patients is to place them under the proper supervision of a
Manager and competent Nurse, or to remove them to another Station, eg,
Pilliga or Brewarrinna. The information is forwarded for the
consideration of your Board." G.R. Thomas Director of Education

2/8/1932 -- Letter from Teacher Gingie Gingie to APB "Some months ago I
wrote you in reference to rations for the single men on the reserve.
When those who are unemployed and not in receipt of rations are here, it
means that the family supply of food runs out a couple of days before
ration day and the children of that family come to school hungry and
have to be fed by Miss Acland (Missionary) or myself. Although the
difficulty has been a good deal lessened recently, it still exists and I
would be glad if something definite could be done -- either to give
these men rations or keep them off the reserve all together. Here is one
instance: - one lad Alan Lance, who is not quite normal and who finds it
hard to get work, has not received rations at Walgett during the past
five years. He went to Collarendabri, Lightening Ridge and Angledool and
at each place was given a certain amount of rations and told definitely
to return to his own town -- Walgett. He is frequently here for weeks at
stretch and helps eat the family supply. I do not know the regulations
regarding these men but it seems to me that some provision should be
made. The missionary and I have met many of the needs of these people
during the winter months -- clothes as well as food, but cannot keep it
up indefinitely. Families, where each member is in receipt and rations,
seem to manage quite well and there is no lack. If some of the single
men receive rations, why not all? Trusting this matter will be
adjusted." Signed McAuley PS: Other lads who are often on the reserve
without food are Gordon Morgan, Eric Morgan and Billy Morgan.

25/8/1932 -- Letter to A.C. Pettit APB -- "Dear Sir, No doubt you will
think I am always complaining but I do not wish to do that. A good deal
of the trouble here arises from the fact that I do not know quite what
is required of me, nor do I know what is due to the people. Officially,
of course, I am merely the teacher, and my duties those of a teacher.
However, as you know, I am endeavoring to perform the duties of matron
without knowing many things a matron should know. For instance, everyone
who stays on the reserve reports to me, and asks to permission to stay.
Now to handle this in a satisfactory manner I should have a list of
those who have been expelled from the reserve. According to the Sergeant
of Police, quite a few have been and others are what he terms
'undesirables'. From what I can gather, most of the men are undesirable.
Then again, the people come to me about their ration troubles. They are
too scared to enquire for themselves and I do not know who is supposed
to get rations and who is not. Ever since I have been here Friday was
ration day till in June it was moved to Monday which meant making seven
days rations last for ten. Then again about a month ago the day was
moved to the following Thursday which meant another ten days. On the
Monday everyone was absolutely out of food and as it was impossible to
have the children in school for three days without food I interviewed
the Inspector of Police. He gave a satisfactory explanation and arranged
for the rations to be given Monday that Week, Tuesday the next and so on
till Thursday was reached. This was a great help but even then it meant
making the rations spin out for eight days. (Page 2) Some of the single
men receive rations and some do not. Naturally they inquire why this is.
Gordon Morgan and Alan rance (?or nance) were told that if they lived in
town they would get the dole. Their homes are here and they have nowhere
to stay in town. Joe Kennedy was told, when he asked for dole, that he
would get rations if he went to the reserve. Now, what [are]
they supposed to do? If I am not supposed to concern myself about these
matters I shall gladly relinquish all but school duties. Then about
clothes. I was given to understand that I must see that the children are
clean and tidy at School. To this end, they have a weekly hot bath in
the school as well as a wash twice daily for which I have provided
combs, towel and soap. But about clothes I have just had to manage the
best way I could. With Miss Aclands help and some parcels of clothes
from Sydney we managed to keep them somewhere near the mark. I quite
understand that all Govt. Depts. Are hard pressed at present and I have
been glad to do what I could but I do think that this is unfair -- that
Govt. clothe should be left in the Police cupboard when there are needy
people here. Out of the clothes you sent only three women and three
girls received any. I saw quite a number of others at the Police Station
and the Inspector told me that I could take them as well as a number of
left over boys clothes and give them out as the need arose. Miss Acland
made a special trip for them and was given a bundle for 6 people only: -
Mrs. Thomas, Mrs. Lance, Mrs, Morgan and children , Thelma Morgan. Doris
and Annie Lance. Mellie Sharpley who has attended this school ever since
my advent received nothing. No explanation was given for this change of
mind. It seems to be a characteristic vagary of the Police mind to
promise and not to perform. There were, I think, clothes for the Dodd
girls but these were refused on (page 3) the grounds that they received
endowment clothes. There are six children and they had one £5 worth of
clothes since I came (seven months ago) and one of them came to school
so indecently clad that I had to suspend her from class while Miss
Acland made her some garments from some of mine. I intended filling in
endowment papers for the others but have changed my mind as those who
get it do not receive any more than the others. When I cam here the
Sergeant told me that the Police would help me all they could. They are
about as much help as a mirage in a desert. The best way to get a thing
done is to do it myself. One of the men's huts had no lavatory and I
asked the Police to get them to erect one as they had the disgusting
habit of using the river bank. It was a matter that could not wait and
as three weeks have elapsed I have had it done myself. As I am no
architect the building is rather a nondescript affair. I also got the
men to (build) erect a windlass and wire to the river to help in our
gardening operations. Although the police promised me some pulley blocks
and they were sent for a few times I had to procure some myself. We have
planted ten packets of seeds and the ground was dug into borrowed pick
and a broken spade. I wrote you some time ago about gardening tools;
also several times about sewing materials. If the Board cannot supply
these things I should be glad to know. Some kind of answer would be much
appreciated. A child was badly burned a few weeks ago but was not bad
enough to be sent to hospital The Dr. suggested my using picrie(?) and
lotion which I bought for 2/-. I have also bought stuff for a poisoned
leg and other things. In such cases, where no remedy has been supplied
and where the patient is not ill enough for hospital, are the police
authorized to supply the need? I did not apply to them the above
mentioned cases. I wrote to you about extra remedies and I have no doubt
my letter is receiving attention. I understood before I left Sydney that
the end of the verandah near my room would be gauzed in. For reasonable
comfort this is a necessity. Even throughout the winter months, the blow
flies have been so bad that in desperation I bought some wire gauze for
my windows and by keeping the door shut and the free us of "Ping" I have
managed to combat the evil a little. But I cannot keep my door closed
during these hot days. In any case it is unsanitary to sleep and eat and
keep food and clothes all in one room 12ft square. I have only been
hanging on in the hope that conditions would improve a little and that
my position would be more clearly defined. As I am not sure if I have
been acting outside my sphere I shall perform only my school duties
until I hear from you In fact I feel that unless matters are placed on a
more satisfactory footing I must relinquish my position. I would be
sorry to do this as I like the work and find the people fairly
tractable, but it is not worth the worry and inconvenience I have had.
Trusting that this letter will have a better fate than some of the
others and that it will at least elicit a reply." Signed Margaret
McAnlay

29/8/1932 -- Police Station Walgett -- The Inspector of Police, Walgett
-- Subject: Miss Mc Auley complaining about matters at the Gingie
Aborigines Reserve, Walgett. Reference: - Instructions Chairman AP Board
No.118/28493 of 19.8.32 -- I beg to report that all aborigines on the
Gingie Aborigines Reserve entitled to rations have been supplied with
the same and every consideration is extended to them by the local Police
and has been for the past five years to my knowledge. The three Morgan
boys referred to by Miss Mc Auley are mostly in work on surrounding
Stations and drink their money when they earn it, and then expect to be
kept by the Board when loafing on the Reserve. The three Morgan's were
told by the Police that if they would look for work they would be
supplied with rations to enable them to do so, but they did not come for
rations. Alan Lance was also offered rations if he would look for work
but he never called for them. He usually follows boxing contests from
place to place in the district and wont work while quite able to do so.
There has never been trouble on the Reserve only when made by female
teachers. During the last term Miss Davies stay on the Reserve she
became troublesome and had to leave. From the time of her departure
until the arrival of Miss McAuley (a matter of months) things went very
smoothly at the Reserve, and there were no complaint of any kind. Miss
Mc Auley carried out her duties satisfactorily for a time and worked in
harmony with the Police, who assisted her in every way. During the past
three months she appears to have lost her reason and I am now satisfied
that she is mentally deranged, and getting worse every day. On the
12^th^. June last, she was found wandering near the Dangar Bridge 3 1/2
miles from Walgett by a lorry driver, she had then lost her reason and
could give no account of herself or her actions. She was taken to the
District Hospital at Walgett and remained there for 12 days. She gave a
lot of trouble to Dr. Schwartz G.M.O of walgett and the Hospital staff.
She kept wandering about the grounds and could not be kept in bed and
imagined that she was in bed all the time. It was believed that she had
met with an accident on the day she was found by the lorry driver but
the Doctor has informed me that from his observations more an indication
that she was more or less mentally deranged. I am of the opinion that
Miss McCauly should be relieved from duty at the Reserve." Signed
Sergeant 1^st^ Class.

6/9/1932 -- Letter from Police Station Walgett to Chairman APB -- RE
Complaints by Miss McAuley, teacher at Gingie Gingie Reserve. "I beg
report having perused the attached file. In a previous file I reported
that, in my opinion, Miss McAuley should be relieved from her present
duties. The Aborigines are being sympathetically dealt with both in food
and clothing. The people are fed and fairly well clothed. Naturally,
where endowment is being received or where the father is earning good
money, as in Dodds case, the issue of Rations are cut down as it is
considered that by making these people purchase their food, when able to
do so, it makes them more self reliant, encourages them to work and is
less of an expense to the Board. Clothing is not given out
indiscriminately. It is given out to the best advantage as, if given out
in one lot, the children would very soon be naked. In my opinion Miss
McAuley has no control whatever over these people and her attitude
encourages them in becoming lazy and useless. The Station owners give
more employment to the Aboriginal then they do to the white people and
the Reserve should not be so expensive to the Department. I will
interview Miss McAulary on 8^th^ inst with a view to trying to work in
harmony."

14/9/1932 -- Letter from Teacher to The Secretary AP Board. "Dear Sir, I
have had a visit today from Mr. Smithers who is, I believe, a
representative of the A. P. Board. If the sentiments he expressed are
those of the Board I am rather surprised. This conversation with me
consisted of a series of accusations of reprimands. He had already seen
the Police and came ready to find fault with me. After the usual
greetings he asked if I had been a missionary in the A.I.M and inferred
that I was not viewing things from the right standpoint. If he means
that I am soft or sentimental about the people he is mistaken. As a
matter of fact I have never been a missionary but have visited some of
the Stations. Since coming here I have not communicated with Mrs. Long
either directly or indirectly. I do not take any part in the missionary
effort here, except that on a few occasions I played the organ at a
meeting. Then Mrs. Smithers told me that I was a teacher only and that
the matters I wrote about do not concern me. I maintain that they do. I
have to live on the reserve and for my own personal comfort, if for no
other reason, I have had to do many things not included in my school
duties. For instance when I am awakened during the night by the screams

Men sometimes came home drunk, after midnight, even as late as 3 A.M,
disturbing everyone, by calling from the boat. Since I prohibited the
use of the boat after a certain hour I have had no trouble. Even the
fights were no serious but would have become so, but for my
interference. Then again there was some difficulty about a couple of the
confinement cases and I was called up in the small hour of the morning.
For humanity sake I had to go and do what I could. I take it that the

acted on that assumption and by care and attention have prevented more

I must insist on its use. Did you mean me to wait till some future date

inquiries -- In company with Inspector Ryan O.I.C. Walgett, Police

10/10/1932 -- Dept Education to APB -- Subject: The case of Miss
Margeret McAulay, Gingie Gingie Abor. School -- Miss McAulay was
medically examined at this office on the 26^th^ Ultimo. Her condition
physically and mentally was found to be satisfactory. Dr. Schwartz, of
Walgett, who had been treating her for a nervous condition about July,
supposedly after a fall from a bicycle, stated, however, in a letter to
me, that he considered that she had been suffering from mental
derangement at that time, and he very much doubted the occurrence of an
accident. HE stated that she was excitable and uncontrollable, but
improved under treatment. Miss McAuley, however, stated that her
condition was due to and followed the accident, but she also admitted
that she was very worried over circumstances concerning the treatment of
the blacks in and around the camp, and that she had had differences with
the Police over these matters. Although Miss McAuley appeared to be
quite normal and rational at the time of examination at this office, I
am of the opinion, in view of Dr. Schwartz's report, that she did have a
definite mental disturbance, probably in the nature of hysteria; whether
this was caused or aggravated by certain happenings is not quite clear.
I would recommend that Miss McAuley be permitted to resume duty, and be
given further trial. In the meantime, I am requesting Dr. Schwartz to
furnish me with particulars of any further developments in her case."
Signed Principal Medical Officer
