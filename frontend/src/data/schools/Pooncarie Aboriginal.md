N/d -- Dept. Education re Establishment of Aborigine School -- "The
Board for the Protection of Aborigines recently brought under notice the
need for educational facilities for the black children on and near the
Aborigines' Reserve at Pooncarie. It was represented that about 20
children were without any education whatever. Miss Murkins, a local
resident, had expressed her willingness to accept the position of
teacher. For accommodation it was suggested that a temporary bough
shelter shed, which could be erected without cost, might be used for a
few months, to ascertain if a more permanent arrangement by justified.
Mr. Inspector R. C. Taylor has visited the locality. He found thirteen
children of school age (including two half castes) without schooling and
was informed that about eight prospective pupils were temporarily absent
from the locality. There were about twelve children under school age.
The parents seemed very anxious to obtain a school. Miss Murkins created
a favourable impression on the Inspector. It might be stated that this
young lady is sister-in-law of the local Constable and this relationship
is likely to impress the inhabitants of the Reserve. Owing to the danger
of fire Mr. Taylor does not favour the Board's suggestion regarding
accommodation. He also thinks that it is not fitting for a white girl to
have to teach in such a structure. A large tent, with a screen of
boughs, would be more suitable but this would be fairly expensive and
the fire danger would still exist. Mr. Taylor considers that a temporary
galvanized iron structure, with space between the walls and the ceiling,
to be closed with blinds, would be most satisfactory. The cost of the
material should be the only expense, the erection being effected by the
aborigines under the direction of the local Constable. In connection
with this case it is well to point out that the conditions at present
existing are extraordinary. The people are of a nomadic type who, in
good seasons, obtain employment on the stations in the district. Owing
to the drought, with a consequent reduction in employment, the
aborigines have been concentrated on the Reserve where they receive
rations. When good rains have fallen it is almost certain that there
will be an exodus from the locality. It will, therefore, be seen that
any arrangements made must be regarded as temporary only, at least, for
some time. Mr. Inspector Taylor considers that educational facilities
should be provided and recommends: -

That an Aborigines' School be established as an experiment on the
Pooncarie Aborigine Reserve.

That the Department provide blackboard, easel, table, chair, desks and
forms. Also 24 copies of Primer 1 and slates

That the Aborigines Protection Board be asked to provide material and
arrange for the erection of a temporary structure as favoured by him.

That Miss. H. M. Murkins be appointed to the school as a temporary
teacher on probation.

These recommendations might be adopted."

APB 10/5/1929 -- Subject -- Inspection of Aborigines Reserve and Camps
at Pooncarie by Inspector Donaldson and Secretary. "We beg to report,
for the information of the Board, having paid a visit to the Aborigines
Reserve and outlying camps at Pooncarie, on the 25^th^ ultimo. There is
an Aboriginal population in the district of perhaps one hundred persons.
There is a Reserve containing 620 acres, situated three miles for the
township of Pooncarie. The area is fenced and has one galvanized iron
hut erected thereon, which accommodates one family. The rest of the
people reside in shanties or gunyahs of their own construction either on
the Reserve or at outlying places along the river bank. Owing to the
drought numbers of Aborigines, who usually secure employment elsewhere,
have concentrated at Pooncarie, where there have been 55 on the ration
list. This number will of course again decrease when good rains have
fallen. There are at present about twenty children of school age on or
near the Reserve, who have never had any education. We accordingly made
inquiries as to the possibilities of establishing a school, and have
arranged for a Miss Murkins, who is a sister of Constable McAvoy's wife
and is now residing with them, to submit an application for the
position. A temporary bough shelter shed will be erected on the Reserve
without cost, in which school could be conducted for a few months, thus
enabling the Board to ascertain whether a more permanent arrangement
would be justified. It is anticipated that, if a school were
established, the women and children would not, as hitherto, follow the
men about the country looking for employment, but would remain on the
Reserve. Later on, the question of erecting some more huts could also be
considered. It might be here stated that this Reserve, because of its
remoteness, has not previously been inspected by an Officer of the
Board, and therefore anything now attempted must be more or less
experimental, and the outlay of much money would not be justified. It is
therefore recommended that approval be given for the above proposals to
be given effect." Signed (Sgd) R. Donaldson INSPECTOR and (Sgd.) A.C.
Pettit. Secretary.

23/9/1931 -- Memo from A. H. Dyce Inspector to Chief Inspector Dept.
Education re Pooncarie Aborigines School. Subject: The matter of
re-opening the School. "I have to state that I visited Pooncarie on the
18^th^ inst. and found that the Aborigines' School at that place not in
operation. The reason for this is that the reservation on which the
school is situated is surrounded by flood waters. The Police authorities
have removed the whole aboriginal population to a place outside the
flood area about four miles from the town of Pooncarie. The people are
housed in tents and the children are prevented from attending the school
by reason of its isolation. As this isolation of the reservation will
last for some considerable time -- probably until Christmas -- I am of
the opinion that some measures should be taken to provide temporary
accommodation where the children could receive instruction until the
school is available. I discussed this matter with the officer in charge
of the Police Station at Pooncarie, Mr. McAvoy, who agreed with me that
a temporary structure roofed with a tarpaulin could be built without
cost at a place about two miles from the town and half way to camp. In
order that this may be done immediately and the school brought into
operation, I recommend that this suggestion be forwarded to the
Aborigines' Protection Board with the request that authority be given to
the Police Officer at Pooncarie to have the work done without delay. I
might point out that the Teacher of the Pooncarie Abor. School is at
present in attendance at the local Public School. She is anxious to
return to her own school and will be glad if an arrangement such as I
suggest can be made as soon as possible." Signed A. H. Dyce Inspector

Letter sent to APB re letter from Inspector Dyce on 2/10/1931

25/9/1931 -- Memo to APB from Constable McAvoy -- Re Conditions of
Aborigine School Pooncarie, and when likely to reopen. "I beg to report
that the Aborigine School at Pooncarie and the Reserve are completely
surrounded by water, the Darling River being at the 21 ft 6 in level at
Pooncarie, and in all probability, the road to the school will not be
trafficable until February or March 1932. Miss Trench is now employed,
and has been, for the past six weeks, assisting teaching at the
Pooncarie Public School." Signed Constable McAvoy.Note from APB to Dept.
Ed "Forward for information. In the event of Miss Trench's services not
being require to the Pooncarie Public School whilst waiting for the
Aborigines' School to re-open, it is suggested that perhaps she might be
transferred to the Aborigines School at Gingie Gingie via Walgett, which
is the only vacancy under this Department. The transfer would involve a
train journey of approximately 690 miles, for which a first class
railway fare, on a mileage basis, would be about £5.10.0. Under the
circumstances I would be pleased to learn whether the cost of transfer
could be defrayed by your Department." Note from Chief Inspector "Miss
Trench's services are not required at Pooncarie where the enrolment is
less than 30. Already she has been there some weeks and is being paid
salary for merely observing. The cost of transfer to Gingie Gingie would
exceed £5/10/- and should, I consider, be a charge of the Aborigines
Protection Board.

8/10/1931 Memo to APB from Dept. Education "I desire to inform you that
this Department is not prepared to bear the cost of transfer of Miss
Trench from Pooncarie to Gingie Gingie."

8/10/1931 -- Memo from Inspector to Chief Inspector Dept. Education --
RE: reopening of Pooncarie Aboriginal School. "I have to state that
while passing through Pooncarie on my way back from Wentworth. I
interviewed Constable McAvoy, and he agreed to erect a temporary
building so that the pupils of the Pooncarie Aborigines' School may be
receive instruction pending the return to normal of the flood waters at
present surrounding the reservation on which the school is situated. As
soon as normal conditions return, the school will be resumed in the
original building. The teacher has now been notified by Constable McAvoy
that the temporary structure is ready and she has returned to duty on
5^th^ instant.

26/10/1931 -- APB to Dept. Education re Temporary Aborigines School
Pooncarie -- "In reply to your above memo regarding the inability of the
Teacher of the Aborigines School at Pooncarie to conduct school in the
regular building upon the Reserve, I beg to advise the receipt of a
report from the local Police to the effect that a temporary bush school
has been erected near the township for use of the Aborigines, until such
time as the road is trafficable to the school on the Aborigines Reserve.
School was started in the temporary building on the 5^th^ instant." Note
from Chief Inspector "Noted, The Aborigines have returned to the
reservation and the school there has resumed operations."

**Teachers**

Letter from Ms. Yolanda .... Trench 14 September 1931 -- "Dear Sir, I
received the enclosed memorandum (copy) from the Department on Saturday.
It is impossible to get Board and residence any nearer to my school than
my present abode. I think there has been some mistake made with
reference to this. I filled in the form Mr. Harkness sent me, making an
application for traveling allowance to and from the school. I think the
best thing for met to do, would be to send a copy of the memo on the
Aboriginal Protection Board. I had a letter from the Secretary some
months back -- re traveling to and from school. He said he had discussed
it with Mr. Harkness, the letter told him he had sent me a form to send
it on to you. I can't picture myself boarding with one of the Abos, that
is the only alternative that could be arrived at, or build quarters for
a teacher near the settlement. I camped out near the school for a month
to get over the difficulty of traveling backwards and forwards during
the wet weather. I have to give that up, as Constable McAvoy, informed
me, he had word from the APB telling him this was not allowed. It
wouldn't be of the slightest use interviewing the Abo's, they wouldn't
know what I was talking about. Please advise me of what to do." Note
from Chief Inspector -- "As this is an Aborigines' School and was not
established in the ordinary way I fail to see how the clause about Board
applies. Miss Trench is obliged to stay at Pooncarie and cannot possibly
live at the Mission Station.
