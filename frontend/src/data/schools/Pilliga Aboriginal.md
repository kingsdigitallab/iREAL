1910 - Police District of Bourke -- "The Aborigines are all in good
health & have been for some time, the only sickness being of a trifling
nature." General Remarks and Recommendations: "The Aborigines at this
camp are not receiving any education, the distance is 4 miles to school
but it is impossible to do much with them by forcing them to school. By
amalgamating Pilliga, Cuttabri and the old Redbank camps about 25
children of school ages could be numbered & which would be sufficient to
give them a school of their own. Most of the latter tribe have until
recently been supplied with rations at Pilliga, & if brought here would
no doubt be contented, which is everything with an aboriginal. The land
would be suitable for mixed cultivation & fruit growing, & if properly
managed could to a great extent be made self supporting." Senior
Constable. Note from Chairman APB -- "Can the Aborigines at Redbank and
Cuttabri be induced to take up their residence at Pilliga Camp? If they
did so, would in mean that they would be giving up employment and could
they obtain suitable work in the vicinity of Pilliga? Please report
fully"

9/5/1910 -- Police Station Pilliga -- Subject : - Re proposal to
establish a school at the Aborigines Reserve Pilliga "Senior Constable
Leonard reports: - There are 7 children of school ages on Pilliga
Reserve & 4 at Cuttabri. The Aborigines state there are 12 on Wingadee
Station, who formerly belonged to Redbank Camp, which has now been
abolished. This makes a total of 23 children of school ages, names,
ages, sex and caste herewith. Twenty-one of the Aborigines now at
Wingadee had been drawing rations at Pilliga from the 7^th^ April to the
9^th^ October 1909. Some of these were natives of Pilliga, 10 of them
being children of school ages. Those at Cuttabri do not wish to come to
Pilliga, but it is possible the ones at Wingadee will consent to come.
Unless they come willingly it would not be much use to persuade them, as
there would be no guarantee of them staying for any length of time. The
cost of providing a suitable school room, with rough forms, desks etc,
would be less than £40. At present there is no accommodation for a lady
teacher in the locality, besides a lady would be totally unfit for the
position. The Aborigines require to be firmly dealt with, & a man of
some age & experience would be more suitable for the position. Only
those who have practical knowledge can understand the difficulty
experienced in the management of Aborigines. Most of the women have at
some time or other been at service, & already have a knowledge of
cooking, & can mend their own garments." Sub-Inspector Miller Walgett.
If a teacher has time to devote to other than educational purposes, it
would be much better employed in giving the Aborigines instructions in
Agriculture, Gardening etc, as by this means they could earn sufficient
to purchase mucho f which is now supplied them by the AP Board, thereby
becoming less of a charge on the State, & no doubt under proper
management they would in time make the Reserve self supporting, they
could be better fed, housed and clothed & their condition bettered in
every way."

6/8/1910 -- Notes from Mr. Erskine concerning establishment of school

...... I cant say for certain as to if the Aborigines will stay on the
present reserve. They have gone from Redbank so I believe, and taken up
their abode 2 miles North from the Station

By joining the two camps I believe it quite possible to make up a good
school. May I say that for the Teachers sake Pilliga would be the best,
as the town is not far off (5 miles) and there are very nice people in
Pilliga.

\(1\) Wingadee camp is 32 miles from Coonamble. (2) Come-by-chance is
the nearest State School. (3) There is no building for school purposes.
(4) Fencing, Shearing and Station work. (5) I believe they have moved
from Redbank and gone two miles north of the Station. (6) Yes they do,
they belong to the same tribe. But they are always going to Pilliga and
back. (7) Redbank is 7 miles from Wingadee Station. It is doubtful that
they will stay at Wingadee should rather personally see them all at
Pilliga. The Aborigines are always changing their camps I believe if the
Government built them houses (as they have done at Gular, Coonabarabran,
Talbragar etc) they would settle down. They are living in a
most-deplorable state in tents, bark huts or humpies. I must own that I
have not been able to see much of them as I have been pressed with other
work and it is some time since I have been up there. My information is
from Aboriginal men who have been up there since I have."

8/8/1910 -- Memo from Dept. Public Instruction Dubbo District to Chief
Inspector re Pilliga and Wingadee Aboriginal Camps: Educational
Requirements -- "Chief Inspector. Cuttabri and Pilliga -- two of the
camps mentioned in the accompanying papers -- are both I understand in
the Narrabri District: Wingadee is on the borders of the Narrabri and
Dubbo districts; but as we have no school at present within 30 miles of
it, I have never actually been in the neighborhood. A subsidized school
was in operation for some time at Wingadee, but closed last years on
account of the resignation of the teacher. An attempt was made to revive
the school during the present year, but I am informed that the
prospective teacher died. I am informed that the aboriginals now at
Wingadee belonged originally to Pilliga, and come at times to get
employment on Wingadee Station at lamb-marking, fencing and similar
work. They are said to work with fair constancy for a short time, and
then apparently for no good reason beyond the dislike to a settled life,
move away either to Pilliga or Cuttabri where they stay until some
quarrel makes them leave again. Under these circumstances it seems
useless to establish an Aboriginal station at Wingadee. If they could be
induced to remove willingly to Pilliga or Cuttabri, as suggested by the
Missionary, a strong and suitable school could be established, similar
to the one in existence at Gulargambone. The Missionary is right I think
in suggesting a woman teacher. If a specially trained woman teacher,
well versed in kindergarten methods, and like Miss Timbery of
Gulargambone anxious and willing to devote her life to the care of
aboriginal children, could be induced to take up this work she could
accomplish a great deal of good. As the Inspector at Narrabri will
probably be more conversant with the case than I, I suggest that his
advise be asked in regard to the question."

19/8/1910 -- Narrabri District Public School re Aboriginal Camp "Sir, I
have the honor to inform you that I have made inquiries as to the
opinions of the aborigines here regarding a combination of the Wingadee
and Pilliga Camps and the establishment of a School for the children at
Pilliga Camp. I saw Senior Constable Leonard about the matter and he
informed me that the Wingadee blacks had been approached with regard to
being shifted to Pilliga camp; but they were not agreeable to the
proposal. Mr. Leonard gave it as his opinion, however, that the blacks
would come down and live at Pilliga camp if a school were established
here. The blacks at both camps appear to be very friendly with each
other as they are always on the move from one camp to another. During
the past Mr. Leonard informed me a number of blacks from Pilliga camp
including 8 or 9 children of school age went over to Wingadee Camp (45
miles distant). This afternoon I went out to the Pilliga Camp, which is
4 miles from the Post Office here, and saw a number of the blacks. They
thought the idea of having a school for their children a splendid one,
and appeared to be very pleased at the prospect of having one. Ask as to
how many children were at Wingadee Camp, they told me that there were
about eleven who could attend school. They said also that they were sure
the Wingadee blacks would come and live at Pilliga if a School were put
up. Shortly after receiving your memorandum yesterday. I saw one of the
Wingadee blacks in Pilliga and he too assured me that the people from
his camp would come here to live. Regarding the Cuttabri blacks, they
seem to hold aloof from other camps and I do no think they would
willingly come to live at Pilliga. I was informed that there are only
two children at Cuttabri camp. Personally, I think that the Wingadee
blacks would come to live at Pilliga, if a school were established and I
think, also that the establishment of a school would be a blessing for
the children. I found that there are at present ten (10) children at
Pilliga camp who would be able to attend school. There are no blacks at
Come-by-chance except the black tracker." Signed Percy Gilbert (Teacher)

24/1/1912 -- Police Station Pilliga Subject Appointment of a Teacher for
the Aborigines School at Pilliga "Senior Constable Leonard reports: -
Miss Kinnear can obtain board and residence at a respectable Settlers
place about one half mile from the Reserve. In previous papers some
discussion arose as to whether a male or female teacher would be the
more suitable. Mr. Erskine, one of the Brotherhood of the Good Shepherd,
and the Education Department both favoured the appointment of a lady
teacher, but the Senior Constable has been opposed to it throughout. The
Senior Constable opposition is not altogether viewed from a moral
standpoint, for in that respect a lady Teacher, would perhaps be
preferable to a male teacher, except in the case of a man of high moral
character such as Mr. Shepherd. The Senior Constable 's strongest
objection is as regards the safety of a lady teacher on such reserves.
It is well known that Aborigines are not to be trusted, and that there
is always the chance of liquor finding its way to these Reserves. The
best conducted Aboriginal either male or female when in drink becomes
familiar, or is threatening or abusive, and require to be firmly dealt
with, and it is questionable if the occasion arose, whether a lady would
be equal to this task, or whether in the event of failure, it may end in
serious trouble, perhaps outrage." Signed Senior Constable Leonard.

Application from Teacher Kinnear for transfer to APB 22/9/1914 -- "Sir,
I beg to make application for a transfer to another Aboriginal school. I
would like you to understand that my desire does not in any way infer
that I have had any difference with the blacks, as my long experience
with the Aborigines could not do otherwise than enable me to work
amongst them, for any length of time without having grievance with them.
One reason I have for wishing to leave here, is that I may be able to
secure a school where I would have a dormitory and therefore give the
girls a better training, that is, training that would be a lasting
benefit to them in after life. The other is, I would like to be closer
to Grafton or Cootamundra."

7/5/1915 -- Memo from Teacher Weston re resignation by reason of severe
operation and weakness consequent of long illness both before and after
operation and no means of walking or driving 4 miles daily to the
Schools. "I have the honor to state that I cannot get to this school, in
my present state of health -- the distance is too great for me to walk
and there is no transport available after a few weeks more -- as you
will note from my letter. Also, I see the papers, that a good many of
your little white Pro-Schools have been closed for want of teachers, and
I beg of you to transfer me to one of them, near a place that I can get
a Dr. if my wound breaks open again. I am very weak and sick and cant
get strong here at all. (?)... request you to consider my circumstances
and help me out, for I am almost distracted at the thought of breaking
down again in this far away place -- out of reach of any assistance or
Hospital -- Narrabri is the nearest and the coaching again -- if
anything happened to my green wound -- would finish me and I must earn
my living and I can teacher, for I was trained for it, and you will
never regret pitying me and helping me, till I get well. The Rev. John
Ferguson will recommend me to your kindness at any time -- he knows me
very well and you know when I cant get a sulky to get to the school, I
can stay on here paying Board and earning nothing so again I must
earnestly beseech you to transfer me to a suitable little school as soon
as possible, I shall pray for a good answer from you. The first rain
this year came last Thursday and I got cold and could not get near the
school for 2 days with the rain -- but the drought is broken, I think,
the roads are in a dreadful state, and it is an old open sulky, I had to
pay a man to fix the seat up securely before I could use it safely --
the road is all pots and waterholes at present." Signed L. C. Weston

6/11/1914 -- Memo from Teacher Kinnear -- concerning explanation for
withdrawing acceptance of transfer to Stony Gully -- "Sir, I have the
honor to write you an explanation for withdrawing my acceptance of the
transfer offered me in answer to my request for removal. I will enclose
the letter received from the teacher in charge Stony Gully. The words
hardship and inconvenience caused me to wire to you and ask you to
withdraw my acceptance of transfer -- when first I arrived at this
school, there was no W.C -- it was about eighteen months after my
arrival here that very necessary building was erected. In the meantime
my health was in danger of serious harm. I thought perhaps the absence
of the above might have been the cause of the inconvenience mentioned in
the letter written to me by the teacher at Stony Gully. I would not call
hatching and living in mission hardship that is what I very much wish to
do, as I could then wield a greater influence over mission people and
guard the children more from the evil surrounding them among their
people. I sincerely regret not, knowing a little about the place, as
there may be a residence there and I could have taken at least one girl
entirely under my own charge and kept her from the degraded influence of
the older folk in the mission. If my wire was too late and you have
already appointed a teacher to the school at present under my charge,
rather than your command, and risk the hardship and inconvenience and
proceed to Stony Gully at my own expense anytime you may wish to send me
there. If you have not moved in the matter, I am willing to stay here
for a longer period and in the meantime I will be obliged if you will
give me some information re abor schools eligible for lady teachers.
This school is splendidly stocked with material at present and during my
2 1/2 years stay here, I have worked really hard for the benefit of my
pupils -- washing, starching, ironing, scrubbing, general cleaning,
cooking, gardening, needlework, macramé and wire work, besides the
curriculum of an ordinary primary school are taught here. -- Perhaps you
will understand some of the difficulty I have teaching manual work, when
I tell you that there is not an extra room, or small cooking room, or
verandah in which to give these lessons -- the cooking is taught at my
own expense. There is one word of praise I must give. This school
building has plenty of ventilation. Hoping I have not caused undue
trouble in your office."

17/3/1938 -- Fly proofing of school building "It appears to me that the
question of providing fly-proof doors and windows at the above school is
one of some urgency. During my inspection I was assailed by a cloud of
flied most of which, no doubt, had already made contact with the many
sore eyes among the children. The manager of the Station is gibing all
possible attention to the eye-trouble but his best efforts must be
largely negatived while the school room remains in its present
unprotected state. The teacher, too, is daily exposed to the danger of
infection in which case there would be an interruption to service. I
consider the matter to be one meriting the most favourable consideration
and recommend that the Station Manager be instructed to submit the
necessary plans and estimates." Letter sent to APB
