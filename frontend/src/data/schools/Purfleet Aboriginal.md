2/11/1904 -- Letter from Teacher to Dept. Public Instruction re teaching
of Sewing at Purfleet Aboriginal School "Sir, With reference to your
letter of 28^th^ ultimo re Miss Oldrey teaching sewing in this school I
beg to inform you that Miss Oldrey has received no appointment to that
position. Miss Oldrey is the resident missionary on the Mission Station
here and gives instruction in sewing after school hours in connection
with her other mission work. I have been given to understand that
permission was given by Mr. Inspector Reay that the school might be used
for any mission purpose after school hours. Miss Oldrey receives no
remuneration with the sewing." Signed W. H. Bruce Teacher. Note from
Chief Inspector "Miss Oldrey is going good work among these aborigines.
I recommend that the teacher be informed the Minister approves of the
Missionary teaching sewing after school hours without pay, and that the
building may be used for mission purposes provided the ordinary school
routine is not interfered with." R. Henderson Inspector.

Letter from Teacher 21/2/1905 Re application for permission to employ a
teacher of sewing. "Sir, I have the honour to apply for permission to
employ Miss Oldrey as teacher of sewing in this school. There are 12
girls on the roll and the parents are desirous of having their children
taught this subject."

Memo from Chief Inspector 25 February 1905 -- "These blacks are under
the care of Miss M. Oldrey, resident missionary. This lady is a
competent needlewoman and about 25 years of age. Excellent work is being
done on the settlement, and the children are developing into creditable
citizens. The school is 9^th^ class and would be a blessing to the
female pupils if sewing were regularly and systematically taught as in
our ordinary schools. NO subject can possibly be of benefit to these
girls. I therefore recommend that the teacher be authorized to employ
Miss Oldrey to teach sewing for two afternoons per week at the usual
rate of £12 per year, such ought to be charged by Mr. Bruce on his
salary abstracts." Signed R. Henderson -- Note from Under Secretary "It
is not the practice to authorize the employment of teachers of sewing in
schools ranking below the eighthclass but as these are aborigines, I
think an exception might be made, and the employment of Miss Oldrey
authorized as suggested. I recommend accordingly."

APB to Dept. Public Instruction 22/3/1907 -- "Sir, I have the honor, by
direction, to acknowledge with thanks the receipt of a repot by Mr.
Inspector William Kennedy on his recent inspection of the School for
Aborigines at Purfleet, Taree, and to inquire whether the Teacher of the
School could instruct the Aboriginal youths in the use of tools, if
supplied by the Board." Note from Chief Inspector "The Teacher Mr. Bruce
is competent to instruct the Aboriginal children attending the Purfleet
School in the use of tools and if the A.P Board would supply the
necessary tools and wood for the work the instruction would be of great
benefit to the youths and help to occupy their spare time."

Memo from Teacher E. P Williams 26/7/1932 re Sewing allowance for
Teachers wife "Sir, In complying with the instruction laid down in the
"Education Gazette" dd June 1^st^ pg 90. I desire to apply for
permission for my wife to teach sewing in the above school. In support
of my application I give further details of the pupils upon the school
roll, and would point out that the boys as well as the girls over 7
years of age would be taught sewing in accordance with the Department of
Educations "Course of Instruction for Aborigines' Schools". No of Girls
on the Roll: - 9 Over 7 years 7. No of Boys on the Roll: - 21 Over 7
years 20." Note from Chief Inspector "Mr. Williams has recently been
appointed to the charge of the Purfleet Aborigines' School, and station,
vice Miss Fraser, transferred to Burnt Bridge. Sewing was previously
taught. As this subject is prescribed for both sexes in Aborigines'
Schools, I recommend that this application be sanctioned, as a special
case, as I understand that Mr. Williams is unclassified yet; provided
that Mrs. Williams is not already under obligation to the A.P Board to
give this instruction."

27/10/1932 -- Application for Teachers wife for sewing allowance. In
this letter he states "....It will be noted that had the teacher in
charge waited until the approval was received the school would have been
without sewing instruction for more than 2 months and the making up of
winter garments for the children of the station would not have been

20/2/1936 -- Memo from Inspector F. Ravenscroft to the Chief Inspector
re PURFLEET (ABOR.) P.S: Position of Teacher -- "Mr. Edward P. Williams
combines his duties as teacher of the school those of Manager of the
Purfleet Aborigines Station. The school contains 35 children. It is
anticipated that the enrolment will reach 50 in the near future. On the
station, which is growing almost daily, the whole population numbers
250. Mr. Williams is assisted, though not in school, expecting with the
needlework, by his wife, who is Matron of the station. With no outside
help they superintend and manage the station, which includes 68 men
registered for emergency relief work (these are under the direct care of
Mr. Williams), they purchase and distribute the rations and clothing for
the whole station (even keeping the accounts is a heavy task), and
dispense with medicine as required -- there were 12 cases this morning.
Mr.Williams superintends the clearing and fencing of new areas, the
erection of cottages, and the sinking of wells. He is responsible for
the general supervision of the aboriginal settlements at Forster and
Dingo Creek. There are other minor duties. The work is in my opinion far
too heavy, and either a teacher should be appointed to take charge of
the school. The remuneration received by Mr. and Mrs. Willliams appears
to be so disproportionate to the value of the work performed that I
wish, on the behalf, to draw the attention of the Aborigines Protection
Board to it. As a teacher Mr. Williams is in receipt of a fortnightly
payment of £7.15.5., exclusive of a deduction of 16/- for annuities. As
Manager and Matron respectively he and his wife receive 13/2 and 12/8
per fortnight, a residence rent-free, and rations to the total value of
16/7 weekly. If the house is worth 12/6 weekly, then these two receive
for their combined services the sum of £6.7.9 per week. Though Mr.
Williams has refrained from protesting against the low salary paid him,
and the extent of the services required in return, I know that he feels
the position keenly. Very naturally, also, he has no desire to lose his
remuneration as Teacher of the school. I feel, however, as one
conversant with the circumstances, that I should state the facts for the
consideration of the authorities, and ask for some alleviation. The
value of Mr. Williams' services as Manager are known, I understand, to
members of the A. P. Board who have visited Purfleet and are therefore
cognisant of the splendid work being done there." - Memo sent to APB --
with note "In connection with the abovementioned subject, I attach a
copy of communication from Mr. Inspector f. Ravenscroft."

1/3/1938 -- Memo to teacher of the Aboriginal School at Purfleet, Taree
re School desks -- provision of back-rests and book-shelves. "It is not
clear from your recent report in the above connection whether you desire
back-rests and book-shelves to be provided for the existing desks.
Please advise me definitely on this point." Chief Inspector -- Note from
Teacher "I beg to report that I do not desire to have back rests and
shelves provided for existing desks. As previously reported, it is
considered that it could not satisfactorily be done and would simply be
a waste of public money. I do desire though that my original application
for dual desks be given serious consideration with view to supply when
funds are available. Meanwhile seating conditions in school are totally
inadequate and unsatisfactory."

25/10/1937 - Memo from Dept. Education re supply of dual desks. "The
teacher of this school has applied through the Aborigines Protection
Board for the supply of dual desks to replace the long desks and forms
at present in use. The present desks have been in use for a considerable
length of time, and are not provided with back rests and bookshelves.
Mr. Inspector Ravenscroft inspected the school and states that he
considers the desks are in a very fair condition and that back rest and
bookshelves might be readily fixed. He also states that whilst he
desires the installation of dual desks in all schools, under the present
policy of the Department he cannot recommend that they be supplied to
this School. IT IS RECOMMENDED that the application for dual desks be
declined, and that back rests and bookshelves be fixed to the existing
desks upon receipt from the teacher of the necessary particulars
regarding the length of desks etc."

Memo from Local Inspector to Teacher Mr. Williams requesting back rests
and bookshelves to be added seeing that the Department are unable to
supply the dual desks to the school. Reply from Mr. Williams to this
request ".... With regard to the desks I sincerely hope the Department
will sanction the issue of dual desks, as I am sure that the building
warrants an issue, and the moral and physical effect on the children
will be very great. Even the addition of shelves and back rests to the
present desks will only be makeshift. You know how old the desks are,
how scored are the surfaces and how broken the legs. Enrolment 33 (with
20 duals more could be accommodated.)

Memo from Teacher E.P Williams to APB 18/8/1937 re Provision of New
Desks at Aboriginal School Purfleet. "Sir, During the recent visits of
the Director of Education to this Station, and the annual inspection, it
was suggested that new desks to match the new school building may be
supplied by a Department concerned. What is required is about 20 dual
desks of varying heights, of modern pattern, ie. With back and foot
rests, and shelves underneath for pupils' books. The present desks are
of the first known design and have been frequently repaired, but are
still very unsatisfactory, and tend to create deformity in the children.
They were supplied to this school in 1903 having been originally issued
to another school in 1876. Would the Board kindly consider the proposal
as I feel sure that it is in the interests of the health and hygiene of
the children that better accommodation be supplied." Forwarded to the
Under Secretary Dept. Education for information and favour of
consideration.

9/12/1936 -- Memo from Teacher Mr. E. P Williams to Dept. Education re
Outbreak of Chicken Pox and Sandy Blight. "Sir, I have been instructed
by the District Inspector of Schools, TAREE, (Mr. F. Ravenscroft) to
report to you the outbreak of Chicken Pox and Sandy Blight among the
adults and children on this Station. School enrolment is at present 34
of which 15 have been diagnosed as suffering from Chicken Pox, and have
been excluded from attending school, of the remainder 6 are suffering
from Sandy Blight. It will be readily understood that the majority of
the aboriginal children on the station are relatives of one another and
the danger of all suffering from it is very imminent, unless the school
is closed for the remainder of the term, thus allowing the manager and
matron to adequately conduct or supervise any medical treatment. I
therefore ask that I may be instructed by wire whether school may be
immediately closed." Signed E. P Williams Teacher/Manager

Letter in reply to teacher 11/12/1936 -- Referring to your memo,
addressed to the Principal Medical Officer, reporting an outbreak of
chicken pox and "sandy blight" amongst the children attending your
school, I wish to state that, in view of the special circumstances to
which you draw attention, approval has been given for the closure of the
school until the beginning of the Christmas vacation."
