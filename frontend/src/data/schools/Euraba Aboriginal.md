28/3/1912 -- Letter from Mr. Crewdson to the Chairman and Members of the
Board for the Protection of Aborigines -- "Gentlemen, I respectfully beg
to apply for the post of Teacher and Manager at your Euraba Station
(near Boomi). Having nine years experience as Asst. Master and Master in
various institutions, taking charge of all new work and repairs in all
building work, also being responsible for cultivation of the land, it is
with every confidence of giving your Board entire satisfaction, that I
ask for your favorable consideration. My wife is a Certified Teacher,
and has passed (with special mention) in the subjects of Domestic
Economy and Needlework and Music. I am a life abstainer, thirty-one (31)
years of age, and favored with good health. Thanking you for your
consideration."

29/3/1912 -- APB to Dept. Public Instruction r Requesting establishment

1917 -- Memo from Dept. Re - Euraba Aboriginal (Provisional) School : -
"Application by Mr. H. E. Hockey, Teacher, for sewing allowance. Mr.
Hockey is a temporary teacher of aboriginal schools, not eligible for
any other position under the Department. He is, of course, unclassified,
and unless his case is treated specially, cannot be paid any allowance
on account of his wife's services as teacher of needlework. He applies
at the instigation of the Aborigines' Protection Board, for the usual
allowance for sewing, and intimates that, unless the application is
recognized, Mrs. Hickey will discontinue teaching altogether. He points
out that, besides giving lessons in needlework, she teaches plain
cooking and laundry work. In view of the special nature of Mrs. Hockey's
services, should a recommendation be made for payment of the Regulation
allowance of £30 per annum, or an allowance of some sort?" Note from
Dept. in reply -- "Refer matter to AP Board -- with a view to their
consideration of paying Mrs. Hickey an allowance for her services in
teaching Domestic Arts at this School. Our regulation make no provision
whereby a claim in this connection could be met.

4/5/1917 -- APB re Application for sewing allowance -- Note "As Mrs.
Hockey is performing the duties of Sewing Mistress, application should
be made to the Department of Education for the usual allowance." Signed
Acting Secretary AP Board. Letter from Teacher to APB Board -- "Mr.
Donaldson the A.P Board's Inspector recently spent two days of
Inspection at Euraba, with the result that I am instructed to apply to
the Department of Education for the usual allowance for teaching sewing
by the Teacher's wife. Under the New Syllabus there are 23 children
entitled to the advantages of sewing. Hence it can scarcely be expected
that the Teacher's wife should carry out this duty without some
renumeration." Note from Inspector Clemens, Inverell -- "Mrs. Hockey's
best qualification is her past 18 years of service, teaching sewing to
the older girls, without renumeration from the Education Department. Now
under the New Syllabus, she is expected to teach all girls and boys of 7
years of age, together with lessons in plain cooking, and laundry work,
hence she has decided to cease teaching altogether, unless an adequate
renumeration is granted. Present enrolment 28."

11/6/1917 -- APB to Dept. Education -- "Sir, With reference to an

Letter from Mr. R. M. Ausland to Secretary AP Board 22/7/1912 -- "Dear
Sir, I have the honor to make application for the position of Manager
and Teacher at the Aboriginal Station, Euraba, as per your advertisement
in the "Daily Telegraph". My qualifications may be concisely stated as
follows: I am 43 years of age married of strictly temperate habits, and
for the last twenty years have held various important appointments at
home and abroad, many of the latter having brought me in close contact
with the native races of the several foreign countries in which it has
been my lot to serve and reside; by which means I was afforded ample
opportunity of studying the best methods of securing their aid and
esteem. Particularly this has been the case in the West Indies, Central
and South America, West and Central Africa, Burma and the Malay
Peninsula, where I had the management and control of large numbers of
natives, on plantation, building and carpentry work. I have a good
knowledge of general agriculture, building construction, carpentry etc.
together with the necessary qualifications for teaching. I am prepared
to take up duties of the position at once in the event of being
appointed. Should my application meet with the approval of the Board, it
will be my aim at all times to render satisfactory service. Copy
references are enclosed here, with, while mention may be made of the
following personal references. Rev. W. McAuley, Waverley, Mr. Lucas of
Burns Philp & Co, and Mr. Steele, Chief Accountant, P.W.D. Signed R.
McAuslan

29/5/1913 -- "Sir, I regret to report that, owing to the recent heavy
rains and the overflowing creeks in the vicinity the whole of the
Aborigines' Reserve at Euraba is inundated with water, and has been
since, Monday the 26^th^ inst: - Hopes were entertained that the
flooding would be only temporary but since the 27^th^ inst: the rise has
steadily increased that, yesterday it became necessary for the whole of
the aborigines to leave the Reserve, and they are now camped 3 1/2 miles
nearer to Boomi. The depth of water at the School is 15 inches, and just
reaches to underneath the flooring."

23/9/1913 -- Closing of School on account of Flood -- Provisional School
Euraba -- "Sir, I have the honor to reply to your enquiry of the 18^th^
inst. As follows: - The School records show that the School was closed
on account of flood for one day only, viz: - 28^th^ May, 1913. I have
the honor to add, however, that I was living here (in Boomi) at that
time, and I know that the entire population of Euraba was camped some
three or four miles from the Reserve for a whole fortnight, during which
time the School was not in progress. Upon receipt of your memo, I made
the strictest enquiry amoung the Natives, and as far as I can ascertain,
they left Euraba on the 30^th^ May, 1913, and returned on the 16^th^.
June, following. Since then, they tell me that the School has been
opened on two occasions -- about the 19^th^ & 20^th^ June, - when the
teacher arrived there about 10.30am and had the children in School for
about two hours on each of the abovementioned days.

13/11/1913 -- Memo from Dept. Public Instruction re Non-attendance of
Scholars. "Sir, I have the honor to report that the whole population of
Euraba, with the exception of three sick people, went into Boomi on
Tuesday evening to attend the Races on Wednesday and Thursday. They
formed a camp near the town and went onto the Course each day. I opened
the School as usual on Wednesday morning, but no scholars appeared, the
parents having taken their children with them. As I hold the dual office
of teacher and Manger, I visited the sick people here and then went to
Boom at 2.00 to see to the Aborigines there. After sports were over I
conducted them all to their camp and then returned to Euraba on account
of the sick ones. On Thursday morning I opened the school with the same
result as before, and after attending to the sick etc, I went to Boomi
at 1.30 to look after the people as on the previous day -- finally
seeing them to their camp and urging them to be on the road early in the
morning, to get the children back in time for school. Today, however,
they did not arrive here until after 4 o'clock, the reason being that
their horses had strayed and could not be found. A number of them came
on a wagon on its way to Sarah; Major Crane M.P, kindly brought some of
the children in his car, and the remainder walked home. Confirmation of
this report may be had be referring to D. E. Turnbull Esqr. Grazier,
Euraba, or to Constable. W. Goodwin, Officer in Charge of Police, Boomi.
" Signed J. Howard Teacher.

15/6/1915 -- Memo to Dept. Education re Teacher Mr. Howard reporting
return to duty after leave, and finding the residents absent from the
Station -- "I have the honor to report my return to Euraba today, 7
June, when I found that the male residents of this Station have removed
their families to the neighbourhood of their present employment about 12
miles away. The whole of the men from here are working on a ring-barking
contract and expect to finish this week, when they will return. I have
sent word to them to send their wives and children back immediately, but
do not expect them until the work is finished, viz: - this weekend. Will
you kindly advise me re above." Signed Howard.

28/6/1915 -- "Non-attendance of pupils -- from the 18^th^ June to 25^th^
June inclusive there was no attendance of pupils at this school on
account of the men all being engaged on contract work 12 miles away and
having taken their wives and families. The work ended a week ago and
they are now kangaroo hunting and do not intend on returning until after
holidays. I was at my post from 7^th^ June to 25^th^ June.

20/7/1921 -- Memo to Dept. Education from Teacher. Mr. H. E. Hockey re
Re opening of Euraba School after being closed by flood waters "I was
rather premature when I wrote stating that the Euraba School would
re-open after the flood, on the 4^th^. July. The day following the
postage of my letter, very heavy rain fell which brought the flood water
higher than ever. Mrs. Hockey and myself were taken away to a place of
safety, where we stayed nearly a fortnight. When we arrived home the
water was still a foot deep around the house, and near the school house
two pelicans were gracefully swimming about. As you are probably aware
our residence, and the school house is built in a swamp, the water
cannot run away, it must soak away, this accounts for the long wait
before the residents could return, however about 20 children and their
parents returned to-day, hence I intend to commence school to-morrow
morning, although ground is still in a boggy condition."

10/8/1921 -- Memo to Dept. Education from Teacher. Mr. H. E. Hockey re
School closed by Flood Water My letters dated 11^th^ & 24^th^ June & 20
July. "I beg to report that all the residents of Euraba were compelled
to leave with their children on the 8^th^? June some of them returned on
the 20^th^ July, and school was re-opened the following day with 18
children. However a fourth flood again compelled the residents to leave
on the 25^th^, and were not able to commence to return until the 8^th^
August. School was again resumed on the 9^th^, with 18 scholars, others
will be returning daily, providing the weather keeps fine, they still
have to plough through over a foot of water and mud to reach here. The
Teacher's residence is still surrounded by water, and is still very damp
underneath, emitting an offensive odour as the water soaks away."
