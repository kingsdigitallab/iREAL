13/8/1891 -- memorandum to the Under Secretary -- Report on the working
of Brungle Aborigines Station. "I examined the school at Brungle on the
5^th^ instant on its working and was furnished on 14^th^ instant. After
examining the school I made careful inquiry into the working of the
settlement, especially as to what changes or improvements have been made
since the Ministers visit in February last. I attach a list of
distinguishing half castes from full bloods, and giving names and ages
of the persons who now regard this station as their homes. Summary of
this list: 43 full bloods, 45 half castes 1 European woman to a
half-caste. On the day of my visit I found sixty men women and children
on the station. The rest were away at work or traveling around the
country. Of these sixty-six, fifty are supplied with rations. Sixteen
children attend the school with more or less regularity. Briefly put,
the school has not at all come up to my expectations. You will
recollect, that in February last the huts were all built together in a
kind of street. Since then the Superintendent has roughly surveyed the
land (77 acres) into plots varying from 1 1/2 to 5 acres and allotted a
plot to each family. Nearly all have removed and have their huts on
their own land. This is the most striking change made since the
Ministers visit. This change is attended with beneficial sanitary
results, as well as lessening the number of petty squabbles arising from
crowding a number of idle people together.

A tolerably good outside fence has been erected. Up to the present, very
little has been done in the way of preparing the sold for toilage. I was
greatly disappointed at this, but Mr. Usher explains that work was
stopped on 26^th^ march pending the consideration of removing the
station. One plot of about 4 acres has been ploughed and the
Superintendent talks about applying for another. I pointed out to him
that this would not be necessary if he kept the one he has constantly
going. Mr. Usher does not seem to understand farming very well and does
not put a sufficient high value on spade and husbandry. If I had my way
I would hardness these hulking, loafing fellows to spades and make them
cultivate their own plots: this would be a lot more effective than
scratching with a plough.

I notice that the paddock is overrun with a lot of weedy horses, and
five or six cattle. I advised that this should be put to stop at once,
as neither crop nor grass can be expected whilst such a practice
obtains.

There are about 150 acres of Crown Land adjoining the station which
might be added to it. I spoke t o the District Surveyor about this and
he sees no objection to granting this land for the use of the Blacks.
With this addition and white good vigorous management I see some
prospect of making the settlement almost self-supporting. Of course this
is an object, but not the main one.

I made careful inquiry about the morality of the Camp. I learn from the
Superintendent, the Police and local men that this is very satisfactory.
There is now no such this as white rowdies visiting and carousing at the
Camp. On this point Mr. Usher has exhibited Commendable vigilance.

The greatest evil of the place is that a number of hulking fellows loaf
about the camp and live on the rations supplied to the woman and
children. Mr. Usher says that has has neither power to make them work or
expel them. Steps should be taken to remedy this. The common desire for
the ownership of property which is such a motive power with the white
races is entirely inoperative with the Aborigines. It is therefore clear
that the external authority must supply the motive to exertion with
these people. Simple and easily understood Regulations ought to be
framed for governing this Camp. I do no think that vague and
unidentified authority ought to be left in the hands of any man in
dealing with these people. Capricious government is the worst mode of
treating blacks. Of course until lately we were proceeding with this
settlement in a tentative manner; the time has arrived when it ought to
be treated as a permanent settlement. An institution of the kind is
required in this part of the Country and I am in a position to say that
no place more suitable than Brungle can be selected.

I venture to suggest as follows:

That an experienced and capable officer be instructed to visit and
report upon the work done at the Aborigines station at Lake Eyres,
Gippland in Victoria. I am informed that the efforts put firth there are
attended with a large amount of success.

That a committee be appointed to enquire into the management at Brungle.
Also to frame a few simple Regulations for the management of the place.

I hope I shall not be considered officious when I suggest the name of
the William McIntyre in connection with this work.: he is a man of great
experience and tact. He is also well known for his advocacy of advanced
social reforms. I request that I am so unwell that I have to offer this
Report in a very slovenly form. Signed GO Byrne District Inspector

18/11/1903 -- Chief Inspector -- Googeedee (Brungle Provisional) --
suggested amalgamation of school with Brungle Public. The enrolment at
Googeedee last quarter was 25, and average attendance 17.5. The building
is the property of the Board for the Protection of Aborigines. The
children are Aboriginals, and are taught only reading, writing,
dictation and arithmetic. A Superintendent appointed by the Board looks

from Googeedee. It would, I have not doubt, give dis-satisfaction to the
parents. And would be detrimental to the interests of the school. I
recommend that the Aborigines Protection Society be informed that Mr.
Dowlings suggestion cannot be adopted. Signed Shelly, Inspector.

21/10/1903 -- Extract from the report of Mr. Edward Dowling, J.P Member
of the Board for the Protection of Aborigines, re visit to Brungle
Aboriginal Station. ... There are, however, two schools built and two
teachers employed within a short distance of each other, one for the
whites and the other for the coloured children. This involves not only
the erection of two buildings and providing duplicate sets of school
requisites, but also entails a salary of £120 per annum being paid to a
male teacher and £80 to a female teacher. Owing to the decreasing
numbers attending both these schools, I think it would be well on the
score of economy that they should be amalgamated and the white children
and Aboriginal children taught together as is done in other localities.

Letter from Mr. Usher to Dept Ed. 26/8/1891 -- Re Explanatory of his
absence from school and defects as his inspection. "In reply to your
letter regarding my absence from the school on the occasion of the
District Inspectors visit I beg respectfully to say that I had gone on a
matter of great importance to myself and should have reported by absence
to Mr O'Byrne, requesting him to sanction it as it was impossible to
apply for leave in time. I have never before been away during school
hours except when I have been obliged to see to the removal of sick
persons to the hospital or other urgent business on the camp. Owing to
the distance I cannot get in to town if I leave after school time. With
regard to the hours of teaching; I kept the usual time at first but
found that it was utter waste of time to keep the school on during the
afternoon, I therefore began at 9 am instead of 9.30 and dismissed the
school at 1pm. I would respectfully point out that these children are
not like those of white people that they have been subjected to no
restraint and are or rather were when I took charge almost wild. There
was only one pupil in the school who could write at all: none of the
rest even knew the alphabet or could write their letters. The first
division have now nearly reached the end of the first reading and can
write fairly. It is exceedingly difficult to teach them anything
concerning numbers but not withstanding some are now learning long
division. Sewing -- with regard to this, although Mrs. Usher has not
been here, instruction has been regularly given by my daughter who is
perfectly capable of taking a class of beginners. With regard to Mr
O'Byrnes remark about the Masters influence not being strongly felt, I
clearly do not understand what is meant. The same Time Table was in use
at the time of the Ministers visit in February last. In conclusion I
would respectfully submit that, giving due consideration to the
circumstances, this school should not fairly be judged by the same
standard as ordinary schools and that a year is not by any means
sufficient time to counter act the effect of years of the lack of
discipline and restraint, and also venture to point out that I am
endeavoring to serve two offices at the same time and that in
consequence a small amount of irregularity must occasionally arise. The
school hours have of course been rearranged as directed by the District
Inspector. I have tried and do try to do my utmost but I have
exceedingly poor material to work upon." Signed O. Garton Usher.

> *Experience*

Mr Ussher experience -- Previous to 1888 had been in the service of the
Department for seven and a half years. During 1888 was dismissed from
Duty for a portion of the year for not forwarding fees, returned etc and
was in turn compelled to resign. In 1889 was asking for reinstatement.

The District Inspector noted in 1889 that in view of this dismissal -- "
I am very sorry for his wife and children, but in the interests of the
service I cannot recommend his readmittance." Following this the Chief
Inspector noted that Mr Ussher had worked "like a real man" to try and
provide for his family and is sorry for his past neglect. He asked if
the Minister could not give Mr Ussher another trial. He writes that
"Both Mr Ussher and his wife are children of English Clergymen, and both
have been well brought up. The School at Brungle Blacks Camp has been
closed for more than a year and Mr Ussher would be glad to get this
place." Recommended employment.

> *Protests/resistance to school*

Brungle Sept 5 1888 -- Protest from white parents at Brungle for
Aboriginal children attending the local school. "On behalf of the
parents of children attending the Brungle Provisional School. I beg
respectfully to protest against the alteration proposed by the
Department, Viz in appointing the teacher of this school as the teacher
of the aboriginals at Brungle for two days a week. Our school having an
attendance of 22 children, we contend that we have a perfect right to
have it continued on at full time, and under any circumstances we
decidedly object to be coupled with the aboriginals in the matter in any

Brungle -- 5/88 Objecting on behalf of residents to have their children
taught with aboriginals. "On behalf of the parents of children attending
the Brungle Provisional School. I desire to bring under the notice of
your Board the following facts in connection with this school. As you
are doubtless aware it was conducted for some time past on the half time
system in conjunction with the Brungle school. Recently the residents at
their won expense removed the building to a more central site with the
result that the attendance was trebled, there being now 22 children on
the roll with an average attendance of 17 or 18, giving it the status of
a first class provisional school. After considerable agitation on our
past it was about 6 weeks since converted into a full time school.
Another arrangement has been made under which our teacher is to teach
the aboriginals at Brungle, two days a week. I need not comment on the
folly of the government attempting to teach these uncultivated
youngsters under a two days a week system, but we decidedly and wholly
object to being coupled with the blacks in the matter in anyway, not to
say anything about the injustice of robbing our children of the time and
we are quite determined that the teaches of the aboriginal shall not
teach for us. Since this arrangement has been made 8 children have been
withdrawn from the school, and it will probably lead to a break up of it
entirely if we do not get speedy redress. We earnestly hope therefore
that in this matter your Board will lend us their influence in
endeavoring to bring about a satisfactory settlement of what is to us a
very important matter."

Further to letter of 4^th^ instant -- 19/9/1888 -- ".... The parents
will not run the risk of having skin diseases imparted to their children
by contamination form the teachers association with them. "

21/2/1888 -- Dear Sir, I have the honor to bring before your notice that
19 children out of an attendance of 23, at the Provisional school
Brungle, in my electorate have been withdrawn from the School through
the parents taking offence at your Department sending the Teacher to the
Aboriginal school Brungle twice a week which arrangement is looked on as
most injudicious and will evidently not be tolerated my constituents,
who I consider have just cause for dissatisfaction shown. Therefore I
respectfully beg to request that you will have some more satisfactory
managements made so that the children will return to their school. In
enclose here with two letters from the Secretary of the Brungle Progress
Committee on the subject and forwarded this letter of the 4^th^ instant
in due course, which has not been replied to, that I am aware of."

> *Living conditions*

Case of Diphtheria at the Brungle Station (1913). Children sent to Tumut
hospital. Principal Medical Officer of APB was of the opinion that there
does not appear to have been any reason for closing the school. Saw no
point in closing the school as the houses on the station were situated
so close to each other. Note to School "In view of the outbreak of
Diphtheria at the Brungle Mission Station, I would recommend you to
close the school and keep it closed till further orders."

8/8/1913 -- Report stating that two other children had contracted
Diphtheria

APB -- 21/7/1913 -- Diphtheria -- Question of closing school. The
manager suggests that the School be closed for a week.

8/8/1913 -- Daisy Hubbard writing to the Department of Public
Instruction informing them that another 2 cases of Diphtheria have
broken out. She wrote -- "The Manger of the Station had the school
closed as it was impossible to keep the children from mixing with each
other and visiting each others homes, owing to the close proximity to
each other."
