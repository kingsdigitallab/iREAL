**Role of the Department and relationship to the APB**

Lands Department -- Commonwealth Surveyor General 25/11/1924 -- To
Department of Education -- "With reference to your letter of 6 November
relative to the erection at Summer Cloud Bay (Wreck Bay) of a building
to provide educational facilities for the aboriginal children. I desire
to inform you that the building which will be erected is intended not
only for use as a school, but also as a recreational hall.

It has been suggested by the Captain of the R.A.N. College, Jervis Bay,
that the hall should be placed in his care, with the Schoolmaster of the
Public School, Jervis Bay, and the Chaplain R.A.N. College as his
advisors. It would appear to be necessary under the circumstances that
the school should be under the care of a responsible officer, and
therefore the Captain's suggestion will be recommended for approval. The
Captain of the College points out the necessity of appointing a male
teacher, as the Native children are somewhat difficult to deal with. In
connection with the appointment of a teacher, it is necessary that a
male teacher be appointed, and he would have to live at Jervis Bay and
be provided with transport, probably a horse would be the most suitable.
The Chaplain of the College advises that there are some secondhand desks
in the Works and Railway Store at Jervis Bay, which he thinks might be
used in the Native School. It would be appreciated if you would give
this matter your early attention, and let me gave your views." Noted
reply ... Seen. No further action on 27 November last. The Commonwealth
Surveyor General was informed that the Department is unable to establish
a school. The education of the Colored children was referred to the A.P
Board.

Memo from Chief Inspector - 13/11/1924 -- 6 or 7 Aboriginal children at
Summer Cloud Bay who have no means of attending school. "The number may
possibly be augmented by some of the children of a similar type coming
from Huskisson. If this happen, the average number of pupils may be as
much as 12, but the nomadic habits of the Blacks are well known, and the
Huskisson people may shift camp at any time, so that any continuity in
the attendance of the children is by no means certain. The Commonwealth
Authorities purpose building a structure to cost £799 to provide a
school building. The Chaplain considers that this sum is too large and
thinks that, for £200, a suitable school could be erected. He suggested
that an Assistant (Male) could be appointed to teach the Aborigines half
of the time and that this teacher could spend the other half of this
time as Assistant at Jervis Bay. I understand that the whole of the cost
of the Teaching staff at Jervis Bay school is paid by the Commonwealth
so that this factor need not necessarily be considered by this
Department. The question for us seems to be whether the Department would
be justified, during the present shortage of Teachers, in taking one of
them to act in this capacity mentioned. I do not think so, as it is by
no means certain that an average of 10 could be maintained. Again, in my
opinion, it would be very unwise to appoint a single man to take charge
of the School composed of Aborigines even if he were a PICKED man, as he
would need to be. The only way to do anything worthwhile is to establish
a Station under the Aborigines Protection Board and to appoint a married
couple to take charge. Such a place is now in operation at Roseby Park,
near Crookhaven. It is not an unqualified success. I recommend that the
Commonwealth Surveyor General who send the original letter, be informed
that, as the number of children who are sure to attend is so small (7),
and in view of the present shortage of teachers, the Department regrets
that it is unable to appoint a Teacher as asked." Recommendation
Approved. Matter referred to APB for consideration.

9/10/1925 -- The APB have referred to Education Department the question
of making provision of some Aboriginal children at Summer Cloud Bay,
which is situated 3 miles from the Jervis Bay Public School. The Board
have raised the question of whether it would be possible to provide for
these children by way of a half-time school to be worked in conjunction
with some other school. Mr.Inspector Sheehy reports that there are only
four children available at Summer Cloud Bay, but that a building is
being provided by the Federal Government in which a school could be
conducted. At Huskisson, 16 miles away, local objection has been taken
to the presence of Aboriginal children who have, as a result, been
excluded from attendance, and the Inspector's suggestion is that the two
groups -- at Huskisson and Summer Cloud Bay -- should be placed in
charge of the half-time teacher. This would only be possible in the even
of accommodation being secured at Huskisson, and if the Federal
Government agree to accommodate the teacher at the Naval College during
his stay at Summer Cloud Bay. It is recommended that the APB be informed
that the only solution of the difficulty appears to be in the working of
a half-time school for the benefit of the Aboriginal children at
Huskisson and Summer Cloud Bay, and that inquiry is made as to whether
the Board is prepared to place a suitable building at the disposal of
the Department at Huskisson. They might also be asked to communicate
with the Federal Authorities as to the accommodation at Summer Cloud
Bay. ... Memo submitted ... note: Owing to the fact that there is no
station at which these children can be grouped, it is a matter of some
difficulty to provide for their education. If, however, the Aborigines
Protection Board can provide a building at Huskisson and induce the
Federal Authorities to accommodate the Teacher at the Naval College
during his stay at Summer Cloud Bay, the experiment of a half-time
school might be tried.

19/11/1926 -- Letter to APB from Dept. Education - ..asking if the Board

20/2/1928 -- Department of Education -- "For sometime past the question
of providing education for a group of colored children residing at
Summer Cloud Bay within the Federal Territory has been exercising the
attention of the Department of the Aborigines Protection Board. At one
time it was proposed that a half-time school should be established for
these children to work in conjunction with Huskisson where another group
of aborigine children were residing but cannot attend the school there
owing to the objection of the white parents. This suggestion was not
proceeded with and he last movement was to endeavour to induce the
Federal Capital Commission to do something for the Summer Cloud Bay
children. The Commission, however, referred the question to the
Aborigines Protection Board and no progress has been made. From the last
report it appears that there are nine children residing at Summer Cloud
Bay and six at Huskisson. There is no definite reserve at Summer Cloud
Bay and as is usual in such cases the number of children varies since
the parents are somewhat nomadic in their habits. In any case the
establishment of a half-time school for fifteen pupils seems rather
venturesome particularly as provision cannot be made for accommodation
at Huskisson. A building is available at Summer Cloud Bay. The only
solution which I can suggest is to ask the Board to consider the
question of appointing a teacher for Summer Cloud Bay on a subsidised
basis. If this be done it might happen that some of the Huskisson
parents would move in order to take advantage of the facilities
available and thereby strengthen the case for a provisional school.

17/2/1928 -- Providing educational facilities for Aboriginal children at
Summer Cloud Bay. Letter from Department of Education to APB. "...the
number of children available is insufficient to warrant the appointment
of a Departmental teacher. With a view to providing for the education of
these children I wish to suggest, for the consideration of your board,
the establishment of a subsidised school and he employment of a private
teacher under similar conditions as outlined in the appended leaflets. I
may add that should your Board decide to adopt this proposal the
Department would be pleased to furnish a list of persons who are
applicants for positions as subsidised School teachers."

**Teachers**

Teacher -- Public School Jervis Bay -- Report in relation to a letter
sent asking if the teacher could state if there would be any opposition
to the admission of "Colored Children" from the parents of children.
13/9/1924 -- the teacher writes that "The Chaplain objects to colored
children being admitted to my school".... In letter from teacher --

"Comments on Mr. Henderson's report:

Par 1: The enrolment or attendance of 12 to 14 is doubtful and depends
on fishing. Probably there would be an influx from Huskinsson and other
centers in order to send children to school.

Par 2. I do not consider it within my province but if agreed to could
arrange for his accommodation but not at my home, see par. 4.

Par 5. I previously informed Mr. Henderson my Department thoroughly
understood I think if an appointment is to be made he would consult you
but that will not be till next year as no sanction has been received for
the commencement of the building. I told Mr. Henderson you would be
pleased to meet him.

Par.6 The Chaplain is quite tight but as the cost is a Commonwealth
matter I must refrain from comments. However, I glanced at the plan and
the proposed building was certainly very nice.

Par.7. The control being in Captain's Lands is very necessary and would
permit me and the Chaplain to speak out with authority and, if
necessary, have orders given to enforce our desires. We have a large
number of single men here and should the colored people be permitted to
entertain freely then I very much fear there would be some nasty
affairs. We would forbid dancing, and should any misbehavior occur we
could close the Hall or forbid entertaining any but coloured people at
Wreck Bay. Its no use missing matters in this respect hence the control
suggested is good. Should our orders be disobeyed the Captain would
forbid the people coming to College for supplies.

Par.8. I forgot to mention that the Chaplain would go to the school
one-half day a week for Religious instruction hence the pupils would
have a littler more than half time. Under ordinary circumstances the
proposal would be declined and I would so recommend, but the agreement
between the State and Commonwealth regarding education in the Territory
may result in the appointment and therefore I urge a male teacher."
(Signed R.L.Lord 7/11/1924)

**Community**

30/3/1927 -- There are now fourteen Aboriginal children over five years
of age at Summer Cloud Bay, and seven at Huskisson. The children at
Huskisson belong to two families, and one of these families is expected
to shortly leave for Braidwood. Parents of children attending Huskisson
still maintain a hostile attitude respecting the admission of aborigines
to the school. A trial might be made of a black's school at Summer Cloud
Bay. A male teacher would be required, and accommodation cannot be
obtained nearer than the village at the Naval College, three miles
distance. I recommend that action be taken to establish an aborigines
school at Summer Cloud Bay.\

**Students**

27/6/1927- Memo from Dept of Education to The Secretary, Federal Capital
Commission. "It has been brought under the notice of this Department
that there are about fourteen aboriginal children at Summer Cloud Bay
for whom no educational facilities are provided. As a rule this
Department does not accept any financial responsibility for the
education of such children though permitting them to attend existing
schools where no local objection is raised. In this case, however, it is
understood that a school building was erected for the coloured children
by the Federal Government and the question has arisen as to whether your
Government would consider any method whereby these children's education
could be provided for."

Letter from Captain of .... Mr Henderson, Chaplain deputed to report,
regarding the establishment of school for residents at Summer Cloud Bay.

There is at present six children of school age at Wreck Bay. But other
children (Half-caste) come from adjacent parts during the fishing season
which lasts about 6 months. The children never attend school. I consider
the average attendance, should facilities be granted, would be 12 to 14
children. I would respectfully suggest for the Department of Educations
consideration that an assistant male teacher be drafted to the Public
School at Jervis Bay and that he be borne for half-time duties at Wreck
Bay. The distance from Wreck Bay to Jervis Bay is approximately 3 miles.
The teacher would have to live at Jervis Bay as no man could be excepted
to endure the conditions of the settlement at Wreck Bay. There are 42
scholars on the roll at Jervis Bay. The Half-caste children are
extremely difficult to teach so care should be exercised in the choice
of a teacher.\
\
I understand that the Department of Home and Territories have plans for
a school building at Wreck Bay to cost somewhere in the region of £700.
I consider this to be quite too elaborate and unnecessary. All that is
needed is a school room capable of seating 20 children comfortably. £200
should be ample for the building as only one room is necessary. There
are sufficient second-hand school desks in the Works Department...."
