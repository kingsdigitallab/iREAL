33/11/1892 -- APB -- Board for the Protection for Aborigines "Extract
from the Report respecting Aborigines, Cowra District" -- Education --
Only six aboriginal children attend the Public School, and the
attendance is not very regular. The reserve upon which the aborigines
and half-castes reside is nearly two miles from the school. None are
being educated privately. If a small school were established on their
reserve, an average attendance of nearly 20 could be secured. These
children do not care to mix with the white children. Signed Jas(?)
Harvey Sergeant, Cowra.

30/11/1892 -- "The Proposed School for Aborigines at Mulyan near Cowra
-- Summary of Report -- Chief Inspector -- I inquired into this matter
yesterday and saw most of the children. 1. The Aborigines have about 30
acres reserved for them, and the land is under the control of the "Board
for the Protection of Aborigines". Most of the blacks are related, and
are natives of the district, where they have lived for many years. They,
with the exception of able bodied men and young women, are supplied by
the authorities with rations, blankets and some clothing; they dwell in
huts on the reserve. The men often go away to work, but the families
remain on the camp. The Sergeant of Police of Cowra considers that they
are likely to be permanent. 2. A list of children is attached. An
enrolment of about 24, and an average of 16 or 17 may be expected. 3. I
have selected a high and dry site of 2 acres in the NW corner of the
Reserve. I consider that the Aborigines Protection should provide a
building, and give up the site mentioned, the Education Department
supplying furniture and apparatus, and paying the teacher. I recommend
(a) That a school, to be called "Mulyan Provisional School" (For
Aborigines) be established, on condition that the Board for the
Protection of Aborigines will hand over to the Education Department the
land described and the accompanying site form, and erect a weatherboard
building to measure 17ft+14ft+9ft (to wallplate), and 2 (?) -- The
Minister to provide the furniture and apparatus, and pay a teacher. (b)
That the inspector be empowered to get tenders for the furniture if
required (c) That the Board referred to, be asked to submit the name of
a competent teacher, to be approved of by the Education Department."
Signed Inspector Friend. Note from Under Secretary: "I concor, except as
to the Board giving up the site to this Department. If they are to
provide the school it is too much to expect them to give the site to
this Dept. " Submitted Approved.

2/9/1928 -- Teacher to Dept. Public Instruction -- Re: Asking for a
continuance of salary at 1^st^ Provisional Rates "Sir, I have the honor
to state that I received notification from the "Accountant" that as the
attendance at the above School did not maintain an average of 16 my
salary will in future be only £72 per annum. I have now the honor to
make application for the continuance of the 1^st^ provisional salary, in
support of which I beg to submit the following facts. (1) I was in chafe
of Wangamella Public Class 9 until April 1898 at Provisional rates. I
then took charge of Mulyan Provisional which ranked as 1^st^ Provisional
but about the time I came some children left the district, and it being
a school for aborigines I have no chance of keeping the average. (2) I
hold myself in readiness for examination to qualify myself for the
charge of a Public School. (3) [I am a married man], and
have to pay 7/6 per week for rent in Cowra, therefore it will be almost
impossible to live with the reduced salary of £72 per annum. So trusting
that my application will be received, and that my salary will be allowed
to remain at £88 per annum, until I am permitted to sit for examination.
" Signed Table (teacher)

17/3/1897 -- Memo to the Chief Inspector to Mr. Ross Teacher of the
Provisional School at Mulyan re Regular Inspection, 12^th^ March "Your
attention is called to the following extracts from Mr. Inspector
Friends' report on the recent Regular Inspection of the School under
your charge: - "One of the water closets is in a dirty condition XXXX
There have been no entries made in the Lesson Registers since the 5^th^
instant. XXXX Lesson Guides not strictly in accordance with the
standards in some particulars. The results indicate faulty methods of
teaching. A large percentage of subjects fail to meet standard
requirements. A distinct retrogression in the attainments is visible."
It is necessary that you should furnish an explanation in regard to the
defects pointed out. This memorandum should be returned to this office
with your reply."

19/5/1897 -- Letter from Teacher to Chief Inspector Sydney re Mulya
(Aboriginal) Explanatory of defects at inspection. "Sir, I beg to state
in reply to your letter pointing out the defects reported by Mr.
Inspector Friend that (1) The dirty condition of the water-closets was
caused through some of the stock belonging to the Aborigines getting
into it at night time where there was no one on the school premises (2)
The reason the entries were not made in the "Lesson Registers" from
Friday the 5^th^ till the 12^th^ instant, is that each day I took a note
of the lesson given, and every Friday afternoon I entered them in the
Registers, my object for this being to keep the Registers neat and
clean. IN the case of Mr. Friend pointed out to me where I was in fault
in my method of teaching. I took a note, and by strictly observing
instruction, I trust to derive beneficial results therefrom in the
future. When Mr. Friend examined the school I was disappointed at some
of the results, but from my experience I find that comparing the
"Aborigines" with other children (From a point of learning) they can
comprehend almost as quick as other children but cannot retain it for
any length of time, and on this account a continual repetition of work
is necessary."
