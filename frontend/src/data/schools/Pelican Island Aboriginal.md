1/1/1891 -- Letter from Teacher Mr. Martin Burke -- "There is no Blacks
Reserve midway between the schools mentioned -- the reserve which
probably you indicate being adjacent to Summer Island school and 5 miles
from Pelican Island School. Hence if it be decided to establish a school
for the blacks, between the two schools mentioned it will be necessary
to resume land which would cost about £50 an area and the building
required would cost about £25. I have interviewed the aboriginals of
Pelican Island and hey state that they will [not] send their
children to such a school if erected, say 2 miles up the river. If such
would be the result I think it more advisable to have the school on
Pelican Island. There are 10 children there who would attend, according
to promise, and further there is a building erected by government on the
island which would be suitable for a school. From what information I
could gather I am satisfied that the aboriginals would not be pleased
with the school -- if it be on Pelican Island even -- But those living
on the island would be compelled by law to attend. The teacher of such
school too, unless he were an aboriginal would have a most disagreeable
position. I would suggest that matters continue as they are, excepting
that a W.C be erected for the Blacks use at all the schools where the
children are. Should you wish for any further information I would
endevour to obtain it."

20/1/1891 -- Police Station Kempsey -- "The exact locality of the
"Blacks" Reserve, Macleay River, between the Pelican and Summer Island
Public Schools is not stated but it is concluded to be that in the
village of Kinchela. At present only three Aboriginals children would
attend a school there. At Pelican Island, five miles lower down the
river, ten would attend and six more who live on Shark Island lower down
would also attend. I would therefore beg to recommend that the School be
built on Pelican Island Reserve."

31/1/1891 -- Letter from Board for the Protection of Aborigines to the

Warell Creek July 14^th^ 1901 -- "Sir, would you kindly lay the
following statement before the Aborigines Board. Sometime ago I spent a
month on the beach at the New Entrance where there is a population of
natives camped there, with 42, intelligent looking children having no
school. I have since thought often of the pity that nothing could be
done for them in the way of education. I have thought out one (?ploy)
which I think would work with very little expense. I taught a school at
Belmore River many years ago where I successfully resisted the prejudice
of the whites against the black children attending I there had a very
intelligent half-caste lad, who learned well. He was then about 15 and
in the highest class. He now has sufficient education for the purpose,
and has been remarkably well behaved since. I send on his letter to me
which is a reply from some inquiries. He is called Alfred Woodlands. If
he were appointed half time teacher to (?) of the places he mentions,
and leaving the native to erect the buildings, I feel certain that his
influence would get a good attendance and be otherwise conductive to
good results. He h as a fair knowledge of gardening and took 1^st^ prize
for onions at one of four agricultural shows and there is plenty of
Aboriginal reserve land found at Burnt Bridge and Pelican Island. But
periodical the nomadic instincts of the blacks will assert themselves
and for this a tent and tressels could be provided to be carried about
and erected at the Camping ground. I feel certain that Woodlands would
have no difficulty in carrying this out. There is too great a gap
between the white and the native and hence the small advance made in
their civilization. I may say that Sir (?Gus Grey) who took a keen
interest in Maori civilization held the same opinion and favoured the
appointment of half caste or pure Maori teachers, I would respectfully
urge them that some reading, writing and gardening be taught these
children." Signed A.P Macklin

July 15 1901 -- Letter from A.P. Macklin to Department -- "In writing to
you about the aborigines I referred to a note from a half-caste, Alf
Woodlands, showing that he was quite sufficiently educated to teacher
all that would be necessary. I forgot to attach the note, which I now
send. Please put it to my letter, or if you forwarded it send the note
itself to the Board. It is a disgrace to us that these children should
not be prepared for absorption into the general community, as with their
Turanian instincts they cannot be kept a pure race and hence their
extinction as such is certain. In the meantime I think it is a Christian
duty to give them every chance."

Police Court deposition taken at the Court of Petty Sessions at
Gladstone in the Case of Thomas Watson Teacher of the Provisional (Abor)
School at Pelican Island (Corporal Punishment Prosecution) --

Harry Mumbler -- (Aboriginal) Laborer, residing on the Aboriginal
Reserve Pelican Island (Jerseyville) I am the father of Harry Mumbler
aged 12 years. He goes to Mr. Watson's school. On 5^th^ December 1922, I
sent the boy to school and I saw him about 4.30 pm on the day. My wife
stripped him and I saw his back all bruised. I saw Mr. Watson that
afternoon on the road near Mr. Mannix?, He said, "I've given your boy a
bit of a caning today for not doing his lessons'. My wife and boy were
going along the road. Watson said, "Your wife is coming along the road
there Micky. Why make a fuss about it? Don't take any notice of her." I
saw defendant next morning and sang out to him to come to the house and
look at the boy. He looked at him and said "nice bloody mess you've got
me in now getting Ehsman on the phone". We stripped the boy. He looked
at him and said, "I admit I belted the boy I didn't think I belted him
so much." I asked him about taking him to the Doctor, he said "All right
I'll take him". Defendant, the boy and I drove to Kempsey in a car
together. When we got to Kempsey Defendant told me not to tell the
Doctor that he belted the boy. I said "I will tell the Doctor; the
Doctor might think that my wife or I belted him if I don't". Defendant
is not always at the school to teach. Sometimes he is drinking. I have
seen him on the island intoxicated. That is where the school is. I saw
him drunk in October 1922.

To Mr. Stewart: I call flogging, belting. Defendant didn't say he caned
the boy. Mr. Drew came back in the car to the Doctor.

Harry Mumbler -- School Boy 12 years old. I go to School to Mr. Watson.
ON 5^th^ December last I was at School and I didn't do my sums right and
Mr. Watson took me to the Blackboard and I did my sums right there. Then
he belted me with a lawyer cane about 3 feet long. He caught me with one
hand and hit me with the cane across the shoulder. It hurt me. I
carried. I told my mother when I went home. I went afterwards to see Dr.
Elhone. Mr. Watson drove my father and me in his car to kempsey. Mr.
Watson was sober when he belted me. Some days he teaches school and some
days he goes to sleep in school.

To Mr. Stewart. I gotten get a belting in school. I did not know how to
do the sums until I went to the blackboard. Mr. Watson grows vegetables
at the school. We boys help. I didn't pull any of the vegetables up when
he said I pulled them up. This is not what he belted me for. I cried for
a long time after we went out of school. The cane was the size of the
one I see. HE caned me with a different cane like that one. That was the
can he used on the other children. I told Mr. Watson I had a sore hand
that day when he went to cane me on the hand. I hadn't it tied up. That
was when he hit me on the shoulder. I fell of a tree five days after I
had been to the Doctor and hurt the same shoulder.

To Mr. Hardiman. The cane I see is the same kind of cane and about the
same thickness as the one I was caned with.

Thomas Ehsman -- Constable at Jerseyville on 5^th^ December last a
complaint was made by Mr. Mumbler. In consequence, I saw Harry Mumbler.
He was marked on the left shoulder. It appeared to be a recent mark. I
spoke to Mr. Watson on the telephone.

Joseph Benedict McElhone -- Government Medical Officer, Kempsey. About
two months ago, I saw Harry Mumbler. Mr. Watson brought him to my
surgery. I found a considerable amount of bruising from the base of the
neck to the shoulder blade. A stick 3 feet long similar to the one I see
would cause such bruising. There were 8 or 10 marks reaching across the
shoulders to the lower side of the shoulder blades. They indicated very
severe punishment. I would say that the punishment was more severe than
was necessary in chastising a child. Mr. Watson told me that he had
given the boy a hiding and asked me to look at him.

To Mr. Stewart -- Watson enquired as to the boy's condition immediately
after my examination. I told Watson I could order no treatment for the
boy and that the mater would have to take its course as in the case of a
black eye. The skin was not broken.

Thomas Watson (Defendant) teacher of the Provisional (Abor) School
Pelican Island. Harry Mumbler is a pupil at my school. I admit I caned
the boy as indicated, I don't think that it was on the neck. In the
first instance, he would not do his sums while seated at the desk. I
took him to the blackboard and put five sums for him to work out. I knew
he could do the work but he had not one of the last fifteen given to him
right. He also pulled the vegetable up, would just bite pieces out. He
at first gave me some insolence and then said his hand was sore. I
didn't cane him on the hand on that account but across the left
shoulder. The cane I used was only about half the size of the one I see
and straight without curls on knobs on it. When I walked out of school
after caning him he can disappeared. I saw him again the same afternoon
and again next day. I saw his back. I said words to the boy's father to
the effect that he was trying to turn Eshman (the local Constable)
against me. I told the boy's father that I had caned him. I admitted
punishing him severely but said for what he had done I was justified. I
was taking Mrs. Drew to the Doctor and told the boy's father I would
take him too. I didn't tell Mumbler not to tell the Doctors that I did
it.

To Mr. Hardiman. It was about 3 pm when I caned the boy. I had
complained from the boy's mother about 5 pm. I met the father and said
to him, "I've caned him". I told him what it was for. I said. "Your wife
is kicking up a fuss about it." I admit the punishment was more severe
than is normally given. I didn't take him to the Doctor because I
considered it a case for the Doctor, it was because the father said he
ought to see the Doctor. The blacks said to me "We will get you kicked
out this time." There has been a Police enquiry since regarding me. It
is not true that I have been under the influence of liquor on the
Island. I was at School on 22^nd^ to 24^th^ January. In only sleep at
the school in lunch hour not because I am under the influence of liquor.

Plea: Not guilty

Fined: £2 cost of court 12/6, Medical expenses £3'3, Witness expenses
12/-, Prof.al costs £3.3 or two months hard labour in kempsey gaol.

From the medical officer's evidence at the Police Court, it is evident
that the flogging was unduly severe. There are times when the most
faithful and diligent teachers may, in their anxiety for results, be
guilty of harsh treatment. In this case, I am inclined to think that the
Teacher was not in that judicial frame of mind essential to the proper
infliction of punishment. I am disposed to think that the Teacher's
statement in regard to the punishment for pulling vegetables was the
result of an after thought. I have little doubt that the Teacher has
allowed himself to lapse into habits of intemperance in the use of
alcohol. His general appearance and manner bear out his own statement
that he had been doing a fair amount of drinking. On the morning of the
enquiry, there was a pronounced smell of liquor about him. At first, he
stoutly denied that he had been drinking but subsequently admitted to
having taken two drinks that morning. A professional gentleman living
with the neighbourhood assured me that the Teacher was in the habit of
consuming drink at home. It is not surprising that he has shown some
negligence in the discharge of teaching duties. He admits that he used
to be down and sleep "for half an hour or so" during recess while the
children were away. The stated that he opened the school on the day
previous to the enquiry. On being further pressed, he stated that he did
not come to the school but gave young Mumbler some lessons in his
father's hut. Young Mumbler's account of the time spent in this work
does not agree with that of the teacher. I consider that the Teacher's
conduct is deserving of the most server censure out. I should like to
mention a few points in his favour. 1. So far as I knew, his lapse is of
comparatively recent date.2. The residents give him some credit for
attention to his duties as manager, 3. His wife is now seriously ill in
a Sydney Hospital she has done some really good work among the
Aboriginals. 4. The court case has caused him considerable worry. I have
little faith in his professions of sorrow not have I any great hopes for
his reform. He should, however, be given an opportunity to amend his
conduct. Recommended the teacher by informed that in future all corporal
punishment must be inflicted in strict accordance with the regulations
laid down by the Department. With regard to his conduct, he should be
warned that unless there is an immediate and permanent improvement in
his general conduct and attention to duty, the question of dispensing
with his services as a teacher under the Department will be considered."
Signed (?Putland)
