8/2/1894 - Dept. Public Instruction replying to Accountants Memo --
Provisional School Cabbage Tree Island -- "As all the scholars of the
above school are Aboriginals, I wrote to Mr. Board, the Inspector having
direct Superintendence of the school, in reference to charging of school
fees. The reply was, that Aboriginal children received a free education,
and further, that the fee forms sent to me, were merely part of my
instructions."

Reply from Chief Inspector -- "As the only habitations on Cabbage T ree
Island are the huts of the Aboriginals the Teacher is obliged to live at
Wardell 2 miles distant. His dependence on the Aboriginals for transit
interferes with his punctual arrival at the school and prevents him from
returning to Wardell till late in the evening. A punt such as is
described in the enclosed tenders can be cheaply obtained and will serve
the purpose well. I recommend that the teacher Mr. Griffin, be
authorized to obtain a punt from Mr. Otterbridge in accordance with his
tender at cost of £3.10/ Under Secretary noted underneath -- "The
attendance here is very small and the experience of the Department in
connection with these small Aboriginal schools is that they last but a
very short time, especially when a school or too many other civilizing
agencies are forced upon the aboriginals. I recommend that the
application be denied."

18/1/1895 -- Dept Public Instruction -- This School, established for the
Aboriginal settlement occupying Cabbage Tree Island had last quarter an
enrolment of 14 and an average of 9.1. Until a larger number of blacks
can be induced to settle on the island there is but little prospect of
an attendance sufficient to warrant the continuance of the school. I
recommend that this school be closed for the present. Mr. Reid, Sergeant
of Police, Wardell, will take charge of the Department's property."

16/11/1899 -- Dept. Public Instruction -- "Re: Occupation of building by
half-caste Jack Roach" -- "By telegram of this date I have stated that
there is no objection to the disused school building at Cabbage Tree
Island by Jack Roach under the Supervision of Senior Constable Reid of
Wardell. Mr Reid has the custody of the building at present and the
oversight of the Aboriginal settlement on the island. If Roach occupies
the building, Mr. Reid will see that he clears away surrounding grass
and properly of the desks etc. The building is likely to be safer then
it is now. I suggest, if Roach is allowed occupation, that Senior
Constable Reid of Wardell be informed of it and asked to continue his
supervision of the premises with a view to such work being done by Roach
as will insure the safety of the building and furniture."

14/11/1899 -- Telegram from Chief Inspector Dept. Public Instruction --

209/9/1893 Chief Inspector -- On visiting the Aboriginal settlement at
Cabbage Tree Island today I found 12 children who would attend the
school if now opened. Another family with 2 children is likely to return
to the island in a week or two. It is stated by the leader of the camp
that 7 other children would return to the island if the school were
opened. The school if opened now would have an attendance of 13 or 14,
and the present is a favorable time for opening it. The key of the
school is in the charge of Mr. Reid of the Wardell Police. I recommend
that the teacher be now appointed."

21/1/1893 -- Cabbage Tree Island -- Summary report on Application for
the establishment of a Provisional School and Recommendation. "In
consequence of the protest lodged by the residents of Wardell, against
the admission of the Aboriginal children of Cabbage Tree Island to the
Public School. I visited the native settlement on 12^th^ instant, in
company with Constable Wells of Wardell, to make inquiry as to the
desirableness of establishing a small school on the island above named.
Constable Wells, having sent word before hand of our intended visit, I
was enabled to see all of the children, and almost all of the adults.
The population all told now reaches about 50. There are about 25
children, 19 of whom are fit to attend school. The parents all seemed
very much pleased at the prospect of having a school to themselves and
....from the ready manner in which the children attending Wardell
school, have submitted to school routine there can be no doubt that the
enrolment and attendance of a locally established school will be
maintained without least appearance of Coercion. The children though
dressed in old clothes all looked clean and healthy, and some of them
appeared to be very intelligent. The adult portion of the population is
engaged in cultivating land, and there are now seven different blocks
under cultivation p the chief crop being cane, which is purchased by the
Col. Sugar Co. at Broadwater. There is one half-caste woman on the
island, who, having been trained as a servant in a white family in
Casino, is superior to the rest, and whose influence over the girls now
growing up is likely to be very beneficial. This woman's husband is a
white man but he does not live on the island. The police authorities are
inclined to insist upon her retirement from Cabbage Tree Island, but in
view of the proposal to establish a school among the residents I am
satisfied that her presence will be a distinct advantage to the rest of
the natives. Whatever official position there may be to this woman's
residence on Cabbage Tree Island, I am inclined to think such objection
should be withdrawn for the reasons stated herein. A good site on the
highest part of the island is available a little to the north of the
Government boat shed (now being erected). IN the accompany site form I
am unable to state with certainty, what parish, if any, the site is in.
.... I recommend that a Provisional School be established at Cabbage
Tree Island Richmond River.

7/7/1919 -- Letter from Mrs. Caldwell to Mr. Foate, "Owing to all the
extra work as well as the strain and worry brought about by the epidemic
of influenza, Mr. Caldwell has completely broken down in health. Dr.
Monti has given him a certificate I forwarded to the Education
Department requesting a month rest from work. He has ordered him
complete rest and a change this he says is essential to restore him to
his usual health. The people at present are not fit to be left but as
they are convalescent, we thought, if the Board will part permission, of
having the last two weeks for a change. Hoping for your favourable
reply."

11/7/1919 -- Application for Sick leave. Letter from Mrs. Caldwell to
Dept. Education - "Enclosed with this letter you will find a medical
certificate given to my husband by Dr. Monti. I wish to state that the
breakdown was caused by nursing and generally caring for the Aborigines
on this station during an epidemic of influenza. During the earlier
period of the outbreak, Mr. Caldwll had to tramp through Mud and water
past his knees day and night, as well as spending entire nights,
watching beside sick beds. As we have had so far eight-six cases of
influenza, over twenty of which were pneumonia, you will understand that
our task has been no light one."

Leave asked for -- One month for Mr. Caldwell to recoperate is

27/2/1893 -- Under Secretary -- In view of the objections of the parents

Police Station Wardell -- 8/5/1908 -- Re Aboriginal children at Cabbage
Tree Island not allowed to attend the Public School at Wardell. --
"Constable Brown No. 4895 respectfully reports that a number of white
residence at Wardell got a petition signed to debar the Aborigines
children from attending the Wardell Public School. The petition was
forwarded to the Department of Education, and came back for the school
teacher to inform the Aborigines children that they was debared from
attending the Wardell Public School. But since then some of the

10/6/1908 -- To Chief Inspector -- Cabbage Tree Island -- "... I have to
report that Broadwater residents interviewed me re the attendances of
these aborigines at the local school. It appears that after their
exclusion from Wardell, the teacher at Broadwater not knowing the facts
of the case admitted them at this school. This is evidently likely to
cause trouble at Broadwater, and pending your decision I have instructed
the teacher not to admit them again until he is instructed to do so.
There is only one way that I can see to settle the difficulty and that
is to re-open the Island school for the Aboriginal children (19 in
number). I again recommend this action and as the school is only 1 1/2
miles from Broadwater, it should be placed in my district.

9/6/1908 -- Chief Inspector Cabbage Tree Island. "In response to a
request from these aborigines, I visited their settlement today. Their
children have been excluded from ordinary public schools and are now
without the means of education. They especially wished me to ask the
Dept. to send them a teacher as soon as possible. I promised to do so.
The enrolment will most probably be 19."

20/2/1908 -- To the Under Secretary of the Department of Public
Instruction -- Petition objecting to the attendance of Aboriginal
children -- "Dear Sir, we the undersigned parents of pupils attending
Wardell Public School kindly beg to draw your attention to the following
(1) that a number of Aboriginal children from the Cabbage Tree Island
home, attend the said school (2) that proper attention is not paid to
their cleanliness (3) that we do not consider it fair to our children to
be thrust into such association with said aboriginal children. Trusting
that you will take immediate steps towards remedying the cause of
complaint. Signed by 11 residents of Wardell.

Teacher Wardell writing to Dept Education re: Aboriginal children --
supplying information requested by the Board. In point (2) he wrote "The
children are examined on the ranks with other children and are
apparently clean. The girls are generally dressed in some dark coloured
print and are tidy. Thte boys are generally dressed in neat Conway
suits. These suits when of crash or drill are well washed and in several
cases splendidly ironed (particularly with the Roberts children). In
spite of this apparent cleanliness. There is the strong smell of Camp
black and I believe that the camp life is very dirty (dogs children and
filth in the one room). (3) the children are not taught separately but
are kept apart from the other children as much as possible. They
generally occupy a seat by themselves in the same room as that occupied
by the first and second classes. When they came first to school I
advised each family to bring a mug for drinking purposes. This was done
and is being done in many cases.

Reply in separate letter from Inspector in describing what the teacher
from Wardell had written he wrote "The same conditions prevail in the
Lismore district school where the enrolment of aboriginals varies from 6
to 12. One of my own children was for 12 months taught in the same
classes as these children. Provided that the children are taught in a
separate seat I see no good reason for prohibiting their attendance. The
settlement is not strong enough to warrant the re-opening of the
Aboriginal Prov. School. I recommend (1) that the teacher be instructed
to continue his policy of keeping the Aboriginal children separate from
the white pupils (2) that the petitioners, through Mr. J. Joyce, be
informed that the custom of admitting Aboriginal children to public
schools, prevails elsewhere. That so long as the children come clean and
tidy, and are taught in a separate sear the Department sees no good
reason for prohibiting their attendance." Note from Under Secretary that
follows "I do not concur with the Inspector. The Department has
established the policy of excluding aboriginal children from schools if
the parents of white children object, and, as objection has been ..."

14/3/1938 - APB -- Subject: The condition of Mrs. Dalleys Health -- On
the 8^th^ Instant I inspected Brewarrina Aboriginal Station as per
attached reports. But I wish particularly to draw attention to the state
of the Matron Mrs Dalleys Health in this report. Mrs Dalleys appearance
shocked me the trouble is mainly mental due to the recent charges made
before the Select Committee concerning her Sons alleged immorality with
Aboriginal girls. Although Ferguson made these serious charges and
admitted that they were based on heresay Yet the press gave full
publicity to the matter. This has so preyed on Mrs. Dalley's mind that
she is in danger of a collapse, and her husband has to be in constant
attendance on her. Both Mr. and Mrs. Dalley have given Loyal and
efficient service to this Board over a number of Years. Mrs. Dalley in
fact doing at times the major part of the Teaching to allow her Husband
more time in the Management duties. The School Inspectors report give
Mrs. Dalley the credit that is due to her for excellent results at
Cabbage Tree. Both Officers volunteered to go to Brewarrina when the
Board experienced difficulty there with previous management. Personally
I cannot speak too highly of both Mr. and Mrs. Dalleys service. And
although the loss of their services to this Department would be
regretted I would strongly recommend that the Public Service Board be
approached with a view to securing Transfer for Mr. Dalley. " Signed
Inspector
