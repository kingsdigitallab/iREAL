**Role of the Department and relationship to the APB**

20 June 1901 -- APN to Dept. Public Instruction -- Re: As to necessity
for establishment of a school for Aboriginals, and nominating Miss
Montgomery as Teacher -- "Sir, At the recently formed Home for
Aborigines at Runnymede, near Casino, there are a number of children
growing up without receiving any education, and my Board are of opinion
that it would be desirable to establish a school for their instruction.
At the present time there are about twenty children on the Station. Mr.
John Montgomery has been appointed Manager of the Home, and the bearer,
Helen Montgomery, (his daughter). Is an applicant for the position of
teacher there. She is 16 years of age and has attended a public school
for a period of nine years, and the Board think it extremely desirable
that she should have a trial, with some reasonable payment for her
services. Mrs. Montgomery is leaving for Runnymede with her family on
Saturday next."

25 July 1901 -- Memo from Chief Inspector to Dept. Public Instruction re
Proposed school for Aboriginals Runnymede -- "At about 14 miles form
Casino, on the main Queensland Road to New Park, a home for Aboriginals
has recently been set down under the supervision of Mr. John Montgomery.
The area, on which these blacks are located, has been dedicated for the
settlement of Aborigines, where it is intended, at a later period to
cultivate the land. Four or five slab huts have been built, and these,
with a few additional bark humpies, provide the shelter. There are
numbers of blacks, old and young, on the settlement, with many children,
about 20 I was informed. In consultation with the Manager, I asked him
if he would be able to muster the children so that I might see them.
About 10 only were to be seen, the others, as stated, being away for the
time being. I attach a list of the children on the station, but the ages
are mere guess work. Miss Helen Montgomery, the Managers daughter, is
about 16 1/2 years of age, intelligent and cheerful. She would be a
suitable Teacher, from appearances... ...... The erection of a building
for school purposes will be necessary, as there is not a house
available, and Mr. Montgomery informed me that he thought the blacks
would be able to cut and provide the slabs for the walls -- also to put
them up. The Department would be required to supply the timber for
joists, rafters, flooring and the iron for roofing....... I submit all
the facts I have gathered, but, not knowing if similar schools exist in
other localities, cannot say what the probable result would be.
Personally, I do not favour the establishment of such a school where so
few are of school age." Note from Under Secretary -- "The number of
children of school age is not sufficient to warrant this Department
incurring expense in providing a building, and as the Aborigines Board
has no funds for this purpose, I recommend that the application be
declined."

19 October 1901 -- APB to Under Secretary of Public Instruction Re
Runnymede Aboriginal -- "Sir, Advertising to your communication of the
9^th^ August last, No.1901/9813, regarding the proposed establishment of
a School for the Aboriginal children at the Runnymede Aboriginal
Station, near Casino, I am now directed to inform you that my Board is
strongly of opinion that it would be highly desirable for the children
to receive some schooling, and to recommend the probationary and
temporary appointment of the Manager's daughter, who is 16 years of age
and competent to undertake the duties, with a small allowance of say £12
per annum. It has been suggested that until a building is erected the
children could, at any rate during the Summer months, received
instructions under the shade of a tree."

24 December 1901 -- APB to Dept. Public Instruction re Requesting supply
of material -- "Sir, I am directed by the Board for Protection of
Aborigines to ask that you will be so good as to cause the necessary
school requisites to be supplied for the use of twelve (12) Aboriginal
children at the Board's Station at Runnymede, near Casino."

25 January 1902 -- APB to Dept. Public Instruction Re Reminder to
request for supply of school material -- "Sir, I am directed to invite
attention to my communication of the 24^th^ ultimo, asking that a supply
of school requisites might be sent for the use of 12 Aboriginal children
at Runnymede Aboriginal Station -- near Runnymede, - and to ask that you
will be so good as to cause the supply to be expedited."

3 Feb 1902 -- Letter from Inspector to Chief Inspector Dept. Public
Instruction -- "Sir, I have no school in my District to which the
accompanying requisitions refer. The establishment of it was declined
last year; and I cannot understand the meaning of these papers sent to
me."

6 March 1902 -- APB to Dept. Public Instruction -- "Sir, Referring to
previous correspondence in regard to the proposed establishment of a
School for Aborigines at Runnymede Home, near Casino, and to the
representations made by the members of this Board who waited upon the
Minister in the matter, I am now directed to ask if any decision has
been arrived at."

11 March 1902 -- APB to Dept. Public Instruction -- "Sir, In reply to
your letter of the 5^th^ instant, No.02/4882, 5546, inquiring upon what
grounds the application for School material for the Home for Aborigines
at Runnymede, near Casino, was made, I am directed to inform you that,
upon being advised that the Minister had declined to establish a School
at this place, the Board decided to allow the Manager's daughter to
undertake the work of teaching the children, and (with a view of
facilitating such instruction) to ask you Department to provide School
requisites."

START DEPUTATION -

Report on RUNYMEDE Aboriginal Home Subject -- "Asking, that a sum of
money be given to enable the Aborigines Board to start a school or in
lieu of same, that the Department should build a school -- Present Mr.
R. Scobie M.L.A and Mr. F. Norrie. Waited upon the Honourable J Perry on
Tuesday December 17^th^ 1901 -- DEPUTATION --

"On Tuesday, December 17^th^ 1901 a deputation consisting of Mr. Scobie
M.L.A and Mr. F Norrie waiting upon the Honourable John Perry, to ask
that a sum of money should be given to the Aborigines Board to enable
them to Build a school for the Blacks at RUNYMEDE -- or failing this
that the Department should build a school.

Mr. Scobbie M.L.A said he & Mr. Norrie had come before the Honourable
the Minister to ask that a sum of money should be given to them to
enable them to build a school to educate the Blacks at Runymede. As Mr.
Norrie was more versed in the matter than he, he would ask Mr. Norrie to
go into details.

Mr. F Norrie, sad, that Runymede they had a home for Blacks and they
took great pride in them. The place had been marked out into plots and
each Black worked a plot. There were about 18 children of school age
principally half-castes. They had applied to the Department for a school
but had been refused They had found out that the Daughter of the Manager
of these Homes, was quite prepared to teach the children for a small
fee. The MINISTER could not the Manager teach them? Mr. Norrie, did not
think he was qualified for that, he did not think that would be possible
at all. The Manager had to be out in the fields with the Blacks, as if
he was absent the Blacks would not work. He instanced the Home at
Grafton where there was a school for the Blacks with a teacher provided
by the Department.

The MINISTER -- Do you think it is any use teaching the Half caste
children?

Mr. Norrie said, that was a very debatable question, but he certainly
did think that it would be useless teaching the children of the full
blooded blacks.

The MINISTER, thought it would be no use teaching the half castes -- he
had had some experience amongst them.

Mr. R. Scobie said, he thought the Honourable the Minister would agree
with him that by putting the children to school it would teach them
obedience and put a restraint upon the,. He would also add, that these
Aboriginals had only been living on about half rations -- They could not
get meat or fish and the total amount voted for the Aborigines was only
about £1-18-0 in this State while South Australia was about £6 per head.

The MINISTER, thought that the Aboriginals in this State were very well
treated they had plenty to eat and tobacco to smoke. Anyhow, he had no
money to spend to build schools at the present time.

Mr. R Scobbie M.L.A asked whether the Honourable the Minister could give
them a small sum to enable them to build a school.

The MINISTER, said he could not do that, IF the matter came within the
four corners of their Educational Act the might be able to do something.
His own opinion was, that it was simply throwing money away. If they
could show him any good result that would accrue thorough building the
school, then he might think about it.

Mr. Norrie asked whether the Honourable the Minister would look at the
papers sent in the Department by the board in connection with this
proposed school.

The MINISTER said, Yes, he would do that and would even go further and
see what had been done in other parts of the State.

Mr. Norrie, said another small matter they wanted to ask about was the
school residence at the Grafton home. The Residence was in a very bad
state and the Board had had plans and specifications prepared for
additional two rooms and the estimated cost was £50. They asked whether
the Department would pay for this, or if they did it, would they be able
to charge a rental for the premises? He might add that a Teacher of the
Department was in charge of the attendance of about 40.

The MINISTER, What do the Blacks do there?

Mr. Norrie said, they farmed and the produce was sold. If the Blacks
worked they were paid, but if they did not, they simply lived there and
had rations given to them.

The MINISTER, asked whether they could not make it self-supporting.

Mr. Norrie, said, that the work they did was not sufficient especially
after they had paid the Manager and the Matron. The MINISTER in reply,
said, he would go into the whole matter and communicate with them in due
course."

END DEPUTATION

Letter from Teacher to APB -- 20/8/1903 re Blankets "Dear Sir, I have
received 50 Blankets from the Public Tender Board Department. I have
also received the Slates, Chalk and pencils required for the school. But
I have not received the dozen 2^nd^ Primer requisitions at the same
time. They are much needed." Signed John Montgomery. Note -- "referred
to the Under Secretary of Public Instruction.

Department of Public Instruction to Teacher 14/9/1903 -- "Sir, With
reference to your letter of the 20^th^ Ultimo, stating that you have not
received 1 dozen 2^nd^ Class Reading Books applied for by you, I am
directed to ask which of the following you require -- "New Australians
Reading Books, Primer Part 2" -- New Australian Reading Books, No. 2"
