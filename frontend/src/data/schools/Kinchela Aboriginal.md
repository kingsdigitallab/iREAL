13/2/1936 -- Department of Education -- Submitted by the Director of
Education -- Kinchela (Aborigines) -- Police inquiry into the conduct of
Mr. McQuiggan, Teacher and Superintendent. "This is a matter which must
be handled very delicately, since it involves two departments -- that of
the Chief Secretary and the Minister for Education. Mr. McQuiggan is the
Teacher-Manager of the Kinchela Aborigines. Home, Kempsey, and is
responsible to the Chief Secretary for the management of the Home and
property, for which he receives a salary of £70 p.a., and to the
Minister of Education for his work as a teacher for which he is paid
£230 p.a. Mr. McQuiggan is recommended for appointment as Manager of the
Kinchela home for Aborigines by the Aborigines' Protection Board, which
however, prior to their recommendations asks for the recommendation of
this Department as to the Manager's qualifications as a teacher. As the
appointee of the Chief Secretary, I take it that final jurisdiction lies
with that Minister. The Minister of Education is responsible of this
man's work as a teacher. If, therefore, any misconduct by Mr. McQuiggan
as teacher were reported, it would be for the Minister for Education to
authorize an inquiry, if necessary, the matter to be reported at once to
the Aborigines' Protection Board. I am of the opinion that no such
inquiry (unless in the most urgent circumstances) should be undertaken
without the knowledge of the Chief Secretary, both as a matter of
courtesy and right. I think, however, that it is quite essential that
each department should be fully informed of what action the other
intends to take for the purposes, if possible, of effective
co-operation. I do not think Mr. McQuiggan can be considered as
"practically a servant of this Department seconded to the Aborigines
Protection Board for certain extra school duties": the only jurisdiction
that this department has, in my opinion is that over Mr. McQuiggans
conduct as a teacher. In the present instance, a report concerning Mr.
McQuiggan's conduct and indebtedness, as Manager of the Home, having
reached the Commissioner of Police, (who is also Chairman of the
Aborigines Protection Board) he asked for a special report on the matter
from one of this officers and subsequently called for an open
Departmental inquiry into the matter at Kinchela Aborigines' Home. The
terms of reference were: - (a) Mr. McQuiggan's alleged drinking habits
(b) Mr. McQuiggans alleged illtreatment of the boys in using hosepipe as
an instrument of punishment. Etc. (c) McQuiggan's alleged indebtedness.
So far as I can see none of these matters have been raised in relation
to his work as a teacher but to that of a Manager, although his alleged
drinking practices and method of punishment as well as indebtedness
would all have direct bearing on Mr. McQuiggan as a teacher. However,
the matter was not referred to this Department until the open inquiry
had been held, and the results reported to the Aborigines' Protection
Board. Our own Inspector (Mr. G. H. Bruce) only learned accidentally
last month that such an inquiry had been held. Whether the Commissioner
of Police as such has the right to call for an open inquiry into the
conduct of one of the servants of the Chief Secretary without his
authority, or whether as Chairman of the Aborigines' Protection Board is
clothes with authority to make such inquiry, I cannot say, but I am
strongly of opinion that the Minister for Education should have been
informed of the action taken. This was not done. Until the papers were
referred to this Department after a full report of the whole matter had
been presented to the Aborigines' Protection Board I was quite unaware
of the matter. The serious point about the whole affair is the procedure
of the police inquiry, which of course, I do not question. It differs so
widely from the conduct of an inquiry of this Department that I feel
representations should be made to the Chief Secretary, where the Manager
is also a Teacher, for the holding of an investigation in accordance
with the principles followed by this Department in investigating
complaints against teachers, as these practices conform with the rules
laid down in the Public Service Act. The inquiry showed (a) that
evidence was obtained before an inquiry was commenced -- in fact it was
practically on that evidence that the open inquiry was ordered, (b) that
the teacher had no warning to prepare his case, (c) that the teacher was
not definitely charged, (d) that the name of the informant was not
disclosed. Inquiries into the conduct of public servants are carried out
on the principles laid down in the Public Service Act, Section 56. I
feel, therefore, that a request might be made to the Chief Secretary
that where a Manager who is also a Teacher is charged with misconduct,
the inquiry, if necessary, should be conducted in accordance with the
principles laid down in the Public Service Act: I am not at all sure
that our own Inspector should not look into the matter on behalf of the
Minister for Education, but, having no complaint against him as teacher,
there is nothing to investigate. At the same time, is one which may
refer directly to the disciplinary method adopted in school, that is, by
Mr. McQuiggan as an officer of this Department. The case has been very
fully considered by this Department and the following recommendations, I
which I fully concur, made: -

that appreciation be expressed to the Board for its courtesy in making

Department of Education Page 1 -- Police Inquiry of the 12^th^ November,
1935 -- Chief Inspector -- From a letter dated 11^th^ November, 1935,
served on him by Constable Clark, Mr. McQuiggan learnt for the first
time that an open inquiry into his conduct was to be held by
Superintendent Carter on the following day. This letter had been
preceded by inquiries made amoungst the inmates and in the district by
Inspector Bower, who, some days previous, had assembled the aborigines
and questioned them, in the absence of Mr. and Mrs. McQuiggan. Under the
circumstances, the evidence of these boys can hardly be regarded as
reliable. In the above letter Mr. McQuiggan was informed that "the
inquiry will cover your treatment of the boys who are inmates of the
said Home, your conduct, particularly your drinking habits, and your
alleged indebtedness to certain business people, as well as any other
matters which appear to be relevant to the issue." It will thus be seen
that Mr. McQuiggan was not made the subject of any definite charge which
he could refute, nor did he have the opportunity to prepare his defense.
The evidence taken ranges generally over the three issues of (1)
drinking habits, (2) treatment of inmates, and (3) Indebtedness, and for
the sake of clarity I refer to these issues in that order.

\(1\) [Conduct, particularly drinking habits."]

In the absence of a definite charge it would appear that evidence given
concerning matters which happened over twelve months ago, and which had
been dealt with by the former Commissioner, should not have been
admitted. In regard to conduct of this kind in more recent times, the
evidence, in my opinion, points unmistakably to the reasonable sobriety
on the part of Mr. McQuiggan. According to the typed evidence, Sharpley
(12. years), Bonds (16) and Morgan (13) are the only inmates who stated
in evidence that they had seen Mr. McQuiggan drunk in recent times. On
6^th^ November, Inspector Bower had reported to the Commissioner --
"Unfortunately the only information I can get -- apart from that
supplied by the boys -- is of a confidential nature, [and the persons
supplying it do not desire their names mentioned. I know them well, and
I am more that satisfied that what they told me was correct. It proves
that Mr. McQuiggan has again drifted into his drinking
habits]. In his evidence at the Inquiry, Inspector Bower-
(Page 2. depositions) -- stated that he had said to Mr. McQuiggin: "I
personally think that you have been drinking". Again, he said -- Page 3.
depositions -- "I would like to add, Mr. Chairman, that I have been at
this Home frequently and I have seen Mr. McQuiggin periodically ... [and
I have never seen him under the influence of liquir] ...[and
I have never at any time]

Page 2 -- noticed alcohol on him. Constable Goodwin admitted "definite
improvement within the last twelve months" (before which period it was
admitted he had been indulging in drink.) Moreover, each of nine (9)
residents stated at the inquiry -- "I have never seen him (Mr.
McQuiggan) under the influence of liquor"; Dr. Mcelhone said -- "I have
never seen him take liquor to excess but over two years ago."; Dr.
Hobden said -- "during the last twelve months I have always found Mr.
McQuiggin quote sober."

\(2\) [Treatment of inmates]

The evidence by inmates against Mr. McQuiggin in this connection covers
(a) "being tied to a fence and beaten by a rope, (b) being beaten with a
piece of rubber hose; (c) being beaten with a stockwhip, (d) being put
on bread and water.

The only affirmative evidence in support of (a) is that given by inmates
George Sharpley (12 years) who states that he was so treated; Lally
Morgan (13) and Jas. Smith, who confirm Sharpley's statement. The
last-named, however, corrected his first evidence and stated that
Sharpley was tied to a peach tree, not a fence. The other inmates
questioned on this matter were Bertie Field (12), Isaac Cruse (12),
William Sloan (18), Clarie Combo (15) -- these, and Alma Rogers, the
maid, had no knowledge of the matter. So unusual a happening would
surely have been known far and wide; and I do not see how credence can
be given to the evidence of these three boys (aged 12, 13 and 13 years
respectively).

\(b\) (c) and (d). The use of these forms of punishment is admitted by
Mr. McQuiggin. So far as the use of a small piece of hose is concerned,
it is obvious that the degree of punishment inflicted would depend
entirely on the degree of force used (see p.62 and 77 deposition).
Apparently it was introduced by Mr. McQuiggin some time after he took
charge, to deal with some of the more unruly inmates (p.144 deposition).
For example, it appears that sodomy has been practiced at some other
aborigines' stations in which inmates of Kinchela had previously been
concerned and also there had been thieving. Evidently the information on
these matters was accepted by Superintendent Carter. Further, Mr.
McQuiggin had been "knocked clean out with a crack on the back of my
neck. I had a weal across the stomach, and one at least of my fingers
was broken". The evidence does not show any cruel use of the hose. Even
the lad Sharpley, apparently one of the most determined of the adverse
witnesses admitted (p. 13 deposition) that he would receive only just a
little smack, and that he would sooner have that than the cane, and in
answer to Superintendent Carter the inmate Smith (13) said that he was
happy at the Home and that the boys were also happy. The Superintendent
stated that in view of the evidence he intended to cease using the hose
for this purpose at once. This followed Dr. McElhore's evidence (p77) in
which he stated that severe use of the hosepipe for punishment might
inflect serious internal injury without showing external evidence. A
stockwhip (doubled) appears to have been used, but rarely -- twice by
Mr. McQuiggin, according to Bertie Fields.

Page 3 (3) [Indebtedness]

At the inquiry Mr. McQuiggin admitted indebtedness to several creditors
amounting to about £100. This indebtedness is probably mainly due to the
fact that when he took charge of Kinchela, he was penniless and had to
buy furniture, etc., on time-payment. Most of the witnesses are
satisfied that he will pay this debts in due course.

In any case, as a teacher under this Department, Mr. McQuiggin is
entitled to be deal with in the matter of indebtedness in terms of
Section 56A of the Pubic Service Act, 1902. Moreover, at the recent
inspection of the school, Mr. McQuiggin's mark was advanced from B to B
plus, and Inspector Bruce's report on his work is most favourable. In
conversation Inspector Bruce expressed the opinion that Mr. McQuiggin is
an ideal man for his position. In speaking about Kinchela to me, he said
it is a "splendidly organized institution -- Mr. and Mrs. McQuiggin are
genuinely interested in the boys and the work, and one could not imagine
a better pair for the work. I often take visitors to see how splendidly
the Institution is run. Work-about clothing is provided, and uniforms
for abroad; and when he takes the boys out one thinks of a bank of
students from a College." It appears that Mr. McQuiggin had four years
service in the Great War, that he held commissioned rank, that he
suffered inquiry by a bullet through the sciatic nerve and a bullet
through the wrist, and that he was a prisoner of war for nine months.

(copy) Office of the Director General of Public Health, 52 Bridge Street
Sydney 11, February 1937 35/2683 -- The Secretary, Aborigines'
Protection Board, Subject: Kinchela Boys' Home, Kempsey. I am instructed
by the Director General of Public Health to inform you that Dr. Shannon
of this Department visited the abovementioned home on the 28^th^. Ultimo
and reports that he was much impressed by the suitability and efficiency
of the institution, and that the boys -- about 57 -- seem to be in
excellent health, well cared-for, and happy. It would appear, however,
that there is room for improvement, when finances permit, in regard to
the following matters -- COOKING AND DINING ARRANGEMENTS: The cooking
range and the dining-room are reported to be definitely too small.
DORMITORY: While the dormitory was very clean and tidy, there was
evidence of overcrowding, in some cases the beds being side by side
without an intervening space. This overcrowding is considered of
particular importance owing to the possibility of the occurrence of
epidemic infectious disease. SCHOOL ROOM: This room, which houses 57
pupils, measures about 15 ft. x 15 ft. and is far too small. There is an
annexe or back room in the school building, used at times for the
purposes of isolating sick boys, which could be used to enlarge the
school room by removing the intervening partition. In the circumstances
it is recommended -- 1. That a larger cooking range be installed. 2.
That the dining room be enlarged; 3. That the milk separating room be
improved by replacing the present wooden floor with cement, installing
additional fly-proof ventilation and limewashing the walls; 4. That the
dormitory be enlarged; 5. That the question of immunization of boys
against diphtheria be considered. In regard to immunistaion it is
pointed out that this would have to be carried out by a local
practitioner, and the cost would probably be high. Arrangements may be
made to this end in the future." Signed C. J. Watt
