vicinity of Lismore for Aboriginal children and that a teacher is to be
appointed. We have on our mission staff a young lady who was for some
years resident at Lismore. She is well acquainted with all the

10/8/1909 -- Letter from Church of Christ, Lismore Fred. T. Saunders
Evangelist -- to APB "Sir, For some few years past, the Church of
Christ, in Lismore, has conducted a mission for the Aborigines living in
the district. At the commencement of this year, on the opening of the
Reserve at Dunoon, the natives moved there, and the work of the mission
has continued there. Though a School-house, and portion of the equipment
of an up-to-date school, have been placed on the reserve so far no
teacher has been sent. The need of a teacher has been recognized by our
workers from the commencement, and it is now proposed to employ a
missionary to labour with the natives, and teacher them secular subjects
on week days as well as religious subjects on Sundays. The missionary,
who is qualified by experience for the work, will be supported by the
Church, but we desire the co-operation of the Aborigines Protection
Board to a certain extent. Will the Board allow the use of the school
building now erected, to our missionary? Will the Board provide any
equipment, that may be necessary, in the shape of stationary etc? If the
Board could do this, much of the present apparent inactivity would be
removed. We would appreciate an early reply to being work at once."

6/7/1911 -- Report of visit to Dunoon Aboriginal Reserve, Richmond River
-- In company with Senior Sergeant Lewis I visited the Aboriginal
Reserve at Dunoon on June 6^th^ and made careful inspection of all the
huts and also noted the requirements. Some of the hunts are very much
overcrowded; there has been but little system adopted in placing the
huts, it would have been much better had there been an alignment. Some
of the aborigines have been made a very good show of cultivation,
Charlie Brown has secured a total of 25 bags of Corn, some of which he
marketed and while I was on the Reserve he was preparing more for
market. My recommendations in connection with the Reserve are as follow:

That point be supplied and the residents expected to paint their
dwellings. The Local Committee might be asked to send in an estimate of
the amount of paint that would be required.

Some of the huts need additional guttering so as to ensure a good supply
of water to the tanks, a requisition should be sent in also for the
quantity of guttering required.

The suggestion for the enlargement of the schoolroom I endorse, the
present building is certainly large enough for the present weather but
in summer would prove altogether too small for the number of children
attending; there are 23 on the roll 15 were present in the school on the
occasion of my visit.

In lieu of the present arrangement for a single woman as teacher I am
strongly of opinion that in the best interest of the Station a married
couple should be appointed, the men to act as Manager and Teacher as is
the case at Edgerton and elsewhere. In connection with this matter Miss
Harding the school teacher has made application for the erection of a
dwelling on the reserve, I trust that the Board will not agree to any
such expenditure but make provisions for the erection of a cottage for a
manager and his wife. I am strongly convinced of the absolute unwisdom
of permitting single persons, either men or woman, to reside on the
reserves under the control of the Board unless there is a Manager, a
married man, also resident thereupon.

There are 3 children above school age for whom provision should be made
by securing situations for them, one of them a lad named William comet
about 15 years of age is anxious for work and his grandfather, Wadgan,
with whom he resides, is also desirous that he should get work. A girl
named Eva aged about 15 or 16 should also be in a situation. A very
smart lad named William Robertson who is attending school might well be
left a little longer at school as he is very quick at learning.

There has been but little fencing done on the Reserve though Archie
Turnbull was engaged in putting up a line of fencing while I was there.
The local Committee have been urging the people to clear a further area
of land before any allotment of definite areas has been made but the
people prefer to have the boundaries of their allotment fixed and then
they will be prepared to make provisions for the clearing of land, they
usually engage labour to do it. I certainly advise that the district
Surveyor be asked to strike the boundary line of the allotment and then
for the Board to insist that the clearing be done.

It would be a very great advantage if the rations could be supplied on
the Station, this of course can only be done having a resident manager.

An Indian known as New Chum or Keebican is residing with a young woman
named Sarah King who has two children. This man should certainly not be
allowed to live upon the Station. He is a strong able bodied man and
should be well able to support the woman if he marries her.

One of the residents George Williams a good bullock driver was met upon
the road between Lismore and Dunoon drawing logs to the timber mill, it
appears that some time since (it is stated by Mr. Garvin member of the
Board) authority was given to this man to draw some logs to be given in
exchange for timber which was to be utilized in additions to his house,
I judge, however, he has continued to fell the timber and I think that
some of the best timber on the Reserve and to dispose of it. A report
might be asked for in the matter as if the timber is to be sold the
Board should certainly have the benefit of it for general purposes,
moreover, it is a question as to whether we have a right without the
permission of the Forestry Dept., to dispose of the timber as is being
done.

There are at present no W.C's on the Station except these connected with
the schools, certainly at least half a dozen should be erected. The
Local Board might be asked for a report in this matter.

Letter from the New South Wales Churches of Christ Foreign Missions
Committee -- 30/9/1914 -- to the Secretary of APB -- "Dear Sir, On
behalf of the above committee, I would like to direct the attention of
the Board to the present state of affairs on the Aborigines Reserve, at
Dunoon, near Lismore. The late teacher of the School on the Reserve has
recently been awarded a scholarship tenable at Herefore House, and has
left the school to enter upon her studies. This has left the School
without a teacher. It occurs to us that this would be a suitable time
for the Aborigines Protection Board to give further consideration to the
question of appointing a Manager on this Reserve, in order to enable the
area of the Reserve to be properly cultivated, and that the Natives
should be taught useful crafts. We do not need to remind the Board, that
on the previous occasions on which this matter has been considered two
considerations have entered and been considered as barriers. The first,
the position of Miss. Harding, the teacher at the School, and the second
that of finance. The first barrier has been removed, though it is just
to Miss Harding to state that for some time she has not only been
willing, but anxious, that a manager should be appointed, and action is
entering for the scholarship which she has since won, was with the
object of removing herself from the way of a manger being appointed. The
second consideration, finance, we presume to be a difficulty still.
Recently in an interview with Mr. Beardsmore, the suggestion was made
that, on account of our interest in the spiritual welfare of the natives
at this Reserve, this Committee might be able to undertake the erection
of a residence for a Manager. This matter has been under the
consideration of our Committee, but owing to our Foreign Missions work
being on a Federal basis, and the present executive located in
Melbourne, some little delay has been occasioned in arriving at a
satisfactory conclusion. But, had not Miss Harding secured this
scholarship, and thus created the vacancy at the school on the Reserve,
we anticipate that we would have approached the Board at an early date
with a definite proposition on this matter. However, it might not be out
of place to state that the church at Lismore has pledged itself to raise
£70 towards the cost of such a building, and it is extremely probable
that we will be able to guarantee the balance of the cost. We have to
wait for further authority before we can make such a definite pledge
however. Should we erect such a building, it would be on the
understanding that a manager would be appointed who would be able to
care for our interests in addition to those of the Board. We understand
also, that should a Manager capable of doing this not be available, that
permission would be granted to us to either remove or sell this
building. In our previous communications on this matter, we mentioned
Mr. A. E. Terry, formerly on the Macleay River, as one who, having had
experience of the work to be done, and also possessing the confidence of
this committee, might be considered as a possible manager for this
Reserve. I understand that Mr. Terry is willing to accept the vacant
position of teacher, should the Board be willing to consider him
suitable for the position, or if preferred by your Board to appoint him
as Manager, to accept that position. I mention this in order to show
that the way is clear for any action your Board could propose to take.
If it is impossible to appoint a Manager at once, I would like to
suggest as a course of action, that if approval is given to Mr. Terry,
that he be appointed as teacher now, and then later be clothed with the
powers of Manager when it is found convenient to make that appointment.
Thanking you in anticipation of your early consideration of this
matter." Signed Fred Sauders Secretary

16/9/1929 -- Memo from Dept. Education to Chief Inspector Lismore -- "I
recently wrote to advise you that the settlement at Dunoon Road was
practically deserted. I understand that the huts are condemned, and from
the attached newspaper clipping, you will note that efforts are being
made to secure a suitable site near Lismore. I have interviewed the
Inspector of Police, who informs me that he hopes to be successful. When
Mr. Smith reported for duty, I endeavored to rent a hall belonging to
the Anglican Church, situated at North Lismore, near the abode most o
the aboriginals, as temporary School room. The hall was not suitable.
Later Mr. Smith, the newly appointed teacher secured a covered in shed
as temporary quarters, and commenced operations forthwith. I had the
furniture and equipment conveyed from the school at Dunoon Road to this
temporary abode. The present quarters are unsuitable as a permanent
school, but are the only ones available at present. The building is
fairly will ventilated, has an iron roof and a concrete floor, also
sanitary conveniences. The average attendance at Dunoon Road was about
ten (10). At present there are eighteen (18) in attendance."

Transcript of newspaper clipping in the 'Star' news 4/9/1929 --
"Aborigines Lismore School -- Recently the Lismore Inspector of Police
(Mr. C. E. Craddock) asked the council if there was any objection to an
aborigines' school being erected near the Robert White bridge in North
Lismore on the banks of Leycester Creek. The matter was again discussed
by the council on Monday night. Ald. S. R. Arthur told the council that
the place where the aborigines were living at North Lismore was quite
unsuitable, and the blacks often lived in a house till vermin hunter
them out and then secured another. He thought that the council should
find a suitable place for them, and suggested a reserve on the Wyralla
Road, opposite Mr. Pollack's farm. Ald. F. L Hacking counseled the
alderman showing a little more humanity toward the original owners of
Australia, from whom the whites had taken the country and everything
else they owned. "Don't treat it as a joke" he said "it is a serious
matter". Ald. D. McA Ross said that he council was in duty bound to do
something for the unfortunate people. Ald S. J. Hosie said that every
alderman had shown anxiety to put the aborigines "where the other fellow
lived" and away from himself. Ald. R. White: Except myself. Continuing,
Ald. Hosie agreed to accept Ald. White from his remarks and moved that
the council agree to the suggested site. It must be understood that the
land was to be used for a school and not for a settlement. Ald. W. Frith
disclaimed any intention of treating the matter jocularly and pointed
out that the council had previously rejected a proposal to establish a
settlement where it was now proposed to erect a school. He contended
that in the place where the children were the parents would be, and
authorization of the school would be the thin edge of the wedge for a
settlement. He had noticed that a number of residents had taken it upon
themselves to criticize the alderman for the opinions expressed but he
was prepared to adhere to his previous contention that, though he had
nothing against the establishment of a school at a suitable place there
would not be a settlement inside the municipality. Ald. Caterson said
that a quarter of a mile further from Lismore than the site suggested
was an ideal position for the blacks. Ald. Arthur proposed that Alderman
R. Kellas, W. Caterson, R. White, and himself be appointed a committee
to select a site and this was agreed by the council."

3/7/1925 -- Dept Education -- This School was burnt down on 16^th^
January last. This information was conveyed in a memorandum from the
Secretary, Aborigines Protection Board, who also intimated that, if
possible, temporary arrangements would be made to carry on the school.
No further advise from the A.P Board was received until 20^th^ May last,
when the Secretary stated that the new building was ready for occupation
and asking that Miss Smith, who he understood was residing at her
parents at Pilliga, be instructed to resume duty. All this time Miss
Smith had been receiving pay as if she were duty at Dunoon, and no word
whatsoever was received from her that she was not teaching. She was
therefore asked to refund the amount of £64/12/3d for the period from
27^th^ January to 14^th^ May, and no further payment made to her. She
was told that consideration would be given to any scheme for repayment
by instalments. In reply, she explained that when she returned to
Dunoon, the Police informed her that they had received word that the
school would be rebuilt within six weeks and asked her to find a
temporary building. This she failed to do so. She was then informed, she
states, that an effort was to be made to provide a Marquee. During this
time she and the children were working in the Garden, expecting each day
that school would continue. Apart from any necessity of the AP Board to
advise this Department of the failure to carry on the school, Miss
Smith's duty was to ask for instructions. She has now been paid salary
as from 1^st^ July, and has been asked to supply further particulars of
her movements. When her reply comes to hand it may then be possible to
say what amount, if any, she should refund. She should certainly be paid
for the period during which she was standing at Dunoon."

Reply from APB concerning this matter. -- In reply to the above I beg to
state concerning Miss Smith's letter dated the 16^th^ instant, that her
statement that she did not receive instructions from my Board that her
services would not be needed is quite correct, as it was assumed that
such information would be conveyed by your Department to her, upon
receipt of the advice that the School has been destroyed by fire. Miss
Smith is also correct in her statement that the School was to be rebuilt
in six weeks, and that efforts were made to secure a temporary building.
The contractor for the erection of the new building anticipated its
completion within six weeks but unavoidable delays occurred and
prevented the completion within the period. Under all the circumstances
it is thought that consideration might be given to the matter of Miss
Smith being allowed payment for the period during which she was waiting
the re-erection of the School. I might state that Miss Smith was not an

**Community**

Report re Aborigines Camp at Dunoon on the 22 September 1908 -- Total of
22 people on reserve. Those on the Reserve are in receipt of rations,
they are employed clearing the timber off the reserve. Since the last
report the children started to go to School, but the Parents of the
other children objected to them going.

Police Department -- Report re Aborigines Camp at Dunoon on the 31^st^
March 1912 --

Form completed with the following headings.

Date of Inspection -- 25 March 1912; Previous Inspection -- 11/11/10;
Situation of Camp -- Dunoon Six Miles from Lismore; Adults Full Bloods
Men 10 Women 5 Total 15; Adults Half Castes -- Men 6 Women 7 Total 13;
Children Full-bloods -- Boys 6 Girls 4 Total 10; Children Half-castes
Boys 10 Girls 14 Total 24.

Particulars of Reserve -- AR No. 35824 Dunoon 480 acres notified
23/5/03. Improvements on Reserve -- There are 12 huts (2 rooms) for the
use of the Aborigines, also school, about 1/2 mile of the frontage is
fenced

Means of Subsistence -- The improvement to this reserve by way of
clearing is being done by the Aborigines and those engaged are in
receipt of government rations.

School attendance -- Number of Children of school age -- Boys 10 Girls
12 Total 22 Average daily attendance -- Boys 6 Girls 9 Total 15

Remarks as to Health of Aborigines -- Good.

General remarks and recommendations -- The only thing that appears to be
necessary is the enlargement of the school. The proposal to erect a
Managers residence and place of a competent person in charge appears to
be very necessary.

APB noted that unfortunately there are no funds available for the
erection of a Managers Residence.
