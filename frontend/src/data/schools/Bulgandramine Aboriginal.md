26/8/1912 -- Subject: The Aboriginals at Bulgandramine Mission Camp
applying for a Public School to be built - Police Station Tomingley.
"Constable Hannaford No.6391 reports that, the Aborigines at the above
camp, have asked the Constable to make an application to the Aborigines
Protection Board, to build them a school at the Aborigines Bulgandramine
Mission Camp. There are at present 18 children under 14 years and able
to attend school, residing in that locality, Nine children are residing
outside the Camp, the parents being unable to reside inside on account
of the Mothers (Aboriginals) living with white men. There is at present
a young man (in connection with the Church of England at Dubbo) pays a
visit to this Camp once in every 8 weeks, and remains about 14 days, and
while he is there, he gives the Aboriginal children as much education as
possible, but they have to be taught out in the open air, as this young
man could not be expected to teach them in the Aborigines huts. The
Constable cannot obtain tenders for this work at Tomingley, no doubt
tenders could be obtained at Peak Hill, and the distance from Peak Hill
will be about the same distance as here. " -- Reply from the Sub
Inspector of Police Dubbo -- "As much information should be given as
possible. How far is the Camp from Tomingley, and Peak Hill. The age,
sex, and caste of all children and name. A full description of all
improvements on the Mission. How is each hut occupied. How far do the
children, who would attend the school, reside off the Mission. What
rations etc, are issued, and to whom. How far is the nearest Public
School distant. Are these people like to remain in the district. Are any
of the Aboriginal women married to the white men referred to? Any other
information likely to be of interest to the A.P Board.

9/10/1915 -- Memo Department of Education -- Re Geraldine O'Neill
Teacher -- Reason for Absence from duty. "I have the honor to state that
on the night of the 12^th^ of last month, I was warned that a man of
unsound mind, and in a rude condition had after leaving the
Bulgandramine wool shed gone as far as the Aborigines Reserve, upon
which my school is built, where he evidently intended to camp for the
night. The police had been telephoned, but had not yet arrived and did
not intend to that night. I had for the past week been walking to school
a distance of five miles, along a very lonely road. It was not safe for
me to go on that morning as I may have met the lunatic. I beg to say
that I was absolutely afraid to go especially as it was sometimes late
when I arrived back from school, and the presence of the lunatic made me
realize the possibility of other dangerous persons being about. I could
not have gone to school under such conditions. In the meantime prior to
making the matter known to the Department, I decided to notify the Local
Board. Upon my doing so, the Secretary of the Board procured a horse for
me which I rode the next-day, when the lunatic had gone; the man was a
few days afterwards secured by the police."

**Other comments**

Complaint regarding the non-enrolment of Benjamin Towney

"Mr. H.O Elliot has made representations to the Minister regarding the
question of enrolment of Benjamin Towney at the Bulgandramine Aboriginal
School.

A report submitted by the Manager of the School indicates that the lad's
father and mother were expelled from the Aboriginal Station. It is his
opinion that the child's attendance would complicate matters to the
extent that so long as her son remained at the school Mrs. Towney would
in all probability continue to defy the Board's authority. A statement
in the report indicates that both the parents are quarter-caste
Aboriginals. If this assertion is correct, the A.P. Board has no control
over the Towney family, owing to the fact that they should never have
been permitted to reside on the reserve.

Mt. Pettit, Secretary of the A.P. Board advised personally that he was
always under the impression that the Towney family were half-castes, but
the teacher of the school is a very competent officer and it is very
possible that the assertion made by him is correct.

Mr. Inspector Griffiths supports the remarks made by the teacher and
suggests that there does not appear to be any reason why the lad should
not continue to attend St. Joseph's Peak Hill. He recommends that the
child be not enrolled at Bulgandramine until final action is taken by
the Board or that the parents are removed.

If Mr. and Mrs. Towney are quarter-caste Aboriginals the Board cannot
remove them, other than from the actual Reserve.

It does not seem fair to the child that he should be penalized for any
wrong doings of the father and mother.

Mr. Petit expresses his opinion verbally that the Department should
sanction the enrolment of the lad in the school, without prejudice to
any subsequent action that the Board might take. This suggestion is now
submitted for your approval"

Further correspondence -- 17/5/1937 -- Minister for Education Mr. D. H.
Drummond to H.O Eliiot, Esq, M.L.A, Parliament House Sydney -- "Dear Mr.
Elliot, Referring to your personal representations on behalf of Mrs.
Towney regarding the enrolment of her son, Benjamin, at the
Bulgandramine Aboriginal School, I desire to inform you that in the
circumstances I have approved of this youth attending the school in

Full report from the Inspector of Schools Forbes concerning Mrs. W.
Towney's Complaint Re My Refusal to Admit her son to the School.

"I beg to submit a full report regarding the above complaint. Mrs.
Towney's husband, William Towney, was expelled from this Station on
1^st^ December 1933, for insolence to the Manager, and defiance of the
Board's authority. Mrs. Towney herself was expelled from the Station on
12.2.1934 for defiance of the Board's authority. The child, Benjamin is
ten years old and has not been guilty of any breach of discipline. On
May 29^th^ 1936 Peak Hill police visited this Station and ordered Towney
and his wife to leave and not to return to any place within five miles
of the Station. This action was on authority from the Board, and it was
then that I told Mrs. Towney her son would be refused admittance to this
School. Shortly afterwards, the child was taken to Peak Hill, where he
attended the Convent School. This family has, on several occasions, been
ordered to leave their place of abode, but defiantly informed the
Police, that they would not leave under any consideration. Their case is
at present receiving attention by the Board and it is thought, that the
Police Court action, will be taken. The Child's attendance would
complicate matters to the extent that so long as her son remained at
this school, she would, in all probability continue to defy the Boards
authority. Both parents are quarter caste Aboriginals. Regarding the
child, I am of the opinion that he would be amendable to school
discipline, if he were again admitted, but in the event of this being
allowed, Mrs. Towney would hold this as an excuse to remain here. It is
owing to her very bad character and behavior, that the Board desire to
enforce the expulsion order against them. She has been, on several
occasions, responsible for a good deal of unrest, generally, among the
rest of the Aborigines. As the family have been expelled by the previous
Manager of this Station I am not responsible for the expulsion, but, it
is my duty to carry out the Board's instructions in the matter of not
allowing an expelled person to return. Mrs. Towney states that she would
not live on any of the Boards Reserves or Stations. If she bears any
malice against me, it is for other reasons concerning the management of
the Station, but I am aware that the fact that her husband was expelled
does not in any way reflect on me. I approach her on what was meant by
"Autocratic behavior" and she said she had no idea of what it meant, and
added that the person who wrote the letter for her, must have included
those words. She also informed me that the "colour line" had been drawn
at the Public School at Peak Hill, but she had never asked for her child
to be admitted to Mr. Johnson's or Mr. Yeo's schools, which are close to
this Station. I would recommend that this child be allowed to attend
this school only on condition that the parents be removed. Mrs. Towney
stated that she would not live on an Aboriginal Reserve again as her
husband can obtain food relief anywhere and she can live without
assistance from the Board. This being so, why does she expect the Board
to educate her child? I am of the opinion that her sole purpose for
making this complaint is to be allowed to remain in her present place of

March 1933 -- Teacher Peak Hill school. "Sir, In reply to your letter --
I beg to inform you that, never at any time, have I had anyone of
dark-blood apply for enrolment at the above school. I can definitely say
that Mrs. Towneys statement that I wouldn't admit her boy is untrue,
because the boy has never applied for enrolment this year or at any
time. In fact the family are unknown to me.

Letter from Mrs. Towney to Education Department. "1 March 1937 -- "Dear
Sir, I will be extremely grateful to you if you will take up my case
with the Education Department at your early convenience. My husband,
self and family resided for three years on the Aboriginal Reserve and
because my husband refused "green" timber on a government reserve, we
were expelled. We are not living about 1/2 a mile from the reserve. My
son aged 10 is refused admission to the school by the teacher and there
is not other school within 6 miles and they refuse him admission because
of the "Color" line. It is a great pity for a boy to grow up ignorant
and does not give him a fair chance competing for a living later in life
because of the autocratic behavior of the teacher. Thanking you in
anticipation. Mrs. W. Towney.
