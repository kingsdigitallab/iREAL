**Role of the Department and relationship to the APB**

26/2/1934- Letter to APB from Margaret Kinnear Teacher Aboriginal School
Moree -- "Dear Sir, I have to advise you that at present some aboriginal
children are attending the Public School in Moree. IN consequence of
this the regular attendance at this school is impaired, as the others
consider that if some are allowed to attend the Public School in Moree,
that they too, should be admitted there also. I am not in receipt of
communication in reference to the exclusion of Aboriginal children from
the Public School Moree, but understand that these children were to be
excluded from attending that school. The children of Jim Cutmore, Bert
Draper, Percy Duncan, Arthur Duncan, Mitchell, Daniels, Talbett &
Haines, have not yet arrived at this school, some are in attendance at
the Public School, but the majority are not attending school anywhere.
Will you please advise me what action I am to take in this matter."

1/3/1934 -- APB to Dept. Education Subject "Non -- attendance of
Aboriginal Children at Aborigines School Recently Opened at Moree -- The
attached report by the Teacher of the recently opened Aborigines School
at Moree is forwarded for information and such action as may be deemed
desirable. In view of the fact that this school was specially built and
opened for the benefit of the Aboriginal children, perhaps steps could
be taken to ensure the attendance of all those for whom it was designed
to cater for." A.C Pettit. Note to Chief Inspector "the H.M. Moree has
reported at some length. Camp black should be required to attend the
aborigines school. To ensure this the matter should be handled by the
attendance officer after having interviewed Miss Kinner and Miss
Jennings.

1/3/1934 -- Memo from Dept. Education -- Non receipt of furniture for
Aborigines School at Moree -- I beg to report that the furniture for the
newly constructed Aborigines School at Moree has not arrived and the
teacher -- Miss Kinnear -- is carrying on under great difficulties. The
Aborigines are also making their absence of furniture an excuse for not
sending their children to school."

15/3/1934 -- Letter to the Secretary APB from the Town Clerks Office

the Aborigines School, Moree, for the convenience of the teacher and
children attending the school. It is understood that the school is bare
of any furniture or seats, which state of affairs is considered

attention."

19/3/1934 -- Letter from Intermediate High School, Moree to L.F. Keller,
M.A. Inspector of Schools, Inverell -- "Dear Sir, Two children of the
families named in Miss Kinnear's letter have attended here this year.
They are William Talbot and Rita Duncan. Talbot, aboriginal, is 10 years
of age. His attendance is very irregular. I believe he assists his
father droving. He is living at the public camping reserve. Rita Duncan,
aged 13, is a white child. Her father is Percy Duncan living in the main
Abor camp. When the order for exclusion was made last year her name was
not included. I do not think she was then living at the camp. Another
boy, Noel Graham, 10 years, aboriginal, is also in attendance. He was
not excluded last year because he was then living in a house in the
town. He has since moved to a camp near the Yarraman Crossing. Since the
last instructions, viz, to re-admit the aboriginal children who had
previously been excluded, no action regarding their attendance has been
taken. The feeling referred to by Miss Kinnear, - "That if some are
allowed to attend the Public School in Moree, " then others should be
admitted also, - is to be expected. The children excluded last year were
strictly camp children. Other aboriginal children attending the school
from abodes in the town or elsewhere were not mentioned. But to maintain
this distinction will be difficult. Some of them change their abodes
living sometimes in the camp and sometimes in a home "off" some road or
street in the town. Two other families, Jack Grose, 10, Jean Grose 12,
Valmai Grose 6, Darcy Grose 8 (Father Ernest Grose, Warialda Rd). and
Eric Seymour 9, Clifford Seymour 13 and Claude Seymour 12 -- Father
"Bert Seymour" Mackenzie St are aboriginal and in attendance here, and
it is quite possible that their attendance is being resented by some of
those excluded. These however could hardly be classed as camp blacks. As
far as I know they have never lived in the camp, but always at their
present residences in the town. Such is not the case with Talbot and
Graham. Rita Duncan presents a difficult problem. I have taken it upon
myself to urge upon Mrs. Duncan unofficially the undesirability of
bringing her up in the camp. She agrees and is definite that she will
not stay in the camp a moment longer than she can help and hopes to move
to another place soon. Possibly some official advice on these lines
might assist. I should say that this girls attendance at the Aborigines'
School (she is commencing 1^st^ year work this year) would not be
desirable. That of Talbot and Graham would be, and an instruction to the
parents that these children should attend at the school provided appears
necessary." Signed R. B. Jennings.

6/4/1934 -- Memo from Dept. Education re Moree Aborigines' -- Non
attendance of Aboriginal Children -- "Moree Aborigines' School was
established recently following complaints by the local white residents.
The teacher recently reported that some of the dark children were still
attending the Public School. This was having an effect upon the parents
of the children who were enrolled at the aborigines' school. Some
children were not attending either school. Miss Kinnear named the
following families in offending in the manners mentioned: - Jim Cutmore,
Bert Draper, Percy Duncan, Arthur Duncan, Mitchell, Daniels, Talbett,
and Haines. The Headmaster of the Public School in his report states
that from the families named William Talbot and Rita Duncan have
attended this year. William Talbot is an aborigine, 10 years of age. His
attendance is irregular. The Headmaster understands he assists his
father droving. Rita Duncan, aged 13, is a white child whose father is
living at the main aborigines' camp. Noel Graham, aborigine, aged 10
years, is also attending the Public School. This boy and Rita Duncan
were not excluded last year when the order was given; the boy because he
was living in town, and the girl because of her colour. (MR. Jennings
understands she was not then living at the camp.) Noel Graham is not
living in a camp near Yarraman Crossing. The children of Ernest Grose
(4) and Bert Seymour (3) are other aborigines in attendance at the
Public School. They reside in the town, however, and cannot be classed
as camp blacks. As far as the Headmaster knows these families have never
lived in the camp. William Talbot and Noel Graham could be expected to
attend the aborigines' school. But this can hardly be expected of Rita
Duncan, although, of course, she could be excluded on other grounds. The
Headmaster has already impressed her mother with the undesirability of
bringing her up in the camp. Mrs. Duncan has expressed her intention of
moving to a better place and hopes to do so soon. The Grose and Seymour
families present a problem. The attendance of these children is probably
the cause of the dissatisfaction that has manifested itself. If Talbot
and Graham only were excluded the position may be accentuated. And yet
Grose and Seymour are evidently superior to the remainder of their
kinsmen. The attendance of these children would probably be accepted by
the white parents. At this juncture I RECOMMEND that the Headmaster of
the Public School be instructed to exclude William Talbot and Noel
Graham. That the case of Rita Duncan and the non-attendance of the
children of Jim Cutmore, Bert Draper, Arthur Duncan, Mitchell, Daniels,
and Haines, be referred to the Child Welfare Department for necessary
action. That the Children Welfare Department be asked to report on the
home conditions of the Grose and Seymour families.

17/4//1934 -- "APB Moree -- Supply of Furniture -- I wish to advise you
that the furniture for the Aborigines' School at Moree was dispatched on
20^th^ ultimo and the Town Clerk was notified accordingly."

23/4/1934 -- Letter to The Secretary Child Welfare Department, Sydney
from Under Secretary Dept. Education re Non-attendance of aboriginal
children at the School for Aborigines recently opened at Moree. "Some
trouble has been experienced recently in connection with the
non-attendance of aboriginal children at the above school. Some still
continue to attend the Moree Public School in opposition to the
Department's instructions, while some do not attend school at all. IT is
desired that you will particularly look into the case of Rita Duncan and
the children of Jim Cutmore, Bert Draper, Arthur Duncan, Mitchell,
Daniels and Haines. I should also like to be furnished with a report on
the home conditions of the Grose and Seymour families. Your Inspector
should in the first place interview the Headmaster, Mr. Jennings, of the
matter. Miss Margaret Kinnear, teacher of the Aborigines School, should
also be helpful in acquainting your Inspector with the position."

101/10/1934 -- Memo Dept. Education -- Moree Aborigines: Non- attendance
of Children -- "Aboriginal children were recently excluded from Moree
Public School, a separate school having been provided for their benefit.
The unsatisfactory attendance of the children of Messrs Jim Cutmore,
Bert Draper, Arthur Duncan, Mitchell, Daniels and Haines, at the
Aboriginal School was referred to the Child Welfare Department. Sickness
was the excuse given to the Child Welfare Inspector by all the parents.
Mr. Chambers doubts its validity but regular attendance in the future
was promised. The children of Messrs Grose and Seymour were exempted
from the exclusion pending a report on the home conditions of the
families. The Grose family reside in their own cottage on the bank of
the Mehi River. The father is coloured but has a light skin. The family
does not associate with the aborigines. The children are still attending
the Public School. The Seymour family is probably quarter caste, by they
keep aloof from the aborigines generally. As the positions is settled,
and the white parents have not objected to the attendance of the Grose
and Seymour children, it is RECOMMENDED that no further action be taken,
at present, respecting their attendance at the Public School; that the
school attendance reports be returned to the Child Welfare Department."

7/9/1934 -- Re School Attendance "When I visited the School in March
last, and also called on several Aboriginal families, I found that the
local Aboriginals had the idea that an invidious distinction was being
made against them, on account of racial prejudice by the provision of a
separate school. I endevoured to remedy this idea, and inculeate the
feeling that a benefit had been conferred by the institution of a
special school intended to meet the requirements of their own race, and
to help their children in their own particular problems. When I again
visited on 16^th^ inst, I found that the children had all settled down
comfortably and are rather proud of their school. I interviewed Mr.
Jenning the Headmaster of Moree H. School and Miss Kinnear Teacher of
the School for Aborigines in march last when at Moree and again last
month to familarise myself with the requirement in studying the
position. The attached sheet shows the visits I made last month in
regard to unsatisfactory attendances and as to the cases mentioned in
Memo of 23^rd^ April last. In regard to the Grose family I found that
they reside on the bank of the Mehi River in their own cottage, not near
either of the Aboriginal camps. Mr. Grose is coloured but has a light
skin. His family does not associate with the Aborigines, and the
children attend at Public School Moree ....(?) 13, Jack 11, Darcy 8, and
Valma 7. Ronald 5 is under school age. The Seymour family is colored but
they reside in the town of Moree. There are probably quarter castes but
keep aloof from the other Aborigines generally. There is difficulty at
times in drawing a line of demarcation." (Note to Wendy will send on
sheet with visits to school with comments made by inspector)

12/3/1935 -- Visit of Inspector -- Chief Inspector of Schools Sydney --
"Dear Sir, I have heard that you will probably make a tour of Aborigines
Schools and I would like the school under my charge included in your
inspection. The school is within reach from the Moree Railway Station,

9/3/1937 -- Memo from M. Kinnear Teacher in Charge Aborigines School

> *Experience*

4/8/1937 -- Letter to Miss Kinnear Teacher Aborigines School Moree from
Chief Inspector re Efficiency Award -- "Having considered the
representation contained in your letter of the 25^th^ ultimo, and having
reviewed the reports upon your work, I am not prepared at present to
increase the award for your efficiency. The Department appreciates your
industry, devotion to duty, and interest in the children and in the
service which you are rendering to the community but it is felt that you
could with profit arrange with the District Inspector to do some
observational work at the Moree Public School along lines that would be
of interest to you, especially in the teaching of reading and number to
infants and in handwork for all classes."

25/6/1937 -- Letter from teacher Margaret Kinnear re Efficiency mark --
"Dear Sir, May I respectfully ask you if it is possible for me, in my
position of Aborigines School Teacher, to gain a higher mark than my
present one, which is B. In reports on my work at Nowendoc, it was
mentioned that the pupils were above the standards for Aborigines'
Schools. One pupil under my tuition at Nowendoc in 1933, passed the
P.E.E. I still take, as I have always taken, the keenest interest in my
work, and during the last yearly inspection, my Inspector informed me
that this school is in advance of other Aborigines Schools. If a higher
award can be gained I will be glad to know exactly what should be don't
to reach it. A great deal of hard and persistent work has been done by
me to bring the pupils in the different schools I have controlled to the
various successes gained."

12/7/1937 -- memo from N.W.Drummond to Chief Inspector re Moree
Aborigines School: Miss Kinnear's Efficiency Mark -- "Miss Kinnear is
very earnest and devoted to her school and the welfare of her pupils.
She is, however, not above average in teaching skill. I have encouraged
her to work on each occasion that I have visited her school. I would
recommend that she be sent an encouraging letter containing some
appreciation of her industry, her devotion to duty, and her interest in
the children and in the service which she is rendering to the community.
At the same time she can be advised to arrange with the District
Inspector to do some observational work at the Moree Public School along
lines that would be of interest to her, especially in the teaching of
reading and number to infants, and in handwork for all classes. I do not
recommend a change in the award for this year."
