5/8/1937 -- "The teacher in Charge of the Summer Vale Aboriginal School
has reported that following upon a visit to his school by an officer of
the Aborigines Protection Board that there was no attendance at school
upon two consecutive days of nine children who reside on the Reserve. He
states that the absence of the children was due, as far as he could
ascertain, to the fact that certain of the children might be taken from
the school by the Aborigines Protection Board officer without the
knowledge of consent of the parents, and removed to an institution. He
asked for advise as to whether he should surrender any child, except of
course, on the production of a Court Order, by an Officer of the
Aborigines' Protection Board. The Secretary of the Aborigines Protection
Board was consulted by telephone in this matter and he advise that,
following the visit of Messers Smithers and Jacobs, one family by the
name of Archibald transferred to Burnt Bridge on their own volition. The
officers mentioned reported that the children of the family named Naylor
required medical attention. The parents were informed that if they were
removed to Burnt Bridge it would be possible for them to obtain medical
attention at Kempsey Hospital, and they would have frequent access to
their children. The parents refused to remove from Summer Vale. It is
not possible for them to obtain medical attention at the Walcha Hospital
as the local Doctor refuses to treat the children. The officers
recommended that proceedings be taken in view to their being committed
to an institution where they would receive the proper treatment. This
matter, it is understood is to be considered at the next meeting of the
Aborigines' Protection Board. Mr. Pettit advised that under the Act the
Board has power to remove children residing on reserves without the
consent of the parents, but it is not the policy of the Board to take
action in this regard; proceedings are usually taken through the Court
so that the parents may make the necessary appeal. He informed me that
the teacher can be assured that there is no necessity for him to worry
in regard to any officer of the Board taking the children from his
school unless the necessary Court Order has been obtained. It is,
therefore, recommended that the teacher be informed accordingly, and at
the same time it is suggested that the Secretary of the board be asked
if he will be so good as to furnish this Department with a copy of the
report furnished to him upon this question, especially with regard to
the matters brought under notice by Mr. Inspector Wilcox.

Letter from School Teacher regarding this matter -- 16/7/1937 -- "Dear
Sir, Following upon a visit to this School and the adjacent Aborigines
Reserve of an officer of the Aborigines Protection Board last Wednesday
afternoon (7^th^ instant) there was no attendance at school on the two
succeeding days of the children (9 in all) of three families resident on
the Reserve. The absence of children in question was due, as far as I
can ascertain, to the fear that certain of the children might be taken
from the School by an AP Board officer, without the knowledge or consent
of parents and removed to an institution. The first intention of the
parents concerned was to refrain indefinitely from sending their
children to school; however, they have been made aware of the serious
possibilities of such action, and attendance at school today is normal.
Nevertheless, the fear is still in the minds of the parents and
children; and in the event of a person coming to this school with the
purpose that is feared I am doubtful as to whether or not I should
surrender any child, except of course, on the production of a Court
Order. Advise on this point would be appreciated.

6/8/1936 -- Memo re unsatisfactory school site -- Memo from temporary
teacher Donald William Bailey -- "In face of the facts and
considerations detailed hereunder, it appears to me advisable to suggest
that this school should be removed to a more satisfactory site. Whether
this matter is more the concern of the Aborigines Protection Board than
of the Department of Education I do not know, but I propose also to make
representations also to that body. 1) The existing school is situated a
quarter of a mile from the nearest boundary of the Reserve on which all
the pupils live, hence all the children travel each day distances
varying from one mile to four miles. Largely because of the distances
they are obliged to walk, the five children of one family in particular
are almost invariably late twice each day. (2) The road traveled by the
children is one which carries up to forty motor vehicles per day between
the hours of 9 am, and 4 pm, and the presence of children on the road is
a source of danger to themselves and annoyance in various ways to
drivers. (3) the children on their way to school and from school
frequently behave in such a way as to cause annoyance to the occupants
of two residences adjacent to the school. (4) it has happened on
occasions that the OHIO CREEK has risen, submerging a low-level bridge,
and has prevented, or made dangerous, the traveling of the children to
school. (5) The present school site is most unsuitable for gardening,
which it is intended should be one of the foremost activities of an
Aborigines School. (6) the present building is in need of renovation,
and will, if the recently enacted amendment to Aborigines Protection
Legislation has the effect I anticipate, be too small to meet the
requirements of an enlarged aboriginal community. My suggestion is that
a new, and larger school be erected on the Summer Vale Aborigines
Reserve, in a more or less central situation, and where the soil would
be suitable for undertaking serious, large-scale school agricultural
projects which would be of material benefit to the community in general.
Further, I would suggest that provision might be made for the use of the
school building or part thereof, for religious and other social
purposes. A school in such a situation as that which I suggest might be
made an object of real interest to the community surrounding it, and
would bring the teacher into a position where he or she could exert a
wider and stronger influence that is at present possible. And in this
connection I might mention that this particular community is without the
services of a resident AP Board staff. A sketch of the present school
locality is appended as an aid to the consideration of my
representations."

Considered and not approved.

6/5/1937 -- Summer Vale Aborigines School -- Unsatisfactory conditions
-- Conditions at the Aborigines' School at Summer Vale, Walcha, are very
unsatisfactory -- (1) the building used is an old Public School
building. It provides none of the facilities required for the special
work of an Aborigines School [a.] the grounds are not
fenced. Cattle and Sheep roam at will in the grounds so that no
gardening work is possible. This should form quite a large part of the
work at the school. [b]. There are no benches or tools of
any sort so that woodwork is out of the question. This side of the
pupils education is being absolutely neglected. [C]. Sewing
is taught by the teacher's wife and good progress is being made. No
provision has been made for the other Domestic work set down in the
syllabus for Aborigines' Schools [d]. The school is some
distance from the reserve so that it cannot be a centre for community
life as it should be. I regard the above matters seriously since they
are preventing effective work of the sort that should be done by an
Aborigines' School. 2) This place is not properly organized as an
Aborigines' Station at all. The teacher is appointed merely to teach. He
has no authority outside of the school. He cannot control the reserve in
any way. He does not live on the reserve. There is no manger of the
reserve at all. The police issue rations but exercise little if any
supervision over the place. The Board has apparently provided the
reserve but has made no provision for carrying it on. I would recommend
that a manager be appointed and that a residence by provided for him on
the reserve. Other facilities usually provided for Aborigines' Stations
should be provided here. The present teacher seems to me to have the
qualifications necessary for manager. (3) the health conditions are
appalling. The head of one of the children is in a shocking state of
ringworms. The teacher is doing what he can to remedy the condition,
with material supplied by the board. The child is still attending school
since the teacher says the source of the infection is the home and
exclusion would serve no useful purpose. The matter has been reported to
the Board by no effective action has been taken. Several other members
of the family, I believe, suffering similarly. Malnutrition is quite
evident. One child at least seems to be suffering from rickets. Two
children have what appear to be corneal ulcers. One is a very bad case.
There are discharges from the eyes and noses of several pupils. The
teacher has reported these or similar matters to the Board. The
Government Medical Officer at Walcha apparently does not wish to have
anything to do with the aborigines. The conditions indicated should not
be allowed to continue. I would recommend that a medical inspection of
the place be made, and that effective means be taken to deal with the
situation. I do not know whether the Board realizes the state of neglect
of this place. Apparently previous reports have not carried conviction.
I am not prepared to allow the present conditions to continue without a
very definite protest. For the good name of the Board and the
Department, immediate action should be taken in this matter." Inspector
Wilcox

15/7/1937 -- Subject Condition of Aborigines at Walcha Reserve --
Attached School Report -- "As requested in your Memo of the 5^th^
instant (Attached) I visited the Walcha Reserve on the 7^th^ instant. I
went up from Kempsey in the Boards Truck accompanied by Mr Jacobs and
the Manager. On arrival at Walcha I called on the Police and discussed
the matters mentioned in Inspector Wilcox Report, in their order.
Paragraph (1) states that the School grounds are not fenced and that as
a result cattle and sheep roam at will. My reply to that is that the
Grounds have been well fenced and unless the School Gates are left open
it would be impossible for Stock to enter. Fencing has been carried out
by Aboriginal Labour. Paragraph (b) States that there are no benches or
tools and that manual work is being absolutely neglected. Reply to that
is that it was not considered necessary to supply an expensive Kit of
tools to a small Centre like Walcha where the Pupils are of a roaming
nature and frequently go on walk about. The teacher was supplied with
Paints some time ago to repaint the school, all the use made of this
supply was to have some part of the inside painted and 2/3 of the paint
placed away in a locker. Paragraph (c) States sewing is being taught by
the Teachers wife and good progress being made but no provision made for
other Domestic work set down in Aboriginal Syllabus. Reply is that each
of Mr Baileys predecessors in the past made satisfactory progress with
the limited means at their disposal. Paragrah (d) States that the School
is some distances from the Reserve, so that it cannot be a centre for
community Life as it should be. Reply to that is that the School
building was handed over to the Board by the Department of Education,
and that the site was selected by Mr Wilcox own Dept. and not by this
Board. Paragraph (2) Reply to this is that the Reserve was never
intended to be organized as an Aboriginal Station. The population does
not warrant this. The teacher was only appointed to be Teacher only and
was never given to understand that he would be appointed a Manager.
Further I state that the Police have always exercised a close
supervision over the Reserve with satisfactory results. The School
Inspector states that a Manager is necessary, and is of the opinion that
the present teacher (Mr Bailey) is a suitable man. I beg to differ with
Mr Wilcox in this matter. Mr Bailey in my opinion has neither the
required temperament, or ability to control an Aboriginal Station. His
Manner proved to me in the presence of Aborigines shows lack of
knowledge, and general attitude would and does make the Aborigines
antagonistic towards hum. Paragraph (3) States that general Health
conditions are appalling, and that although the matter has been reported
to the Board no action has been taken. Also that malnutrition is
evident. The reply to this is that the Inspectors information is
evidently based on that supplied to him by Mr Bailey. And that in my
opinion is misleading. The facts are as follows : - I made an inspection
of the Reserve accompanied by Mr and Mrs Bailey and the Manager of
Kempsey Station and the only cases noted are

Roy Griffin : - Suffering from weak and slightly inflamed eyes (Father
given the opportunity to either send him back in Truck for treatment at
Kempsey Hospital or Sydney Hospital. Father who is a cripple refuses.

Noel Griffin: Aged (14) fourteen years. Similar condition, and as in the
case of Brother Roy, Father refuses to allow boy to be sent away for
treatment.

Bill Naylor: - Aged 13 Years. Suffering from Ringworm caused through
filthy surroundings. Father objects to Board treating boy in recognized
hospital.

Nabelle Naylor: - In my opinion worms and filth. Father refuses to send
Child away for treatment.

Dorothy Griffin: - Sister to Noel and Bill also suffering from weak
eyes. Father refuses to allow children to be taken away for treatment.

Doreen Naylor: - Aged 12 years -- Another case of weak eyes. Father
refuses to allow Doreen to be taken away for treatment.

I cannot seen any cases of malnutrition on the Reserve. The Naylor
family are living in an old Tin and bag Shack about 10' \* 7' with an
earthen Floor, and several Dogs also camp with them. The position with
regard to these Children is that if the Board deem it necessary to
provide Medical treatment it will have to be at some Centre other than
Walcha Hospital and Drs. at Walcha as the Aborigines are not welcomed
there (Police I understand dealing with Report with that condition). If
the Board attempt to use force in removing the Children for Medical
attention there will be trouble from the Parents and Relatives. From my
previous experience of the above cases, and having in mind the
information given by Sister Pratt while teaching at Walcha I am of the
opinion that the abovementioned are Chronic. Their parents have suffered
from V.D Griffin himself is a cripple from some form of paralysis and
has been bed ridden for over 9 years. Naylor is a filthy type of
Aboriginal and prefers to live like an Animal. Should the Board decide
to take his Children away for treatment, then I would recommend that the
whole of the Children belonging to Naylor be placed in the Boards homes
after being cleansed and cured. With all die respect to the definite
opinions so forcibly expressed by Mr School Inspector Wilcox. I am of
the opinion that he has been to say the least misinformed by the School
teacher Mr. Bailey. Mr Bailey from his conversation to me at Walcha
appears to be under a delusion that a full Aboriginal Station should be
formed at Walcha and that the Board should to build up the Reserve
population force Aborigines from surrounding Districts to reside on the
Reserve. Mr Bailey is not so much concerned about the welfare of the
Aborigines as his own future and progress. I understand that Mr Bailey
came to the Boards service from a subsidized school. I think it would be
in the interests of the Aborigines if he returned to a subsidized White

> *Protests/resistance to school*

Dept Ed. 19/7/1926 -- Question of re-admission of aboriginal children --
Last year there was an element of overcrowding at this school and a
number of parents took exception to the attendance of aboriginal
children thereat. In view of these objections the children were excluded
from attendance and the Aborigines Protection Board informed
accordingly. Mr. Inspector Wilson now reports that the attendance is at
present not more than could be accommodated at the school and that even
if the coloured children were re-admitted the teacher would not be
unduly embarrassed. He points out, however, that the same parents who
objected to the attendance of the aboriginal children last year are
objecting to their re-admission. The teacher's report goes to show that
whereas 5 parents are in favour, there are 13 parents who object. Of
those parents who are in favour some have no children in attendance. It
is unfortunate that a number of coloured children is not sufficient to
warrant the Aborigines Protection Board in establishing a school and
thus solve this vexed question. This Department, however, has no option
in the circumstances but to adhere to the decision to exclude the
children in consequence of the local opposition to their attendance. It
is recommended, therefore, that matters be allowed to remain as at
present and that the petitioners be informed accordingly. I also
recommend that the Aborigines Protection Board be informed of the facts
as disclosed in the last report from the Inspector."

Chief Inspector -- 2/7/1926 -- "... Personally I can see no good reason
why the dark children should not be admitted, but, if it is the rule to
refuse admission to such children when the parents of the white children
object, I do not see what can be done. The dimensions of the school are
17' \* 3' \* 14'. At the rate of 10 sq ft to a pupil this gives
accommodation for 24 children. The teacher states that the enrolment of
the school at the commencement of the September quarter will be 17 or
18. From 7 to 10 dark children in addition are likely to be enrolled, if
the embargo is lifted. The maximum possible enrolment is therefore 28,
but it will most likely be less. This enrolment would not mean more than
an ordinary attendance of 24, so that the need for additional
accommodation, hardly exists, even if the dark children are re-admitted.
I recommend (1) that the children of the Aborigines be admitted to the
Summer Vale school, if it is possible for this to be done in spite of
the protests of parents of the white children (2) that the petitioners
be informed that the enrolment of the school would not justify
additional accommodation being provided even if the aborigines were
re-admitted. (3) that if it is not possible to admit the aborigines
under the above circumstances, the Aborigines Protection Board be urged
again to make other provision for their education."

25/6/1926 -- Memo from Teacher Miss. E. Cook -- Exclusion of Aborigines
-- "I beg to state that I have received your communication this day, and
have interview the parents and guardians of the white children attending
this school. Those parents who protested against the attendance of the
Aborigines have not changed their views on the matter. Enclosed, are two
lists, signed by the parents themselves. One, signed by the parents who
favour the admittance, the other signed by the parents who do not favour
admittance. (1) Five parents favour admittance (includes names of
parents) (2) Thirteen parents do not favour admittance (includes names
of parents). ... From the information gathered when I visited the
Aborigines homes, from seven and eight to ten children are able to
attend school if the embargo was lifted. I was unable to interview some
of the parents who were absent from their homes on the "Reserve". The
children are not receiving education and an inclination on part of two
parents not to allow their children to attend Summer Vale School is
felt, even if the embargo is lifted."

Letter attached from local residents writes "I am enclosing with this
two petitions -- I will be grateful if you would be kind enough to
present them to T.D.Mutch Minister for Education -- Some of the Petition
has been signed by White Residents of Summervale (about 4 miles from
Walcha) the other by Dark people resident on the Reserve. Both request
that the dark children by re-admitted to the Summervale School and that
the needful accommodation be provided. I would respectfully point out
that these people are citizens, and the young married couples do not
receive any assistance from the Aboriginal Protection Board." (Miss E.
Smith Missionary, Aboriginal Inland Mission, Australia)

Letter from Residents of the Reserve. "Sir, we the undersigned Residents
on the Reserve Walcha wish to place before you the following facts. For
23 years we and our children have attended the Summervale Public School
at the request of White residents to make up the number required to keep
the School open. We are all wither half-caste 1/4 caste or nearly white,
and are enrolled citizens of both State and Commonwealth and compelled
by law to vote at all Elections on penalty or fine. We respectfully
request that our children be re-admitted to the above school and that
the necessary accommodation by provided to enable us to comply with the
law, which requires all children between the ages of 6 + 14 years to
attend school -- Requesting your sympathetic consideration of this
petition." (Written in the hand writing of M. E. Smith, Missionary)

An additional letter from Residents of the Summervale and District goes

14/7/1930 -- Memo from Teacher -- Summervale Aborigines School -- Skin
trouble pupils- "One of the boys enrolled in First Class is suffering
from a disease of the scalp This does not appear infectious, as I
understand that he has been suffering from some time, but it looks very
unpleasant. The teacher report that parents have been approached with
reference to it and say they are treating it with ointment. I recommend
that the AP Board be asked to obtain full report from Teacher re
condition of the child with a view to having the child examined and
treated by qualified medical exam." Note from Inspector -- "The
description of this scalp condition suggests ringworm which may persist
for years if not properly treated. The teacher might be asked to report
on the condition in the right of the information contained in the
pamphlet attached for his guidance ..."

15/10/1935 -- Memo from Teacher Miss. Pratt re Discharging of eyes
(infectious) "I have to report that the majority of the pupils
(aborigines) attending this school have discharging eyes which are
infectious. They are, handling books, pencils and pens, which are in
turn handled by the other pupils and my self, thus exposing all to risk
of infection. The following is the result of a rough examination, gloved
being needed for a thorough examination." Note from Chief Inspector --
The School Medical Officer is at Glen Innes PS. I suggest that Dr.
Campling be asked to examine the pupils at Summer Vale. The general
question of sores with these pupils calls for special attention.
