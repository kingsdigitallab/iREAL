25/11/1907 -- Police Department -- Police Station Nymbodia re Aboriginal
children for School at "Nymbodia" -- "Constable Wood No 8253
respectfully reports -- that 16 aboriginal children applied for
schooling at the public school at Nymbodia, ages ranging from 6 years to
14 years, and were also supplied with rations and clothing from the
Aborigines Protection Board. And are now ready to attend school. The
Constable made careful inspection, and inquiries, of an additional 5
children ages from 6 yrs to 16 years, making a total of 21 children that
would attend a school. Besides a few other aborigines children that
reside at no great distances from Nymbodia. The Aboriginal children who
were educated, are fairly good scholars, and those now in the camps, are
intelligent and need schooling. A School could be erected at a very low
cost on water reserve No. 360 parish Shannon, County Fitzroy. If set
apart for Aborigines Reserve and the present Aborigines Reserve No.
2958. Gazzetted 15^th^ Jan 87 revoked, as no inducements yet made, will
cause the Aborigines to occupy themselves being too isolated. A rough
print taken by the Constable where issuing the clothing, to a few
children may give some information." Signed R. Wood Constable. Note from
Super. Cameron Armidale "Forwarded. Should it be decided to establish a
school, I fear these Aborigines would only attend for a time, as they
are frequently wandering about."

3/4/1908 -- Dept. Public Instruction -- "There is an application for the
establishment of a Provisional School for Aborigines at Nymbodia. There
are seventeen (17) children of school ages who have been excluded form
the ordinary public school at Nymbodia, and are now without the means of
education. It is difficult, if not impossible, to state whether the
school will be a permanent one; but the local white settlers are of
opinion it will be. The children are at present growing up in ignorance
and idleness -- a condition of affairs not conducive to the morale of
the district. As there is a possibility of the school lasting but a
short time, and because out Dept. will find teacher, furniture and
apparatus, etc. I think the A.P Board should be asked to contribute half
the cost of the building. Recommendation: (a) That a provisional school
for Aborigines be established at Nymbodia (b) that I be authorized to
have a suitable building erected and furniture supplied at a cost of
about (£56) Fifty -- six pounds (c) That, for reasons above stated, the
AP Board be asked to contribute £28 -- half the cost of the building (d)
that if the A.P. B cannot pay this or any other sum, our Dept. to bear
the whole cost, rather than allow existing conditions to continue.

Application for establishment of School -- Distance from Post Office 1
1/2 miles. Nearest State School -- Nymbodia 1 1/2 miles. Population
likely to attend school -- About 40 Aborigines. White population not
included. Is the locality likely to be permanently inhabited? What are
he occupations of residents? -- Yes. Most of the Aborigines are casual
labourers. They have been here for years and are likely to remain "Bob
Little" is the police Black Tracker. 4. State any information you have
obtained relative to the circumstances of the people of whose children
the School is intended, especially whether they have a permanent
interest in the locality -- "Circumstances very poor. No farming done by
these people on their own account. No permanent interest in the locality
nor ever likely to have still its looked upon as their "home". If they
go away for a short time, I am told, they invariably return." 5. What
school exists in the neighborhood, and at what distances from the
proposed School are they situated? Do they provide sufficient
accommodation for the whole number of children in the District?
"Nymbodia Public 1 1/2 miles. Yes, but the white people objected to
these aborigines associating with their children; and our Dept. rightly
upheld the objection of the whites." 14. In addition to the foregoing
matters, state any other circumstances which you may consider material
for the Minister's information when deciding upon the application. Your
summary report and your recommendation should, however, be made upon the
separate form provided "These unfortunate children, who are to be
pitied, are entirely without the means of education. There is always
some risk in establishing schools of this kind as the residents are of
nomadic habits. Still there are 17 children here growing up in
ignorance, and undoubtedly some risk should be taken on their behalf.
The white settlers all agree that the children are likely to remain, as
they have done for years past. As our Dept. will find teacher, furniture
and apparatus, I think the AP Board might fairly be expected to
contribute to the cost of the building. But if the Board cannot see its
way to do so, then I think our Dept. should bear the whole cost rather
than suffer these children to grow up in ignorance and idleness as they
are now doing." Signed Inspector R. Henderson

14/3/1917 -- Memo from Nymbodia Police to Dept. Report on Aborigines
School at Nyumbodia -- "I have the honor to inform you that I visited
the Aboriginal School at Nymbodia, as requested, and inspected the
school and contents. The school has been closed for about two years and
is not likely to be required again as very few aboriginals are living
here nor are they likely to come back, as I understand many have found
employment in more congenial localities. The building was erected about
ten years ago, but has a new pine ceiling. The timber is in a fair state
of preservation, but the white ants have done a little damage. I could
not find any traces of them in the ceiling where one would expect them
to be. Mr. Godwin is living in the Managers house and cares for the
school building. In my opinion the contents of the school have not been
interfered with. The school is 24 ft long 18ft wide and 10ft high. There
is also an open porch ......... (more details of state of school).......

10/7/1917 -- Memo concerning Aborigines School Land for Return Soldiers
"Sir, I have the honor to inform you that the land on which the Nymbodia
Aborigines School and Residence is situated would be eminently suitable
for two returned soldiers especially as there is a big reserve adjacent,
part of which could probably be secured as additional to the above.
About twenty five or thirty acres may be classed as first class maize
growing land, and could be made productive at once, as it was so farmed
for some years before the Aborigines Board secured it and there is
already a serviceable barn on the land. Nothing at all has been done by
the Board on this property which is on the opposite side of the river to
the school buildings, and it is simply a dumping ground for local
residents stray stock. The late teacher Mr. Newnham did very well with
poultry on the land at the school, and also grew fine crops of maize,
potatoes, and all kinds of vegetables. In my opinion two returned
soldiers especially if in partnership could make splendid livings by
raising poultry, pigs and cattle, and growing maize and vegetables.
There is a fair local market for the latter, as very few farmers grow
their own. Dairying also could be profitably carried on. .............."

16/3/1911 -- Teacher Application for resignation "Sir, As my application
which was forwarded in January last has not been acknowledged I beg to
ask if Mr. W. J. Fryer is likely to be appointed to succeed me as
teacher of the above school. As my health is run down I am desirous of
having a rest prior to my marriage with Mr. Fryer whom I desire to

to be relieved of school duties not later than the end of the month

21/2/1911 -- Employment of Mr. Fryer as Teacher, now Miss Hamer
resigned. "Acting Under Secretary -- On account of her approaching
marriage Miss Hamer has tendered her resignation as Teacher of the above
school to take effect from 28^th^ instant, provided that her intended
husband, W. W. Fryer be appointed to the vacant position. W. Fryer is a
missionary under the Australian Aborigines Mission and is interested in
the welfare of he dark people. I therefore recommend that he be
recognized as Teacher of the Nymbodia Aborigines School on the distinct
understanding that this appointment gives no claim to any other position
in the services. Salary of £100 per annum from date of entry on duty."

William John Fryer -- Experience -- " Mr. Fryer has been engaged for
some time on an Aborigines Mission Station near Inverell and has done
creditable work there both as a teacher and a missionary. He possesses a
knowledge of manual work and farming which will be of maximum value to
the aboriginal boys"

Reference for Mr. Fryer -- I have had opportunities of observing the
bearer of this Mr. Fryer both in business and away from it for a period
attending over five years, and have formed the highest opinion of his
moral worth and character. If he should leave Maclean he will be greatly
missed from his Church the Presbyterian of which he is a member, and
from the Young Men's Christian Association of which he has always taken
an active part. He has also taken an interest in the Aborigines on

> *Protests/resistance to school*

16/8/1907 -- Letter from resident "Dear Sir, the authorities of the
Aboriginal Protection Board are, we understand, taking steps to have the
Aboriginal children educated in the Nymbodia Public School. The parents
of the children attending the school one and all object to such a
course. On the enclosed petition will be found the names of every person
having one child going to the school. I have been requested to send this
petition to you on behalf of the residents of Nymbodia." Signed George
Ellis.

Petition "The education of the Aboriginals at Nymbodia -- We the
residents are parents interested in education of the pupils at the
Nymbodia Public School do hereby protest against the inclusion of the
Aboriginal children with our children at the school here. We also
undertake to suggest to the Aboriginal Protection Board that should
these children desire to be educated, that there is a home, a good
school, and a good teacher, at the common in Grafton to meet their
requests and that it is easier and better for them to be fed and clothed
at Grafton than at Nymbodia." Signed by 15 residents including Boarding
House Keeper, Farmer, Land Owner, Grazier, Farmer and Carrier.

Note from Chief Inspector: Nymbodia Public School is in Inspector
Byrnes' District. The village is near Grafton, and this I presume is the
reason the petition has been sent to me. The inclusion of aborigines at
our ordinary public schools is an old trouble. If they are permitted to
attend Nymbodia School then I have reason to believe that white children
would be withdrawn. Judging from my experience it is much better now for
the aborigines to be taught in a school of their own. We have had to do
this at Taree, Forster, Watcheye, Grafton and do doubt many other
places. Recommended that the teacher of Nymbodia Public School be
instructed that he must not allow the aborigines children to attend the
public school; but he should inform the aboriginal parents the Minister
is prepared to consider an application for the establishment of a
separate aborigines school at Nymbodia." Signed R. Henderson Inspector.

5/11/1907 -- Letter to APB from Dept. Public Instruction "Sir, I have to
inform you that in view of representations made by parents of children
attending the Nymbodia Public School, the Teacher has been instructed
not to permit any aboriginal children to attend the school. Will you be
good enough to inform me whether the number of aboriginal children at
Nymbodia is sufficient to warrant the provision of a school for them."
